Refresh AS research methods

Experiments

  • Investigate cause and effect by manipulating an independent variable (IV) and measuring a dependent variable (DV).
  • Lab experiment: controlled setting, high control of extraneous variables.
  • Field experiment: real-world setting, IV still manipulated.
  • Natural (quasi) experiment: IV occurs naturally, not manipulated by researcher.
  • Controls reduce confounding variables (standardised procedures, counterbalancing).
  • Designs: independent measures (different people per condition), repeated measures (same people in all conditions), matched pairs (participants paired on key variables).

Self-reports

  • Questionnaires: written questions, open or closed; gather large data quickly.
  • Interviews: structured, semi-structured or unstructured; richer detail.

Case studies

  • In-depth study of one person or small group, often using multiple methods.
  • Rich, detailed, often longitudinal and qualitative.

Observations

  • Types: naturalistic vs controlled, participant vs non-participant, covert vs overt, structured vs unstructured.
  • Behaviour is watched and recorded, often using behavioural categories.

Correlations & longitudinal studies

  • Correlation measures the relationship (strength and direction) between two co-variables; no cause and effect.
  • Positive, negative or no correlation, shown on a scattergraph.
  • Longitudinal studies follow the same participants over a long period.

Refresh AS research methods — evaluation & recall

One strength + one weakness of each

  • Lab experiment: strong control so high internal validity; but low ecological validity / artificial.
  • Field experiment: higher realism; but less control of extraneous variables.
  • Questionnaire: cheap, lots of data fast; but social desirability and low response rates.
  • Interview: rich detail; but interviewer effects and hard to analyse.
  • Case study: rich and detailed; but not generalisable and researcher bias.
  • Observation: sees real behaviour; but observer bias and possible ethical issues (covert).
  • Correlation: shows relationships, useful when experiments unethical; but cannot show cause and effect (third variable).
  • Longitudinal: tracks change over time; but attrition (dropout) and time-consuming.

Key terms

  • Reliability = consistency; a study/measure that can be repeated with same results.
  • Validity = whether it measures what it claims to measure.
  • Quantitative data = numbers; qualitative data = words/descriptions.
  • Objective = free from bias/opinion; subjective = based on personal interpretation.

New A-level methods: RCTs, postal questionnaires, rating scales

Randomised controlled trials (RCTs)

  • An experiment (often testing a treatment) where participants are randomly allocated to conditions.
  • Random allocation ensures each participant has an equal chance of any group, balancing individual differences.
  • Control group vs experimental group; may include a placebo group receiving a dummy treatment.
  • Single blind: participants do not know which group they are in.
  • Double blind: neither participants nor the researcher administering it know, reducing experimenter and demand effects.
  • Considered a gold-standard method for testing effectiveness of therapies and drugs.

Postal questionnaires

  • Self-report questionnaires sent and returned by post (or now electronically).
  • Reach large, geographically spread samples cheaply.
  • Completed in the respondent's own time, without a researcher present.

Rating scales

  • Respondents rate an item on a scale rather than answering open questions.
  • Forced/fixed choice: a limited set of responses (no neutral option forces a stance).
  • Likert scale: statement rated e.g. strongly agree to strongly disagree, usually 5 points.
  • Produce quantitative data that is easy to compare and analyse.

New A-level methods: RCTs, postal questionnaires, rating scales — evaluation & recall

RCTs — evaluate

  • Strength: random allocation controls participant variables, allowing strong causal conclusions.
  • Strength: double blind removes experimenter bias and placebo effects.
  • Weakness: expensive, time-consuming; may raise ethics (withholding treatment from controls).
  • Weakness: controlled conditions can be artificial, lowering ecological validity.

Postal questionnaires — evaluate

  • Strength: cheap, large samples, no interviewer effects, anonymity encourages honesty.
  • Weakness: very low response rates and non-response bias (only certain people reply).
  • Weakness: no one to clarify questions; cannot check who actually completed it.

Rating scales — evaluate

  • Strength: quick, produce quantitative data, easy to compare and test for reliability.
  • Weakness: forced choice restricts answers and may not reflect true views.
  • Weakness: response biases (acquiescence, central tendency / social desirability) reduce validity.

Psychometric tests & validity

Psychometric tests

  • Standardised tests that measure psychological characteristics, e.g. intelligence, personality, mood or symptoms.
  • Standardisation: the same procedure and scoring for everyone so scores are comparable.
  • Norms: average scores from a large representative sample used to interpret an individual's score.
  • Must be reliable (consistent) and valid (measures the intended construct).

Types of validity

  • Internal validity: the study measured what it intended, free from confounds.
  • External validity: how far results generalise beyond the study.
  • Ecological validity: generalising to real-life settings and situations.
  • Temporal validity: whether findings still hold over time / across eras.
  • Construct validity: the test measures the underlying theoretical construct.
  • Concurrent validity: agreement with an existing established measure taken at the same time.
  • Predictive validity: how well scores predict future behaviour or outcomes.
  • Face validity: whether it appears, on the surface, to measure what it claims.

Psychometric tests & validity — evaluation & recall

Evaluating psychometric tests

  • Strength: standardisation and norms allow objective comparison between people.
  • Strength: produce quantitative data, quick and often reliable.
  • Weakness: may lack validity if the construct is oversimplified (e.g. reducing 'intelligence' to one score).
  • Weakness: culturally biased norms can disadvantage some groups; social desirability affects self-report tests.

Using validity to evaluate a study

  • Ask: could a confound explain the result? (internal validity).
  • Ask: was the task artificial? (ecological validity).
  • Ask: is the sample/era limited so results do not generalise? (external / temporal validity).
  • Named validity types make evaluation points precise and worth more marks.

Improving validity

  • Use controls, standardised procedures and single/double blind to raise internal validity.
  • Use realistic tasks and natural settings for ecological validity.
  • Check a new measure against an established one (concurrent) and follow up outcomes (predictive).
  • Use representative samples and update norms to protect external and temporal validity.

Hypotheses: null, one-tailed, two-tailed

The null hypothesis

  • States there is NO effect or NO relationship — any difference is due to chance.
  • Example: 'There is no difference in recovery scores between the therapy group and the control group.'
  • It is what statistical testing actually tries to reject.

The alternative (experimental) hypothesis

  • States there IS an effect or relationship between the variables.
  • Called the experimental hypothesis in an experiment, alternative hypothesis in a correlation.
  • Must be operationalised — variables defined in measurable terms.

Directional vs non-directional

  • Directional (one-tailed): predicts the DIRECTION of the effect (which group scores higher, or positive/negative correlation).
  • Non-directional (two-tailed): predicts a difference or relationship but NOT its direction.
  • Choice depends on whether prior research suggests a direction.

Operationalising

  • Turn vague variables into specific, measurable ones.
  • 'Stress' becomes 'score out of 40 on the Perceived Stress Scale'.
  • Both IV and DV (or both co-variables) must be operationalised.

Hypotheses: null, one-tailed, two-tailed — evaluation & recall

Worked examples

  • Null: 'There is no difference in number of symptoms reported before and after CBT; any difference is due to chance.'
  • Two-tailed: 'There is a difference in number of symptoms reported before and after CBT.'
  • One-tailed: 'Participants report fewer symptoms after CBT than before.'
  • Correlational one-tailed: 'There is a positive correlation between hours of exercise and mood score.'

When to use which

  • Use one-tailed when previous evidence or theory strongly predicts a direction.
  • Use two-tailed when there is no clear prior evidence, or findings are mixed — it is the safer default.
  • Two-tailed is more cautious; one-tailed has more power if the direction is correct but risks missing an opposite effect.

Common mistakes

  • Writing a hypothesis that is not operationalised (too vague to measure).
  • Confusing the null (no effect) with a two-tailed hypothesis (there is an effect, direction unknown).
  • Stating a direction (one-tailed) without any justification from prior research.
  • Forgetting to include both variables.

Issues & debates (part 1)

Application of psychology to everyday life

  • Definition: how far psychological findings can be used practically to improve or change everyday life.
  • Considers usefulness of theories and studies in the real world.
  • Clinical example: CBT derived from cognitive theory is applied to treat depression and phobias.
  • Health example: findings on adherence are applied to design reminder systems that improve medicine-taking.

Individual vs situational explanations

  • Definition: is behaviour caused by internal characteristics (individual) or the external environment/situation?
  • Individual = personality, biology, dispositions; situational = context, social setting, pressures.
  • Clinical example: depression explained by individual (biochemical imbalance) vs situational (stressful life events).
  • Health example: unhealthy eating explained by individual willpower vs situational food environment.

Nature vs nurture

  • Definition: is behaviour due to inherited biology (nature) or experience and environment (nurture)?
  • Most psychologists now favour an interaction of both.
  • Clinical example: schizophrenia has genetic vulnerability (nature) triggered by stress/environment (nurture) — diathesis-stress.
  • Health example: addiction involves genetic predisposition plus learned/environmental cues.

Use of children in psychological research

  • Definition: the special ethical and methodological issues of studying children.
  • Children cannot fully give informed consent — parental consent and assent needed.
  • More vulnerable to harm and to demand characteristics.
  • Clinical/health example: studying childhood anxiety or child health behaviours requires extra safeguarding.

Issues & debates (part 2)

Cultural differences

  • Definition: how behaviour and psychological findings vary across cultures, and the risk of imposing one culture's norms on another.
  • Beware ethnocentrism and culture-bound findings; distinguish individualist vs collectivist cultures.
  • Clinical example: symptoms and expression of disorders (e.g. what counts as 'normal') differ across cultures, affecting diagnosis.
  • Health example: attitudes to illness, diet and help-seeking vary by culture.

Reductionism vs holism

  • Definition: reductionism explains behaviour by breaking it into simplest parts (e.g. biology); holism considers the whole person and all influences.
  • Reductionism is scientific and testable; holism is richer but harder to test.
  • Clinical example: explaining OCD purely via neurotransmitters (reductionist) vs including cognitions and environment (holistic).
  • Health example: explaining stress only by cortisol vs the whole biopsychosocial picture.

Determinism vs free will

  • Definition: determinism says behaviour is caused by factors beyond our control; free will says we choose our behaviour.
  • Types include biological, environmental and psychic determinism.
  • Clinical example: is a person with addiction determined by their genes/environment, or do they have free will to change?
  • Health example: does health behaviour reflect free choice or determined by upbringing and circumstance?

Idiographic vs nomothetic

  • Definition: idiographic studies the individual in depth (unique experience); nomothetic seeks general laws across many people.
  • Case studies are idiographic; experiments and psychometric norms are nomothetic.
  • Clinical example: a detailed case study of one patient (idiographic) vs diagnostic criteria applied to all (nomothetic).
  • Health example: individual illness narrative vs large-scale correlational health studies.

Comparing and contrasting debates in an essay

  • Show you understand a debate is a spectrum, not either/or — most explanations sit between the extremes.
  • Contrast two positions on the SAME study: e.g. a biological account is reductionist AND deterministic, whereas a holistic account allows more free will.

  • Swap in the relevant debate and always finish with the real-world implication.

Schizophrenia: diagnosis & symptoms

ICD-11 diagnosis of schizophrenia

  • Persistent psychotic symptoms lasting at least 1 month.
  • At least two of: delusions, hallucinations, disorganised thinking (formal thought disorder), experiences of influence/passivity/control, negative symptoms, grossly disorganised behaviour, psychomotor disturbance.
  • At least one core symptom (delusions, hallucinations, disorganised thinking, or influence/passivity) must be present.
  • Symptoms not due to another condition, substance, or medication.

Positive symptoms (excess/added to normal function)

  • Delusions: fixed false beliefs held despite contrary evidence.
  • Hallucinations: perceptions without a stimulus (most often auditory).
  • Disorganised speech/thought: derailment, incoherence ('word salad').

Negative symptoms (loss of normal function)

  • Avolition: reduced motivation/goal-directed behaviour.
  • Alogia: poverty of speech.
  • Anhedonia: reduced ability to experience pleasure.
  • Flattened (blunted) affect: reduced emotional expression.

Types of delusion

  • Persecutory: belief one is being harmed/plotted against.
  • Grandeur: belief in exaggerated importance/power/identity.
  • Reference: belief that neutral events carry special personal meaning.
  • Control (passivity): belief that thoughts/actions are controlled by external forces.
⭐ Key study — Freeman et al. (2003)

Aim: To investigate persecutory ideation (paranoia) in non-clinical individuals using immersive virtual reality (VR).

Method: Lab experiment; non-clinical adults entered a neutral VR library populated by computer-generated avatars programmed to behave neutrally. Participants completed measures of paranoid thinking and were interviewed about their attributions.

Results: A substantial proportion attributed persecutory/paranoid intentions to the neutral avatars (e.g. feeling watched or judged); others formed positive/neutral interpretations. Paranoid appraisals were linked to anxiety and interpersonal sensitivity.

Conclusion: Persecutory ideation occurs on a continuum in the general population and can be triggered by ambiguous social cues; VR is a valid, controlled tool for studying paranoia.

Schizophrenia: explanations

Biological — genetic

  • Schizophrenia runs in families; risk rises with genetic closeness to an affected relative.
  • Twin studies: concordance is markedly higher in MZ (identical) than DZ (non-identical) twins, but MZ concordance is well below 100%, so genes are not the whole story.
  • Adoption/family studies support heritability; likely polygenic (many genes of small effect) rather than one 'schizophrenia gene'.
  • Diathesis-stress model: inherited vulnerability + environmental trigger.

Biological — biochemical (dopamine hypothesis)

  • Original hypothesis: schizophrenia caused by excess dopamine activity / too many/oversensitive D2 receptors.
  • Evidence: dopamine agonists (amphetamines) can induce psychotic symptoms; antipsychotics that block dopamine reduce positive symptoms.
  • Revised version: hyperdopaminergia in the mesolimbic pathway = positive symptoms; hypodopaminergia in the prefrontal cortex = negative/cognitive symptoms.

Psychological — cognitive explanation

  • Symptoms arise from faulty information processing and dysfunctional thought.
  • Attentional/cognitive bias: failure to filter irrelevant stimuli; misattribution of internal events (e.g. inner speech attributed to an external voice = hallucination).
  • Metacognitive/reasoning bias: 'jumping to conclusions' — accepting conclusions on little evidence, sustaining delusions.
  • Frith: dysfunction in monitoring one's own intentions/actions explains passivity and hallucinations.

Schizophrenia: explanations — evaluation & recall

Compare biological vs cognitive explanations

  • Biological: strong twin/adoption and drug evidence; but MZ concordance <100% shows environment matters, and dopamine hypothesis is oversimplified (drugs block receptors fast but symptoms take weeks to ease).
  • Cognitive: explains the form of specific symptoms (why a voice, why a delusion persists) and underpins CBT; but may describe rather than cause — cognitive faults could be a consequence of an underlying biological problem.
  • Not mutually exclusive: cognitive faults may stem from dopamine dysregulation → integrated biopsychosocial/diathesis-stress account is most complete.

Research methods

  • Concordance studies are correlational — cannot establish that genes cause schizophrenia; shared family environment is a confound.
  • Post-mortem and PET/drug studies of dopamine can be indirect and hard to isolate cause vs effect.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Nature vs nurture — twin concordance below 100% supports interaction of genes and environment (diathesis-stress).
  • Reductionism vs holism — dopamine hypothesis is biologically reductionist; a holistic view adds cognitive and social factors.
  • Determinism vs free will — biological explanations imply symptoms are largely determined, reducing personal control.

Schizophrenia: treatment & management

Biological — antipsychotics

  • Typical (first-generation), e.g. chlorpromazine: dopamine antagonists that block D2 receptors, reducing positive symptoms.
  • Atypical (second-generation), e.g. clozapine: act on dopamine and serotonin receptors; treat positive AND some negative symptoms with fewer motor side-effects.
  • Side-effects: typicals cause extrapyramidal effects (tardive dyskinesia); clozapine risks agranulocytosis (needs blood monitoring).

Biological — ECT

  • Electro-convulsive therapy: controlled electric current induces a brief seizure under anaesthetic and muscle relaxant.
  • Mainly used for severe, treatment-resistant cases or catatonia; mechanism not fully understood.
  • Concerns: memory impairment, ethical issues around consent and invasiveness.

Psychological — CBT for schizophrenia

  • Aims to reduce distress from symptoms rather than 'cure' them.
  • Normalising: framing unusual experiences as understandable to reduce fear/stigma.
  • Reality testing / challenging: gently examining evidence for delusions and generating alternative explanations.
  • Coping strategies for hallucinations; usually an adjunct to medication.
⭐ Key study — Sensky et al. (2000)

Aim: To test whether CBT reduces symptoms in patients with schizophrenia resistant to medication, compared with a non-specific 'befriending' control.

Method: Randomised controlled trial; medication-resistant patients randomly allocated to CBT or a befriending condition (matched for therapist time/attention). Both ran for around 9 months; symptoms measured before, after, and at follow-up. Therapists delivered both to control for attention.

Results: Both groups improved by the end of treatment, but only the CBT group continued to improve at 9-month follow-up, showing significantly greater reductions in positive and negative symptoms and depression.

Conclusion: CBT produces durable benefits beyond non-specific therapist contact; it is an effective adjunct for medication-resistant schizophrenia.

Schizophrenia: treatment & management — evaluation & recall

Compare effectiveness & side-effects

  • Antipsychotics: fast-acting and effective for positive symptoms; but side-effects harm adherence and they do little for negative/cognitive symptoms.
  • Atypicals generally better tolerated than typicals, aiding compliance, but clozapine needs monitoring.
  • CBT: no physical side-effects, durable gains (Sensky), and targets distress/negative symptoms; but slower, needs a skilled therapist and patient engagement, and is usually combined with drugs.
  • Combined drug + CBT is often most effective.

Ethics & research methods

  • Ethics: side-effects and ECT raise harm/consent concerns; drugs may be used as 'chemical control'.
  • Sensky used a matched befriending control and randomisation — strong internal validity, isolating CBT's specific effect from attention alone.
  • RCTs allow cause-effect conclusions; long follow-up shows durability.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Determinism vs free will — biological treatments imply a determined disorder; CBT restores some patient agency.
  • Reductionism vs holism — drugs are reductionist (fix the chemistry); CBT is more holistic.
  • Application to everyday life — combined treatment improves daily functioning and reduces relapse.

Mood disorders: diagnosis

ICD-11 depressive disorder (unipolar)

  • Episode lasting at least 2 weeks, most of the day, nearly every day.
  • Core: depressed/low mood and/or markedly reduced interest/pleasure (anhedonia).
  • Additional: reduced concentration, worthlessness/excessive guilt, hopelessness, disturbed sleep, appetite/weight change, fatigue, psychomotor changes, recurrent thoughts of death/suicide.
  • Significant distress or impairment in functioning; not better explained by another cause.

ICD-11 bipolar disorder

  • Alternation of mood episodes: at least one manic (or hypomanic) episode, usually with depressive episodes.
  • Manic episode: extreme elevated/irritable mood plus increased energy/activity for ≥1 week; grandiosity, reduced need for sleep, pressured speech, racing thoughts (flight of ideas), distractibility, risky behaviour.
  • Bipolar I = full manic episodes; Bipolar II = hypomania + major depression.

Measure — Beck Depression Inventory (BDI)

  • Self-report questionnaire; 21 items scoring symptoms 0-3 over the past two weeks.
  • Higher total = greater severity (minimal/mild/moderate/severe bands).
  • Assesses cognitive, affective and somatic symptoms; used to screen and to track change over time.

Mood disorders: diagnosis — evaluation & recall

Reliability & validity of diagnosis

  • Standardised criteria/tools (ICD-11, BDI) improve inter-rater reliability and consistency.
  • BDI has good test-retest reliability and validity, but self-report risks social desirability and lack of insight.
  • Validity threatened by symptom overlap (e.g. depression vs anxiety) and comorbidity; low mood is also a normal reaction, risking over-diagnosis.

Cultural issues & research methods

  • Culture shapes symptom expression — depression may present somatically (physical complaints) in some cultures, so Western checklists can miss cases (imposed etic).
  • BDI developed on Western samples; translated items may not carry the same meaning.
  • Quantitative BDI scores allow comparison/tracking but reduce a complex experience to a number.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Cultural differences — somatic vs psychological presentation challenges the cross-cultural validity of Western diagnostic tools.
  • Nomothetic vs idiographic — BDI/ICD categorise (nomothetic); a clinical interview captures the individual (idiographic).
  • Application to everyday life — reliable diagnosis enables appropriate treatment and support.

Mood disorders: explanations of depression

Biological explanations

  • Biochemical (monoamine hypothesis): low activity of serotonin and/or noradrenaline is linked to depression; supported by antidepressants that raise these neurotransmitters.
  • Genetic: depression and bipolar disorder run in families; higher MZ than DZ twin concordance suggests inherited vulnerability, likely polygenic.
  • Candidate genes include those affecting serotonin (transporter and receptor genes).

Beck's cognitive theory

  • Negative triad: negative views of the self, the world, and the future.
  • Cognitive distortions/errors: e.g. overgeneralisation, catastrophising, selective abstraction, black-and-white thinking.
  • Negative self-schemas (often from early experience) bias interpretation of events, maintaining depression.

Learned helplessness / attributional style (Seligman)

  • Learned helplessness: after uncontrollable aversive events, people/animals stop trying to escape even when they can.
  • Reformulated as depressive attributional style: attributing bad events to internal, stable and global causes.
  • Such attributions produce hopelessness and low mood.
⭐ Key study — Oruč et al. (1997)

Aim: To investigate whether variants (polymorphisms) of the 5-HT2C serotonin receptor gene and the serotonin transporter (5-HTT) gene are associated with bipolar (unipolar-bipolar) affective disorder.

Method: Association (case-control) study; DNA from patients diagnosed with bipolar disorder was compared with healthy controls (a Croatian sample). Allele/genotype frequencies of the two candidate genes were compared, and results were analysed separately by sex.

Results: A weak association was found between the 5-HT2C receptor gene and bipolar disorder in female patients, but no clear overall association for the transporter gene; effects were small and not consistent across the whole sample. (check book for exact allele figures)

Conclusion: Serotonin-related genes may make a small contribution to genetic vulnerability for bipolar disorder, but no single gene is a strong cause — supporting a polygenic, multifactorial account.

Biological — antidepressants

  • Tricyclics (TCAs): block reuptake of serotonin AND noradrenaline, raising both; effective but more side-effects (anticholinergic) and dangerous in overdose.

Psychological — Beck's cognitive restructuring

  • Identify negative automatic thoughts and the negative triad.
  • Test them against evidence (reality testing); homework/'patient as scientist' to gather evidence.
  • Replace distortions with more realistic, balanced thoughts.

Psychological — Ellis's REBT

  • ABC model: Activating event → Belief → Consequence; irrational Beliefs (not events) cause emotional distress.
  • Extended to D and E: Dispute the irrational belief, producing new Effects.
  • Therapist actively/forcefully disputes 'musturbatory' irrational beliefs (e.g. 'I must be approved of') to replace them with rational ones.

Impulse control disorders: diagnosis

ICD-11 — impulse control disorders (overview)

  • Repeated failure to resist an impulse/urge to perform an act that is harmful to self or others.
  • Mounting tension before the act and relief/gratification during or after it.
  • The behaviour is not better explained by another mental disorder.

Kleptomania

  • Recurrent failure to resist impulses to steal objects NOT needed for personal use or monetary value.
  • Rising tension before the theft, pleasure/relief while stealing.
  • Not committed out of anger, revenge, delusion or hallucination.

Pyromania

  • Recurrent, deliberate fire-setting for its own sake, driven by an irresistible urge.
  • Fascination with/attraction to fire; tension beforehand, gratification afterwards.
  • Not done for gain, to conceal crime, out of anger, or due to another disorder/intoxication.

Gambling disorder

  • Persistent, recurrent gambling causing distress/impairment.
  • Impaired control (frequency, intensity, duration), increasing priority over other interests, continuation despite negative consequences.
  • In ICD-11, pattern typically evident over about 12 months.

Measure — K-SAS

  • Kleptomania Symptom Assessment Scale: self-report measuring severity of stealing urges/behaviour over the past week.
  • Items rated on a scale and summed; higher = more severe; used to assess severity and track treatment change.

Impulse control disorders: explanations

Overview (ICD-11)

  • Impulse control disorders = repeated failure to resist an impulse/urge to perform an act that is rewarding but harmful (self or others).
  • Examples: gambling disorder, pyromania, kleptomania, intermittent explosive disorder.
  • Rising tension before the act; pleasure/relief during; sometimes regret afterwards.

Biological explanation

  • Dopamine and the brain's reward (mesolimbic) system are central.
  • Performing the act triggers dopamine release, reinforcing the behaviour like a substance addiction.
  • Under-active reward system may drive sensation-seeking to reach normal pleasure levels.
  • Individual differences in dopamine sensitivity may explain vulnerability.

Behavioural (learning) explanation

  • Positive reinforcement: the 'win' (money, thrill) rewards the behaviour, increasing repetition.
  • Variable/intermittent reinforcement schedule (unpredictable wins) is highly resistant to extinction.
  • The 'near miss' can act as a conditioned reinforcer.
  • Explains persistence despite mounting losses.

Cognitive explanation

  • Miller's feeling-state theory: an intense positive emotion becomes fixed to the behaviour.
  • A 'feeling-state' links a specific desired feeling (e.g. power, excitement) to the act.
  • The state is later triggered by cues, producing an urge that overrides rational control.
  • Cognitive distortions (illusion of control, gambler's fallacy) maintain the disorder.

Impulse control disorders: treatment

Biological treatment

  • Opioid antagonists (naltrexone, nalmefene) block opioid receptors, reducing dopamine-linked reward/'high' from gambling.
  • Reduces urges and cravings; taken orally.
  • SSRIs sometimes used where anxiety/mood features are present.
  • Strengths: targets the biological reward mechanism directly; can be combined with therapy.

Covert sensitisation (Glover 1985)

  • A CBT/aversion technique: client imagines the problem behaviour paired with an unpleasant imagined consequence (e.g. nausea, shame, arrest).
  • Aim: create a learned negative association so urges are reduced.
  • 'Covert' = takes place in imagination, not real aversive stimuli.

Imaginal desensitisation (Blaszczynski & Nower 2003)

  • Client is taught relaxation, then imagines gambling scenes while staying relaxed.
  • Reciprocal inhibition: relaxation replaces the arousal/tension normally driving the urge.
  • Client imagines approaching then leaving the situation without gambling.
  • Reduces arousal-driven impulsivity.
⭐ Key study — Grant, Kim & Hartman (2008) — predicting response to opiate antagonists in pathological gambling

Aim: To identify clinical features predicting response to opioid antagonists (naltrexone/nalmefene) versus placebo in pathological gambling.

Method: Analysis of data from earlier double-blind, placebo-controlled RCTs of pathological gamblers treated with naltrexone or nalmefene. Response measured on gambling-symptom scales; predictors examined (notably family history of alcoholism and strength of gambling urges at baseline).

Results: Patients with a family history of alcoholism responded significantly better to opioid antagonists than those without; stronger baseline urges also predicted better drug response. Placebo response was not predicted by these features (check book for exact figures).

Conclusion: Family history of alcoholism can help predict which pathological gamblers will benefit from opioid-antagonist drugs, supporting a shared reward/biological mechanism and enabling more targeted treatment.

Anxiety & fear-related disorders: diagnosis

ICD-11: Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD)

  • Marked, general and persistent anxiety/worry not restricted to any particular situation ('free-floating').
  • Present most days for at least several months.
  • Symptoms: muscle tension, restlessness, autonomic arousal, difficulty concentrating, sleep disturbance.
  • Worry is excessive and hard to control.

ICD-11: Agoraphobia

  • Marked, excessive fear/anxiety about situations where escape or help may be difficult (crowds, public transport, open/enclosed spaces, being outside home alone).
  • Situations are avoided, endured with dread, or require a companion.
  • Out of proportion to actual danger; persists for months; impairs functioning.

ICD-11: Specific phobia (blood-injection-injury type)

  • Marked, excessive fear of a specific object/situation, out of proportion to danger.
  • Blood-injection-injury (BII) type: fear of blood, injections, injury, medical procedures.
  • Distinctive diphasic response — initial rise then sharp drop in heart rate/BP causing fainting (vasovagal syncope).
  • Provokes immediate anxiety; avoided or endured with intense fear.

Measures

  • GAD-7: 7-item self-report questionnaire; scores 0-21 rate severity of GAD symptoms over 2 weeks; brief screening/monitoring tool.
  • Blood Injection Phobia Inventory (BIPI; Mas et al. 2010): self-report inventory measuring cognitive, physiological and behavioural responses across BII situations.

Fear-related disorders: explanations

Biological explanation

  • Genetic vulnerability: phobias run in families (Öst 1992) — relatives of phobics show higher rates.
  • BII phobia especially shows strong familial/heritable pattern and the inherited diphasic (vasovagal) response.
  • Evolutionary preparedness: we are biologically prepared to fear ancestrally dangerous stimuli (snakes, blood, heights).
  • Explains why phobias focus on certain stimuli, not random objects.

Behavioural explanation (classical conditioning)

  • Phobia is a learned association: neutral stimulus (NS) + fear-producing unconditioned stimulus (UCS) → conditioned fear response.
  • Watson & Rayner (1920) 'Little Albert' — conditioned fear of a white rat via loud noise.
  • Maintained by operant conditioning: avoidance reduces fear = negative reinforcement (two-process theory).
  • Can be acquired by observation/modelling (social learning).

Psychodynamic explanation

  • Freud (1909) 'Little Hans': fear of horses = displaced unconscious anxiety.
  • Hans's Oedipal fear of his father was displaced onto horses (a symbol/defence mechanism).
  • The phobia protects the ego from the true, unacceptable source of anxiety.
  • The feared object symbolically represents the real unconscious conflict.
⭐ Key study — Watson & Rayner (1920) — 'Little Albert'

Aim: To demonstrate that a fear response (phobia) can be learned in a human infant through classical conditioning, and that it can generalise.

Method: Controlled observation/case study of one infant (~11 months). Baseline: Albert showed no fear of a white rat. Conditioning: each time he reached for the rat (NS) a steel bar was struck behind him producing a loud noise (UCS→fear UCR). Pairings were repeated.

Results: After several pairings Albert showed fear (crying, avoidance) to the rat alone (now a CS producing a CR). Fear generalised to other white/furry stimuli (rabbit, dog, fur coat, Santa mask).

Conclusion: Fear responses/phobias can be acquired through classical conditioning and can generalise to similar stimuli, supporting the behavioural explanation of phobias.

Anxiety & fear: treatment

Systematic desensitisation (SD)

  • Behavioural therapy based on classical conditioning (counter-conditioning).
  • Step 1: teach deep muscle relaxation.
  • Step 2: build a fear hierarchy (least to most feared situations).
  • Step 3: gradually work up the hierarchy while relaxed.
  • Reciprocal inhibition: you cannot feel relaxed and anxious at once, so relaxation replaces fear.
  • Can be in vivo (real) or in vitro (imagined).

Applied tension (for BII phobia)

  • Standard relaxation is unsuitable for BII phobia because the danger is fainting (low BP), not high arousal.
  • Applied tension: client repeatedly tenses large muscle groups (arms, legs, torso) to raise blood pressure.
  • This counteracts the diphasic vasovagal drop and prevents fainting on exposure.
  • Combined with graded exposure to blood/injection cues.

CBT

  • Combines behavioural exposure with cognitive restructuring of distorted, catastrophic thoughts.
  • Manualised, session-by-session structure.
  • Includes psychoeducation, exposure tasks, and relapse prevention.
⭐ Key study — Chapman & DeLapp (2013) — manualised CBT for blood-injection-injury phobia

Method: Case-study format describing a structured programme of approximately 9 sessions. Included psychoeducation, applied tension training (to prevent fainting), building a fear hierarchy, and graded exposure to feared blood/injection stimuli, with cognitive components.

Results: The client showed reduced fear/avoidance and improved ability to tolerate BII situations (e.g. blood draws) by the end of treatment; fainting response managed via applied tension (check book for exact outcome measures).

Conclusion: A short, manualised CBT programme incorporating applied tension can effectively treat blood-injection-injury phobia, showing structured psychological treatment is practical and replicable.

OCD: diagnosis

ICD-11: Core features

  • OCD = persistent obsessions and/or compulsions that are time-consuming (e.g. >1 hour/day) and cause distress or impairment.
  • Obsessions: recurrent, intrusive, unwanted thoughts, images or urges that cause anxiety.
  • Compulsions: repetitive behaviours or mental acts the person feels driven to perform, often to rules, to reduce anxiety or prevent a feared event.

Common presentations

  • Contamination obsessions with washing/cleaning compulsions.
  • Doubt/harm obsessions with checking compulsions.
  • Need for symmetry/order with arranging compulsions.
  • Compulsions are not realistically connected to what they aim to prevent, or are clearly excessive.

Case example: Rapoport (1989) 'Charles'

  • Charles: an adolescent with severe washing compulsions (hours of showering, hand-washing).
  • Driven by contamination obsessions; behaviours were distressing and time-consuming.
  • Illustrates the obsession→anxiety→compulsion→relief cycle in a real individual.

Measures

  • Maudsley Obsessive-Compulsive Inventory (MOCI): self-report; subscales e.g. checking, washing, doubting, slowness.
  • Yale-Brown Obsessive-Compulsive Scale (Y-BOCS): clinician-rated interview; separately rates severity of obsessions and compulsions (time, interference, distress, resistance, control).

OCD: explanations

Biological: biochemical

  • Low serotonin activity linked to OCD; disrupted serotonin function impairs regulation of anxiety and repetitive behaviour.
  • Supported by SSRIs (which raise serotonin) reducing OCD symptoms.
  • Abnormal function in the orbitofrontal cortex–basal ganglia (caudate) 'worry circuit' proposed.

Biological: genetic

  • OCD runs in families; higher concordance in MZ than DZ twins suggests heritability.
  • First-degree relatives of OCD patients show elevated rates.
  • Candidate genes affecting the serotonin system implicated (a diathesis, not a single 'OCD gene').

Cognitive explanation

  • Thinking errors: everyone has intrusive thoughts, but OCD sufferers misinterpret them as significant and threatening.
  • Inflated sense of responsibility — belief they must act to prevent harm.
  • This drives anxiety and neutralising compulsions; thought-action fusion (thinking = doing/causing).

Behavioural & psychodynamic

  • Behavioural: compulsions are operantly conditioned — performing them reduces anxiety, so the behaviour is negatively reinforced and repeats.
  • Psychodynamic: symptoms reflect unconscious conflict; ego defences (isolation, undoing, reaction formation) manage id impulses; fixation at the anal stage.

OCD: treatment + Clinical wrap-up

Biological treatment: SSRIs

  • SSRIs (e.g. fluoxetine, sertraline) block reuptake of serotonin, raising synaptic serotonin levels.
  • Reduces obsessions and compulsions; often first-line drug treatment.
  • Higher doses and longer time to effect than in depression (weeks).
  • Strengths: effective, easy to take; weaknesses: side effects, relapse on stopping, treats symptoms not causes.

Exposure & response prevention (ERP)

  • Behavioural therapy: client is exposed to the anxiety-triggering situation but prevented from performing the compulsion.
  • Anxiety rises then falls naturally (habituation), breaking the obsession→compulsion→relief cycle.
  • Lehmkuhl et al. (2008): ERP effective (used with a child with autism and OCD in one example).
  • Often delivered within CBT with cognitive restructuring.

CBT

  • Combines ERP with challenging thinking errors (inflated responsibility, thought-action fusion).
  • Structured, can be manualised, and delivered face-to-face or remotely.
  • Tackles both maintaining behaviours and distorted cognitions.

Results: Both groups improved significantly on OCD symptoms, with no significant difference between telephone and face-to-face CBT — telephone CBT was non-inferior (check book for exact scores).

Conclusion: Telephone-administered CBT is as effective as face-to-face CBT for OCD, offering a more accessible, flexible and cost-effective delivery method.

Patient-practitioner: interpersonal skills

Non-verbal communication (practitioner)

  • Practitioner appearance/clothing affects patient confidence and trust before a word is spoken.
  • McKinstry & Wang (1991): showed patients photos of a GP in different dress. Formal dress (suit/white coat) increased patient confidence and willingness to disclose; casual dress rated lower on competence.

  • Jargon = specialist medical terminology patients may not understand; barrier to shared understanding and adherence.
  • McKinlay (1975): studied use of technical terms by staff to maternity patients. Staff used jargon even when they knew patients would not understand it.
  • Patients often understood more terms than staff assumed, but staff underestimated them ('coping' function — jargon used to save time / maintain professional distance).
  • Poor verbal communication linked to lower satisfaction, misunderstanding of instructions, non-adherence.

Why communication matters

  • Good practitioner communication -> higher satisfaction, better recall of advice, greater adherence, faster recovery.
  • Two-way process: both verbal (clarity, avoiding jargon) and non-verbal (appearance, body language) contribute.

Patient-practitioner: diagnosis & style

Making a diagnosis

  • Diagnosis depends on the patient disclosing accurate, full information — practitioner must build enough rapport for honest disclosure.
  • False positive: test/diagnosis says disease present when it is not -> unnecessary anxiety and treatment.
  • False negative: says disease absent when it is present -> missed/delayed treatment, danger.
  • Both errors have psychological and physical costs; practitioners weigh sensitivity vs specificity.

Presenting a diagnosis

  • How news is delivered affects understanding, emotional response and adherence (clarity, empathy, checking understanding, avoiding jargon).
  • Bad news should be paced, honest and supportive; overload reduces recall.

Practitioner style

  • Doctor-centred (directed): practitioner controls the consultation, closed questions, gives instructions, little patient input — efficient, good for acute/simple problems.
  • Patient-centred (sharing): open questions, patient's views and feelings explored, shared decision-making — better for chronic conditions and satisfaction.
  • Neither is best for all situations; style should match patient and problem.
⭐ Key study — Savage & Armstrong (1990) — GP consulting style and patient satisfaction

Aim: To investigate whether a doctor-centred (directive) or patient-centred (sharing) consulting style produces greater patient satisfaction.

Method: Field experiment in one London general practice. Patients with physical problems randomly allocated so one GP used either a directive style ('you have...', clear firm statements) or a sharing style ('what do you think is wrong?'). ~200 patients (check book) completed satisfaction questionnaires immediately after and 1 week later.

Results: Patients in the directive (doctor-centred) condition reported higher satisfaction and greater confidence that they had received an effective consultation, especially for those with physical/acute complaints.

Conclusion: A directed style can produce higher satisfaction in some contexts; the 'best' style is not simply patient-centred and depends on patient and complaint type.

Misusing health services

Delay in seeking treatment

  • Safer et al. (1979) identified three stages of delay: appraisal delay (time to decide a symptom means illness), illness delay (time from deciding ill to deciding to seek care), utilisation delay (time from deciding to seek care to actually attending).
  • Total 'patient delay' = sum of these; delay increases risk, e.g. with cancer symptoms.
  • Factors increasing delay: mild/painless symptoms, fear, cost, embarrassment, competing priorities.

Alternative explanation: Health Belief Model

  • HBM explains delay via perceived susceptibility, perceived severity, perceived benefits of acting minus perceived barriers, plus cues to action.
  • Low perceived severity/susceptibility or high barriers -> longer delay in seeking treatment.

Munchausen syndrome vs malingering

  • Munchausen syndrome (factitious disorder): deliberately faking/inducing illness for the psychological role of being a patient — NO external reward. Essential feature = intentional production of symptoms; supporting features = dramatic plausible history, 'peregrination' (moving between hospitals), pathological lying, eager for invasive tests.
  • Malingering: faking illness for an EXTERNAL/tangible gain (money, time off, drugs, avoiding duty).
  • Key difference = motivation (psychological need vs external incentive).

Case example

  • Aleem & Ajarim (1995): case study of a woman with factitious illness — presented with a recurrent breast abscess she was secretly self-inducing, leading to unnecessary investigations/surgery before deception discovered.

Adherence: types & reasons for non-adherence

Types of non-adherence

  • Not following treatment: not taking medication, wrong dose/timing, stopping early once feeling better, not following lifestyle advice.
  • Not attending: missing appointments/follow-ups.
  • Can be unintentional (forgetting, misunderstanding) or intentional (deciding not to).
  • Problems caused: worse health outcomes, relapse, drug resistance (e.g. antibiotics), wasted resources, misleading data for doctors.

Rational non-adherence

  • Laba et al. (2012): adherence is a rational cost-benefit decision — patients weigh factors before taking medicine.
  • Used a discrete-choice survey: factors reducing adherence included cost of medicine, side-effects, dosing frequency/complexity, symptom relief, and beliefs about whether the medicine works.
  • Non-adherence can be a logical choice, not just forgetfulness.

Health Belief Model (Rosenstock/Becker)

  • Adherence predicted by: perceived susceptibility to illness, perceived severity, perceived benefits of treatment, perceived barriers (cost, side-effects, effort).
  • Cues to action (symptoms, reminders, media) trigger behaviour; also health motivation and self-efficacy.
  • High benefits + low barriers + felt susceptibility/severity -> greater adherence.

Adherence: measuring non-adherence

Subjective measures

  • Self-report via clinical interviews and semi-structured interviews with patients (or parents/carers).
  • Riekert & Drotar (1999): compared adolescents who did vs did not consent to a study on adherence; non-consenters/dropouts tended to have poorer adherence — self-report samples can be biased and over-estimate adherence.

Objective / behavioural measures

  • Pill counting: count remaining pills vs number that should remain — simple but patients can dump pills; doesn't prove they were taken.
  • Medication dispensers / MEMS (Medication Event Monitoring System): electronic cap records date/time each bottle is opened.
  • Chung & Naya (2000): used a Turbuhaler/electronic tracking device (MEMS-style) to measure asthma medication adherence; electronically recorded adherence (~70%, check book) was lower than self-reported adherence — objective methods reveal more non-adherence.

Biological measures

  • Blood and urine samples detect drug/metabolite levels — most direct evidence the drug was taken.
  • Objective but invasive, expensive, affected by individual metabolism, and only shows recent doses ('white-coat adherence' just before appointment).

Adherence: improving adherence

Improving adherence in children

  • Chaney et al. (2004): the 'Funhaler' — an asthma inhaler for children with incentive toys (spinner and whistle) that work only when the child breathes correctly.
  • Used self-report parent questionnaires before (normal inhaler) and after (Funhaler).

Individual behavioural techniques

  • Behavioural contracts: written agreement between patient and practitioner setting goals/rewards.
  • Prompts/reminders: texts, calls, alarms, packaging (unit-dose blister packs).
  • Customising/tailoring treatment: simplify regimen, fit to daily routine, reduce dose frequency.
  • Reinforcement of adherent behaviour.

Community interventions

  • Whole-population/environmental approaches: reminders, incentives, reducing barriers (access, cost, transport) delivered at community level rather than to one patient.
⭐ Key study — Yokley & Glenwick (1984) — increasing immunisation of preschool children

Aim: To evaluate applied community interventions (prompts and incentives) for increasing attendance at immunisation clinics for preschool children.

Method: Field experiment. Outcome = actual immunisation attendance from clinic records.

Results: The monetary incentive and the specific/increased-access prompt conditions produced significantly more immunisations than the control; a general educational prompt alone had little effect. Behavioural (incentive/specific-access) methods worked better than information alone.

Conclusion: Community-level behavioural interventions, especially incentives and specific prompts, effectively increase immunisation; simply providing information is not enough.

Pain: types & theories

Functions & types of pain

  • Function: warning/protective signal of tissue damage; promotes rest and avoidance to aid healing.
  • Acute pain: short-term (<3-6 months), clear cause, resolves with healing.
  • Chronic pain: long-term (persists beyond normal healing, >3-6 months), may have no clear ongoing cause; harder to treat, linked to depression.
  • Also: organic pain (physical cause) vs psychogenic pain (psychological, no clear physical cause).

Phantom limb pain & mirror treatment

  • Phantom limb pain: pain felt in an amputated/missing limb — evidence pain is generated by the brain, not just the periphery.
  • Mirror treatment (mirror-box therapy): mirror reflects the intact limb so the brain 'sees' the missing limb moving, reducing pain.
  • MacLachlan et al. (2004): case study of a male amputee using mirror therapy; reported reduced phantom limb pain and improved sensation of movement over sessions.

Theories of pain

  • Specificity theory (von Frey): specific pain receptors send signals directly along dedicated pathways to a pain centre in the brain — pain intensity is proportional to tissue damage. Simple but cannot explain phantom pain or psychological influences.
  • Gate control theory (Melzack & Wall 1965): a neural 'gate' in the spinal cord (substantia gelatinosa) opens/closes to control pain signals to the brain.
  • Gate opened by small-fibre (nociceptive) activity; closed by large-fibre activity (rubbing) and by descending signals from the brain.
  • Ascending: signals up to the brain. Descending control: brain (mood, attention, expectation) can close/open the gate — explains why distraction/anxiety change pain.

Pain: types & theories — evaluation & recall

Comparing the theories

  • Specificity theory: explains acute pain from clear injury but is purely biological/bottom-up; cannot explain phantom limb pain, placebo, or why identical injuries hurt differently.
  • Gate control theory: incorporates psychological (top-down) and physical factors -> explains phantom pain, distraction, mood effects; basis for treatments (TENS, mirror therapy, relaxation).
  • Gate control is more complete and supported; the exact gate mechanism is still not fully specified physiologically.

  • Phantom/mirror evidence from case studies (MacLachlan et al.) — rich and useful for rare cases but idiographic, not generalisable, and open to placebo/expectancy effects.
  • Gate control theory is testable and has strong applied value (pain management).
  • Reductionism vs holism: specificity theory is reductionist (pain = damage); gate control is more holistic (biological + psychological).

  • Reductionism vs holism — specificity theory reduces pain to tissue damage; gate control adds psychological/top-down factors (holistic).
  • Application to everyday life — gate control underpins real pain treatments (TENS, mirror therapy, distraction, relaxation).

Pain: Measuring Pain

Self-report: clinical interview

  • Clinician asks patient to describe pain in own words: location, quality, intensity, timing, triggers.
  • Rich qualitative data; builds rapport; flexible follow-up questions.
  • Subjective and open to interviewer bias; hard to standardise/compare across patients.

Psychometric measure: McGill Pain Questionnaire (MPQ)

  • Melzack: patient selects adjectives from word groups to describe pain.
  • Three dimensions: SENSORY (e.g. throbbing, stabbing), AFFECTIVE (e.g. tiring, sickening), EVALUATIVE (e.g. annoying, unbearable).
  • Produces a Pain Rating Index; body map locates pain; quantifies quality of pain, not just intensity.

Visual measures: Visual Analogue Scale (VAS)

  • 10 cm line from 'no pain' to 'worst pain imaginable'; patient marks a point.
  • Quick, simple, sensitive to change; usable across languages/ages.
  • Faces scales (e.g. Wong-Baker) used with young children.

Behavioural/observational: UAB Pain Behaviour Scale

  • University of Alabama scale: observer rates ~10 pain behaviours (vocal complaints, grimacing, limping, guarding, use of medication, time reclining).
  • Each scored 0 / 0.5 / 1 for frequency; totalled.
  • Useful when self-report unreliable (children, cognitive impairment); observable and repeatable.
⭐ Key study — Brudvik et al. (2016) — pain assessment by physicians, parents and children

Aim: Compare how physicians, parents and children rate the child's pain intensity in an emergency setting.

Method: ~243 children aged 3-15 in a Norwegian ED; child's pain rated using age-appropriate visual scales (e.g. faces/VAS) by the child, a parent and the treating physician; ratings compared for agreement (check book).

Results: Physicians systematically rated pain LOWER than both children and parents; agreement between physician and child was poor, especially for severe pain; parents' ratings were closer to the child's own than physicians' were.

Conclusion: Physicians tend to underestimate children's pain; children's self-report and parental report are valuable and should be included in assessment.

  • MPQ gives detailed multidimensional data but requires good vocabulary/reading level, limiting use with children or non-native speakers.
  • VAS is quick and quantitative but reduces complex pain to one number (reductionist).
  • Observational scales avoid reliance on language but infer internal state from behaviour, which can be misleading (stoical patients under-rated).
  • No single measure is objective; combining self-report + behavioural improves validity.

  • Standardised scales (MPQ, VAS) improve reliability and allow comparison/quantitative analysis.
  • Observer scales need inter-rater reliability checks.
  • Concurrent validity assessed by correlating different measures.

Pain: Managing & Controlling Pain

Biological: biochemical (analgesics)

  • Non-opioids: e.g. paracetamol, aspirin/NSAIDs reduce prostaglandins, ease mild-moderate pain and inflammation.
  • Opioids: e.g. morphine, codeine bind to opioid receptors in CNS, block pain signals for severe pain.
  • Local anaesthetics block nerve conduction at the site.
  • Fast and effective but risks: tolerance, dependence, side effects (drowsiness, constipation, overdose).

Psychological: cognitive strategies

  • ATTENTION DIVERSION: focus attention away from pain onto something else (e.g. counting, external focus like watching TV).
  • NON-PAIN IMAGERY: imagine a vivid pleasant scene unrelated to pain (e.g. a beach) to occupy attention.
  • COGNITIVE REDEFINITION (reinterpretation): change how the pain is thought about, e.g. positive self-statements ('I can cope, it will pass'), reappraising sensations.
  • Cheap, no drug side effects; give patient a sense of control; work best for milder/short-term or predictable pain.

Alternative: acupuncture

  • Fine needles inserted at specific points; traditionally to balance energy flow.
  • May trigger endorphin release and stimulate nerves (gate control explanation).
  • Non-drug; some evidence for chronic pain, though placebo effects debated.

Alternative: stimulation therapy / TENS

  • Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation: electrodes deliver mild current to the skin.
  • Gate control theory: stimulating large non-pain fibres 'closes the gate', reducing pain signal transmission.
  • Portable, non-invasive, patient-controlled; useful for chronic and labour pain.

Comparing approaches

  • Biological methods act fast and suit acute/severe pain but treat symptoms not coping, with dependence risk.
  • Psychological methods build long-term coping and self-efficacy but need effort/practice and suit milder pain.
  • Alternative methods (TENS, acupuncture) are non-invasive but evidence is mixed and placebo hard to rule out.
  • Best practice often combines methods (e.g. analgesics + cognitive strategies) for chronic pain.

Evaluation & application

  • Cognitive strategies raise validity questions: hard to measure objectively how much attention is diverted.
  • Individual differences: distraction works better for some personalities/pain types than others.
  • Application: antenatal classes teach breathing/imagery; TENS widely used in physiotherapy and childbirth.
  • Reduces reliance on drugs, lowering side-effect and addiction burden.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Reductionism vs holism — biochemical analgesics target biology alone vs psychological strategies treating the whole person.
  • Application to everyday life — TENS, imagery and acupuncture are used in clinics, childbirth and chronic pain care.
  • Free will vs determinism — cognitive strategies give patients active control over their pain experience.

Stress: Sources of Stress

Physiology: Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

  • ALARM: stressor detected; sympathetic/adrenal response, adrenaline released, 'fight or flight', arousal rises.
  • RESISTANCE: body adapts and copes; arousal stays high but stabilised; cortisol sustained.
  • EXHAUSTION: prolonged stress depletes resources; defences collapse, stress-related illness appears.
  • Effects on health: suppressed immune function, raised blood pressure, cardiovascular disease, ulcers.

Life events: Holmes & Rahe SRRS

  • Social Readjustment Rating Scale: 43 life events each given a value in Life Change Units (LCUs).
  • Highest: death of spouse (100 LCU); includes negative AND positive events (marriage, new job).
  • More change (higher LCU total) = more readjustment = higher illness risk.
  • Assumes any change is stressful because it requires adaptation.

Work stress: Chandola et al. (2008), Whitehall II

  • Large longitudinal cohort of British civil servants examining work stress and coronary heart disease.
  • Chronic work stress linked to higher risk of CHD, especially in younger workers.
  • Effect partly via biological pathways (poor heart-rate variability, cortisol) AND behaviours (poor diet, less exercise).
  • Shows workplace as a major situational source of chronic stress.

Personality: Friedman & Rosenman Type A

  • TYPE A: competitive, impatient, time-pressured, hostile, achievement-driven.
  • TYPE B: relaxed, patient, easy-going.
  • Type A linked to higher rates of coronary heart disease.
  • Suggests stress and its health effects depend partly on personality (individual factor).

Stress: Sources of Stress — evaluation & recall

Evaluation of sources & measures

  • SRRS ignores individual interpretation — same event differs in meaning per person; daily hassles may predict illness better.
  • Correlational life-events research cannot prove change causes illness; illness may cause life change.
  • Type A concept criticised: hostility component may be the key toxic factor, not the whole type.
  • GAS based partly on animal (rat) research; underplays psychological appraisal of stressors.

  • Whitehall II: longitudinal design shows change over time but is costly and risks participant attrition.
  • Self-report scales (SRRS) risk memory bias and social desirability.
  • Cohort/correlational designs limit causal claims; confounding lifestyle variables.
  • Combining biological measures + self-report improves validity.

  • Individual vs situational — Type A personality (individual) vs work/life-event stressors (situational).
  • Idiographic vs nomothetic — SRRS applies one universal scale vs individual appraisal of events.
  • Nature vs nurture — personality predispositions vs environmental workplace demands shaping stress.

Stress: Measuring Stress

Biological: physiological recording

  • HEART RATE / blood pressure: rise with sympathetic arousal; measurable objectively.
  • fMRI (Wang et al. 2005): brain imaging during a stressful mental arithmetic task showed increased activity in the right prefrontal cortex and raised heart rate; identifies brain regions active under stress.
  • Objective, quantitative; but equipment is costly and the lab setting is artificial.

Biological: salivary cortisol

  • Cortisol is the key stress hormone; sampled non-invasively from saliva.
  • Evans & Wener (2007) — commuting study: longer/more crowded train commutes linked to higher salivary cortisol and poorer task performance/mood.
  • Objective biochemical marker; but cortisol varies with time of day (diurnal rhythm), diet and caffeine.

Psychological: self-report questionnaires

  • Holmes & Rahe SRRS: totals Life Change Units to estimate stress from recent life events.
  • Type A questionnaires (e.g. structured interviews / Bortner scale) assess stress-prone personality.
  • Quick, cheap, large samples; measure the person's subjective experience and appraisal.
  • Risk memory bias, social desirability, and misinterpreting items.

Stress: Managing Stress

Biofeedback

  • Electronic equipment gives real-time feedback on a bodily process (e.g. muscle tension via EMG, heart rate).
  • Budzynski et al. (1969): EMG biofeedback trained tension-headache sufferers to lower frontalis muscle tension, reducing headaches.
  • Patient learns voluntary control over 'involuntary' responses; effect persists after training.
  • Non-drug, gives control; but needs equipment, training and motivation.

Imagery & relaxation

  • Progressive muscle relaxation: tense then release muscle groups to reduce physical arousal.
  • Guided imagery: vividly picture a calm, safe scene to induce relaxation.
  • Reduces sympathetic arousal (lower heart rate, muscle tension); cheap and self-administered.

Stress Inoculation Training (SIT) — Meichenbaum

  • A cognitive-behavioural 'vaccination' against stress, in three phases:
  • 1. CONCEPTUALISATION: educate client about stress; identify and reframe stressors.
  • 2. SKILLS ACQUISITION (and rehearsal): learn coping skills — relaxation, positive self-talk, problem-solving; practise them.
  • 3. APPLICATION (and follow-through): apply skills to real/imagined stressors gradually, building resilience.
  • Targets thoughts AND behaviour; builds long-term coping, not just symptom relief.
⭐ Key study — Bridge et al. (1988) — relaxation and imagery for stress in breast cancer patients

Aim: Test whether relaxation, with or without imagery, reduces stress/mood disturbance in women receiving radiotherapy for breast cancer.

Method: Field experiment; ~139 women with early breast cancer randomly allocated to three groups: (1) control (talk about themselves), (2) relaxation training, (3) relaxation PLUS guided imagery; practised daily for 6 weeks; mood measured by Profile of Mood States (POMS) and Leeds scales (check book).

Results: Both intervention groups showed less mood disturbance than the control; the relaxation-plus-imagery group showed the greatest improvement in mood; effect grew over the 6 weeks.

Conclusion: Relaxation reduces stress in cancer patients, and adding imagery enhances the benefit; simple psychological techniques usefully support medical treatment.

  • Biofeedback is objective and gives measurable control but requires costly equipment; simple relaxation may achieve similar effects more cheaply.

Health Promotion: Strategies

Fear arousal

  • Uses frightening messages/images to motivate healthier behaviour (e.g. graphic anti-smoking ads).
  • Assumes fear creates discomfort people act to reduce by changing behaviour.
  • Works best at MODERATE fear plus a clear, achievable action (efficacy); too much fear can cause denial/avoidance.

Example study: Janis & Feshbach (1953) — dental hygiene

  • Aim: test effect of level of fear on adopting good dental hygiene.
  • ~200 US high-school students shown a talk on tooth decay at HIGH, MODERATE or LOW fear (plus control).
  • High-fear group reported most anxiety but the LOW-fear group showed the greatest actual change in tooth-brushing a week later.
  • Conclusion: high fear can backfire (denial); minimal/low fear plus practical guidance is more effective.

Providing information

  • Gives clear, accurate knowledge so people can make informed choices (leaflets, labels, campaigns).
  • Assumes lack of behaviour change is partly due to lack of knowledge.
  • How information is framed (gain vs loss; source credibility) affects impact.

Example study: Lewin et al. (1992) — cardiac rehabilitation

  • Heart-attack patients given a self-help rehabilitation booklet (the 'Heart Manual') with information and relaxation guidance vs standard care.
  • Booklet group had less anxiety and depression and fewer GP visits/readmissions over follow-up.
  • Conclusion: well-designed information/self-management material improves recovery and reduces service use.

Health Promotion: Strategies — evaluation & recall

Ethics & effectiveness of fear appeals

  • Fear appeals can cause distress, anxiety or defensive denial — ethical concern about deliberately frightening people.
  • Fear without a clear 'what to do' (efficacy) often fails or backfires (Janis & Feshbach).
  • Information alone raises knowledge but knowledge does not reliably change behaviour (intention-behaviour gap).
  • Combining moderate fear + information + practical steps is generally most effective.

Research methods / application

  • Janis & Feshbach: lab-based independent-measures experiment — control over fear level but artificial, self-report of behaviour.
  • Field studies (Lewin) have high ecological validity and real outcome measures (readmissions).
  • Behaviour-change measures more valid than attitude/intention measures.
  • Application: public-health campaigns balance fear and clear guidance; self-help manuals reduce healthcare costs.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Application to everyday life — fear appeals and information campaigns are core public-health tools.
  • Determinism vs free will — do fear messages 'push' behaviour, or inform free choice?
  • Reductionism vs holism — behaviour change reduced to fear/knowledge vs broader social and economic factors.

Health Promotion in Schools & Worksites

Health promotion in schools: rationale

  • Schools reach whole child populations early, when habits form.
  • Can combine information, role models, rewards and peer influence.
  • Aim to build lifelong healthy behaviours (diet, exercise, hygiene).

Schools — healthy eating: Tapper et al. (2003), Food Dudes

  • 'Food Dudes' programme to increase fruit and vegetable eating in UK primary schoolchildren.
  • Uses video role models (heroic 'Food Dudes' who eat fruit/veg to gain power) + rewards (stickers, prizes) + repeated tasting.
  • Based on modelling, repeated exposure and positive reinforcement.
  • Increased children's fruit and vegetable consumption, with effects maintained at follow-up; worked even for initially fussy eaters.

Health promotion in worksites: rationale

  • Adults spend much time at work; workplace can target safety and lifestyle.
  • Employers benefit from fewer accidents, less absence, higher productivity.
  • Methods: incentives, feedback, safety campaigns, token economies.

Worksites — safety: Fox et al. (1987), token economy in mining

  • Token economy at two US open-cast mines to improve safety behaviour.
  • Workers earned tokens (trading stamps) for working without lost-time injuries and for group safety; tokens exchanged for goods; tokens lost for injuries/unsafe acts.
  • Based on operant conditioning (positive reinforcement).
  • Over several years lost-time injuries and costs fell sharply and stayed low — a durable, cost-effective improvement.

Changing Health Beliefs + Health Wrap-up

Unrealistic optimism: Weinstein (1980)

  • People believe they are LESS likely than peers to experience negative events (illness, accidents) and MORE likely to experience positive ones.
  • Weinstein (1980): ~250 US students rated their own chances vs classmates' for 42 events; showed a systematic optimistic bias.
  • Causes: lack of personal experience, belief the problem is preventable, stereotype of the 'typical victim' as unlike oneself.
  • Barrier to health promotion: 'it won't happen to me' reduces precautionary behaviour.

Positive psychology: Seligman (2004)

  • Focus on strengths and wellbeing, not just treating illness.
  • Three routes to happiness: PLEASANT LIFE (positive emotion, enjoying pleasures), GOOD LIFE (engagement/flow using signature strengths), MEANINGFUL LIFE (using strengths for something larger than oneself).
  • Wellbeing can be actively built, supporting prevention and resilience.
  • Underpins interventions that cultivate gratitude, strengths and optimism.

Applying the ideas

  • Tackle unrealistic optimism by personalising risk and countering the 'typical victim' stereotype.
  • Positive-psychology programmes taught in schools to boost adolescent wellbeing and reduce depression.
⭐ Key study — Shoshani & Steinmetz (2014) — school-based positive psychology intervention

Aim: Test whether a positive psychology programme improves adolescents' mental health and wellbeing and reduces distress.

Method: Longitudinal controlled study in Israel; ~1,000+ middle-school students; intervention school received teacher-delivered positive-psychology sessions (building strengths, optimism, positive emotion) over ~one year vs a control school; self-report measures of self-esteem, optimism, wellbeing, anxiety and depression before and after (check book).

Results: Intervention students showed increases in self-esteem, optimism, self-efficacy and general wellbeing and DECREASES in anxiety, depression and general distress compared with controls.

Conclusion: School-based positive psychology can improve wellbeing and reduce psychological distress in adolescents; supports prevention-focused mental health promotion.

  • Descriptive stats: measures of central tendency (mean/median/mode) and spread (range/SD)

Key studies mastery - Clinical

Freeman 2003 - VR & persecutory ideation

  • Aim: investigate whether persecutory (paranoid) thoughts occur in a neutral virtual-reality environment
  • Method: experiment; non-clinical participants entered a VR room with neutral computer avatars
  • Finding: a substantial proportion attributed persecutory/hostile intent to avatars, showing paranoia in non-clinical people
  • Evaluation: high control and standardisation (lab experiment) but low ecological validity/artificial VR setting

Oruc 1997 - genetics of bipolar

  • Aim: examine association between serotonin and dopamine-related genes and bipolar affective disorder
  • Method: correlational/case-control genetic analysis comparing patients with controls (by sex too)
  • Finding: weak/limited associations; some sex-specific links but no strong single-gene cause
  • Evaluation: objective biological measures (reliable) but correlational - cannot establish genetic causation

Grant 2008 - opiate antagonists in gambling

  • Aim: test whether nalmefene (an opioid antagonist) reduces pathological gambling vs placebo
  • Method: randomised double-blind placebo-controlled drug trial
  • Finding: higher-dose nalmefene reduced gambling symptoms more than placebo
  • Evaluation: rigorous double-blind RCT controls placebo effects but side-effects/high dropout limit application

Chapman & DeLapp 2013 - CBT for BII phobia

  • Aim: treat blood-injection-injury phobia using CBT with applied tension
  • Method: case study/clinical intervention describing therapy and applied tension technique
  • Finding: applied tension counters the fainting (vasovagal) response and reduces phobic avoidance
  • Evaluation: rich detailed case (idiographic) but low generalisability from a case study

Lovell 2006 - telephone CBT for OCD

  • Aim: test whether CBT delivered by telephone is as effective as face-to-face for OCD
  • Method: randomised controlled trial comparing telephone vs face-to-face CBT
  • Finding: telephone CBT was non-inferior - similar symptom improvement, with greater access
  • Evaluation: RCT with real patients (high validity) but self-report OCD measures may be biased

Key studies mastery - Health

Savage & Armstrong 1990 - consulting style & satisfaction

  • Aim: compare directing (doctor-centred) vs sharing (patient-centred) consulting styles on patient satisfaction
  • Method: field experiment; GP randomly used directing or sharing style; patients rated satisfaction by questionnaire
  • Finding: patients were generally more satisfied with the directing style, especially for physical problems
  • Evaluation: real consultations (high ecological validity) but single-doctor sample limits generalisability

Yokley & Glenwick 1984 - immunisation intervention

  • Aim: test which community prompts increase children's immunisation attendance
  • Method: field experiment comparing conditions (e.g. general prompt, specific prompt, incentive, monetary) vs control
  • Finding: specific prompts and incentives raised attendance more than general prompts/control
  • Evaluation: controlled real-world design (applicable) but findings culture/time-specific to that community

Brudvik 2016 - pain assessment

  • Aim: compare pain ratings of children given by doctors, parents and the children themselves
  • Method: comparison study using pain rating scales across the three rater groups
  • Finding: doctors tended to underestimate children's pain relative to parents and children
  • Evaluation: practically important for treatment; but rating scales are subjective and age-dependent

Bridge 1988 - relaxation & imagery in breast cancer

  • Aim: test whether relaxation (plus imagery) reduces mood disturbance in breast cancer patients during radiotherapy
  • Method: field experiment; patients allocated to control, relaxation, or relaxation + imagery groups
  • Finding: relaxation groups (especially with imagery) showed less mood disturbance than control
  • Evaluation: RCT-style control with real patients (valid) but self-report mood measures and small sample

Shoshani & Steinmetz 2014 - positive psychology in schools

  • Aim: test whether a positive psychology school intervention improves adolescents' mental health/wellbeing
  • Method: longitudinal field experiment; intervention vs control schools, self-report measures over time
  • Finding: intervention reduced distress (anxiety/depression) and improved self-esteem/optimism
  • Evaluation: large sample and real setting (generalisable) but reliance on self-report and possible demand characteristics

Paper 4 application practice (timed)

Answering 'describe/evaluate the research method' questions

  • Name the method precisely (lab experiment, field experiment, self-report/questionnaire, correlation, case study, observation) as used in the named key study.
  • Describe HOW it was used in that study: IV/DV or co-variables, controls, sample, procedure - link every point to the study, not generic textbook detail.
  • For evaluate: give strengths and weaknesses of the method AS APPLIED here (e.g. lab = high control but low ecological validity in this specific task).
  • Always contextualise: 'In Canli et al., the fMRI scanner controlled extraneous variables but the artificial setting reduced ecological validity.'

Application questions (AO2)

  • Read the novel scenario/stem carefully; underline the person, behaviour and setting.
  • Do NOT just describe a study - apply the concept or method directly to the stem's details, naming the person/situation given.
  • Use the stem's own vocabulary in your answer to show explicit application.

Section B planning-question technique

  • State the general features of the chosen method (what a lab experiment / observation / questionnaire is).
  • Then give SPECIFIC features in replicable detail: exact IV/DV and operationalisation, sampling method, how many participants, materials, step-by-step procedure, how data is recorded/analysed.
  • Include controls, standardised instructions, and how you would measure the variables.
  • Then evaluate the plan: validity (internal/ecological), reliability (standardisation/replicability), and ethics (consent, confidentiality, protection, right to withdraw) - each applied to YOUR plan.
  • Make it so detailed another researcher could copy it exactly.
