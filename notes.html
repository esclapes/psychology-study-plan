Orientation: how Paper 3 & 4 work

Key terms

  • Specialist Options: the four applied areas of psychology you study at A Level (Clinical, Health, Organisational, Consumer). You answer questions on only the two you have been taught, which for you are Clinical and Health.
  • AO1 (knowledge and understanding): showing you know and can describe theories, studies and concepts accurately. This is the 'describe' skill.
  • AO2 (application): taking psychological knowledge and applying it to a novel scenario, a real-world problem, or a research design. This is the 'use it' skill.
  • AO3 (evaluation): making reasoned judgements, for example about strengths and weaknesses, methodology, ethics, or how well a theory is supported. This is the 'judge it' skill.
  • Mark bands: examiners place answers into bands (levels) based on quality, so a top-band answer is detailed, accurate, well-organised and directly relevant, not just longer.

Paper 3: Specialist Options (Approaches, Issues & Debates)

  • Duration and weight: 1 hour 30 minutes, 60 marks total, worth 25% of the A Level. You answer questions on your two chosen options only, so you split time roughly 45 minutes per option.
  • Structure per option: three short-answer questions worth 14 marks in total, plus one structured essay with part (a) 'describe' worth 6 marks and part (b) 'evaluate' worth 10 marks.
  • Because part (b) is worth 10 marks, evaluation and the use of issues and debates matter enormously here; a purely descriptive answer will cap in a low band.
  • The focus of Paper 3 is depth of understanding of the option content plus your ability to argue using approaches (biological, cognitive, etc.) and the named issues and debates.

Paper 4: Application & Research Methods

  • Duration and weight: 1 hour 30 minutes, 60 marks, worth 25% of the A Level, so together Papers 3 and 4 make up half your A Level grade.
  • Section A: two structured questions per option, each worth 18 marks, testing your ability to apply option knowledge to scenarios and suggest applications or improvements.
  • Section B: one planning question worth 24 marks split as 'plan a study' 10 marks and 'evaluate your plan' 14 marks. You design a piece of research and then critique it.
  • Section B rewards genuine research-methods skill: choosing an appropriate design, operationalising variables, sampling, controls, ethics, and then honestly evaluating weaknesses. This is why Days 2-5 (methods) are foundational.

How the two papers differ

  • The specific topics examined can differ between Paper 3 and Paper 4 within the same exam series, so you must revise the whole option, not gamble on one topic appearing on one paper.
  • Paper 3 leans toward describe-and-evaluate of theory and studies; Paper 4 leans toward apply-and-design. The underlying content is the same, but the skill demanded differs.

How to revise effectively

  • Active recall beats re-reading: close the notes and try to write out a study's aim, method, results and conclusion from memory, then check. Retrieval strengthens memory far more than highlighting.
  • Do past papers to time and mark them against the mark scheme, because knowing content is different from knowing how to earn the marks.
  • Deliberately practise applying issues and debates (Days 6-7) to every study, so that in part (b) you can reach for them automatically.
  • Spaced repetition: revisit topics after increasing intervals rather than cramming, which improves long-term retention.

What each assessment objective demands

  • AO1 marks are lost by vagueness. 'Freud said childhood matters' is weak; naming the psychosexual stages and explaining fixation is strong. Precision and use of terminology earn AO1.
  • AO2 marks require you to actually engage with the scenario given, using its specific details (the patient, the setting, the behaviour described), not to write a generic textbook answer.

How marks map to bands

  • Top band answers are accurate, detailed, coherent and fully relevant, with evaluation that is balanced and well-argued rather than a list of assertions.
  • Middle band answers show reasonable knowledge but may be less detailed, partly descriptive where evaluation is needed, or only loosely applied to the scenario.
  • Low band answers are brief, muddled, contain errors, or answer a different question than the one asked. Reading the command word carefully prevents this.

  • Command words are contracts: 'describe' wants AO1 only (no evaluation), 'evaluate' or 'discuss' wants AO3, 'suggest' or 'design' wants AO2. Match your writing to the command word.
  • Time management: with 60 marks in 90 minutes you have roughly 1.5 minutes per mark. Do not overwrite a 6-mark part and starve a 10-mark part.
  • In part (b) evaluations, plan two or three developed points rather than many shallow ones; depth scores higher than breadth.

Refresh AS research methods

Key terms

  • Reliability: the consistency of a measure. A reliable study or test gives the same results when repeated under the same conditions.
  • Validity: whether something measures what it claims to measure and whether the findings are genuine and generalisable.
  • Quantitative data: numerical data (scores, counts, times) that can be statistically analysed. Qualitative data: descriptive, non-numerical data (words, meanings, themes) that gives depth.
  • Objective vs subjective: objective measures are free from the researcher's personal bias and interpretation; subjective measures depend on personal opinion and are harder to verify.
  • Extraneous variable: any variable other than the IV that could affect the DV and should be controlled. Confounding variable: an extraneous variable that actually did vary systematically with the IV, so it confounds (ruins) the results.
  • Demand characteristics: cues that make participants guess the study's aim and change their behaviour, threatening validity.

Experiments

  • An experiment manipulates an independent variable (IV) to measure its effect on a dependent variable (DV), allowing cause-and-effect conclusions. Example: giving one group caffeine (IV) and measuring reaction time (DV).
  • Operationalisation means defining variables precisely in measurable terms, for example defining 'aggression' as 'number of hits on a doll in 5 minutes' so it can actually be measured.
  • Lab experiment: conducted in a controlled setting; high control but can be artificial. Field experiment: in a natural setting with a manipulated IV; more realistic but less control. Natural experiment: the IV varies naturally (e.g. a disaster or an existing condition) and is not manipulated by the researcher, so causation is harder to claim.
  • Experimental designs: independent measures (different participants in each condition), repeated measures (same participants in all conditions), matched pairs (different participants matched on key characteristics).
  • Repeated measures can suffer order effects (practice or fatigue improving or worsening later performance); counterbalancing (half do condition A then B, half do B then A) controls for this.

Self-reports

  • Questionnaires collect answers to set questions. Closed questions give fixed options producing quantitative data that is easy to analyse but shallow; open questions allow free responses giving rich qualitative data but harder to compare.
  • Interviews: structured (fixed questions, replicable), unstructured (free-flowing, in-depth but hard to replicate), and semi-structured (set questions plus follow-ups, a middle ground).

Case studies, observations, correlations, longitudinal

  • Case study: an in-depth investigation of one person, group or event, often using several methods. Rich and detailed but hard to generalise and open to researcher bias.
  • Observations: naturalistic (natural setting) vs controlled (set-up environment); participant (observer joins in) vs non-participant; covert (hidden) vs overt (participants know). Structured observations use behavioural categories (a coding scheme listing specific behaviours to record).
  • Correlations measure the relationship between two co-variables, shown on a scattergraph. A positive correlation means both rise together; a negative means one rises as the other falls. Correlation does not prove causation because a third variable may be responsible.
  • Longitudinal studies follow the same participants over a long period, tracking change over time, but risk participant dropout (attrition) and are time-consuming.

Refresh AS research methods — evaluation & recall

Evaluating each method

  • Lab experiments have high internal validity due to control, but low ecological validity because the artificial setting may not reflect real behaviour.
  • Self-reports are efficient and can access thoughts and feelings, but suffer social desirability bias (people answer to look good) and may be untruthful or inaccurate.
  • Observations of natural behaviour have high ecological validity, especially covert ones (no demand characteristics), but covert observation raises ethical issues around consent and privacy.
  • Correlations are useful when experiments are unethical or impractical and can reveal relationships worth investigating, but their inability to establish cause is their central limitation.

Research methods (concepts to deploy)

  • Distinguish reliability from validity: a bathroom scale that always reads 3kg too high is reliable (consistent) but not valid (wrong). You need both.
  • Extraneous vs confounding is a favourite distinction: an extraneous variable is a potential nuisance you try to control; it becomes confounding only if it systematically differs between conditions and biases the DV.
  • Quantitative data allows statistical testing and comparison; qualitative data gives meaning and detail. Strong studies sometimes combine both.

Going deeper / exam use

  • In Paper 4 you constantly choose a method and must justify it. Practise saying why a method fits the aim: correlation for relationships, experiment for causation, observation for real behaviour.
  • When evaluating any study, scan for: sampling and generalisability, control and internal validity, ecological validity, demand characteristics, and ethics. These give reliable AO3 points.
  • Always link a criticism to a consequence: 'demand characteristics may have occurred, which lowers validity because participants may not have behaved naturally.'
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Application to everyday life — field and natural experiments are prized because their realism means findings apply to real settings, a useful evaluative angle.
  • Reductionism vs holism — quantitative experiments reduce complex behaviour to a number, which qualitative case studies resist by capturing the whole picture.
  • Nomothetic vs idiographic — experiments and questionnaires seek general laws (nomothetic), while case studies build detailed individual accounts (idiographic).
  • Determinism vs free will — lab experiments assume behaviour is caused by measurable variables, a deterministic stance you can critique.

New A-level methods: RCTs, postal questionnaires, rating scales

Key terms

  • Randomised controlled trial (RCT): an experiment, common in clinical and health research, that tests a treatment by randomly allocating participants to groups and comparing outcomes.
  • Random allocation: assigning participants to conditions by chance (e.g. drawing names), so individual differences are spread evenly across groups rather than biasing one group.
  • Placebo: a fake treatment with no active ingredient, used so that the effect of merely believing you are treated (the placebo effect) can be separated from the real treatment effect.
  • Blinding: keeping people unaware of who is in which group. Single blind = participants do not know; double blind = neither participants nor the researchers interacting with them know.
  • Rating scale: a self-report tool where respondents rate something on a fixed range (e.g. 1 to 5), producing quantitative data.

RCTs in depth

  • Structure: participants are randomly allocated to an experimental group (gets the real treatment), a control group (gets no treatment or standard care) and sometimes a placebo group (gets a fake treatment). Comparing groups isolates the treatment's true effect.
  • Random allocation is the key strength: it minimises the chance that pre-existing differences between people (age, severity of illness, motivation) explain the results, because such differences are distributed by chance across groups.
  • Single blind controls participant expectations (they cannot behave differently based on knowing their group). Double blind additionally controls experimenter bias, because researchers cannot unconsciously treat groups differently or bias their measurements.
  • Why RCTs are the 'gold standard': the combination of random allocation, a control/placebo comparison, and blinding gives strong control over confounding variables and expectation effects, so cause-and-effect conclusions about a treatment are well justified.
  • Example: to test a new antidepressant, one group gets the drug, one gets an identical-looking placebo pill, neither patients nor prescribing clinicians know which is which, and mood is measured before and after.

Postal questionnaires

  • A questionnaire sent and returned by post (or its modern equivalents, email/online forms), completed by the respondent without a researcher present.
  • They can reach a large, geographically spread sample cheaply and quickly, and the absence of a researcher reduces interviewer effects and can increase honesty on sensitive topics.
  • Example: mailing a health survey about smoking habits to thousands of households across a region.

Rating scales

  • Forced/fixed-choice scales make respondents pick from set options, producing easily quantifiable, comparable data.
  • A Likert scale presents a statement (e.g. 'Exercise makes me feel better') and asks the level of agreement, typically Strongly agree / Agree / Neutral / Disagree / Strongly disagree, scored numerically.
  • Rating scales let researchers turn attitudes and feelings into numbers for statistical analysis and comparison across people or over time.

New A-level methods: RCTs, postal questionnaires, rating scales — evaluation & recall

Strengths and weaknesses of RCTs

  • Strength: high internal validity because confounding variables are controlled through random allocation, blinding and a comparison group, so differences in outcome can be attributed to the treatment.
  • Weakness: they can be expensive, time-consuming and sometimes ethically difficult, because withholding a possibly effective treatment from the control group can harm those participants.
  • Weakness: strict controls and eligibility criteria can make the sample and setting unrepresentative, lowering ecological validity and generalisability to ordinary patients.

Strengths and weaknesses of postal questionnaires

  • Strength: large samples, low cost, and reduced social desirability on sensitive topics because no researcher is watching.
  • Weakness: low response rates are common, and those who reply may differ systematically from those who do not (response bias), harming representativeness.
  • Weakness: no one is present to clarify ambiguous questions, so misunderstandings reduce validity, and you cannot verify who actually completed it.

Strengths and weaknesses of rating scales

  • Strength: produce quantitative data that is quick to analyse, compare and correlate, and Likert scales capture strength of feeling, not just yes/no.
  • Weakness: response biases such as acquiescence (tending to agree) and the central tendency bias (avoiding extreme options) distort results.
  • Weakness: a number cannot capture nuance, and different people interpret 'agree' differently, so the data may look precise while being subjective underneath.

Going deeper / exam use

  • In Section B, an RCT is often the ideal design for testing a clinical or health intervention; name random allocation, control/placebo groups and blinding to earn design marks, then evaluate the ethics of the control group.
  • If you plan a questionnaire, specify open vs closed questions and include a rating scale with a defined range, and address response rate in your evaluation.
  • Always tie your method choice to your aim: RCT for testing whether a treatment works, questionnaire/rating scale for measuring attitudes at scale.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Application to everyday life — RCTs directly test whether treatments help real patients, which is why evidence-based medicine relies on them.
  • Determinism — RCTs assume outcomes are caused by the treatment variable, reflecting a deterministic, cause-seeking model of behaviour and health.
  • Ethics (use of participants) — placebo and no-treatment control groups raise the dilemma of withholding help, a key evaluative point for planning answers.
  • Nomothetic vs idiographic — large postal questionnaires and rating scales seek general patterns across many people rather than individual depth.

Psychometric tests & validity

Key terms

  • Psychometric test: a standardised instrument designed to measure psychological characteristics such as intelligence, personality, or symptom severity, producing a score.
  • Standardisation: administering and scoring the test in exactly the same way for everyone, so scores are comparable and not affected by differing procedures.
  • Norms: typical scores from a large representative sample, used as a benchmark so an individual's score can be interpreted (e.g. above or below average).
  • Reliability (of a test): consistency, for example test-retest reliability (same result on re-testing) and inter-rater reliability (different scorers agree).
  • Validity: whether the test actually measures the construct it claims to measure.

What makes a good psychometric test

  • It must be standardised so every person takes it under the same conditions, otherwise differences in scores might reflect differences in procedure rather than the trait.
  • It must have norms from a representative sample so a raw score can be given meaning by comparison; a score of 55 means nothing without knowing the average.
  • It must be reliable (consistent over time and across scorers) and valid (measuring the right thing). A test can be reliable without being valid, but a valid test must be reasonably reliable.
  • Example: an IQ test administered with fixed instructions and time limits, scored against age-based norms, is a psychometric test.

Types of validity

  • Internal validity: whether the study genuinely measured the effect of the IV on the DV without confounds. High control raises it. Example: a well-controlled lab experiment has high internal validity.
  • External validity: whether findings generalise beyond the study (to other people, settings, times). It is the umbrella for ecological, population and temporal validity.
  • Ecological validity: whether findings generalise to real-life, everyday settings and tasks. A memory test using nonsense syllables has low ecological validity.
  • Temporal validity: whether findings still hold over time, across different eras. Attitudes to gender roles from a 1950s study may lack temporal validity today.
  • Construct validity: whether the test truly measures the underlying theoretical construct (e.g. does a 'happiness scale' really measure happiness).
  • Concurrent validity: whether a new test agrees with an established measure taken at the same time (e.g. a new depression scale correlating with an existing validated one).
  • Predictive validity: whether test scores predict future behaviour or outcomes (e.g. entrance exam scores predicting later grades).
  • Face validity: whether the test looks, on the surface, like it measures what it claims. It is the weakest form because appearance can deceive.

Psychometric tests & validity — evaluation & recall

Using validity to evaluate studies

  • Ask targeted questions: Was the setting artificial (ecological validity)? Could a confound explain results (internal validity)? Do findings apply to other groups or eras (external/temporal validity)?
  • Do not just label a study 'low validity'; specify the type and the reason, e.g. 'low ecological validity because the task (recalling word lists) does not reflect everyday memory use.'
  • Recognise trade-offs: tightly controlled lab studies gain internal validity but often lose ecological validity, and vice versa for field studies. Naming this trade-off is a sophisticated AO3 point.

How to improve validity

  • Improve internal validity by controlling extraneous variables, using standardised procedures, and adding control/placebo groups.
  • Improve ecological validity by using realistic tasks and natural settings, and by observing genuine behaviour rather than contrived responses.
  • Improve construct/concurrent validity by correlating the measure with established tests, and improve predictive validity by checking whether scores actually forecast later outcomes.
  • Reduce demand characteristics (single blind, cover stories) so participants behave naturally, protecting validity.

Research methods / going deeper

  • In clinical psychology, diagnostic tools and rating scales must be reliable and valid; questioning the validity of a diagnostic measure is a strong evaluation of clinical studies.
  • In health psychology, measures of stress, adherence or pain rely on self-report scales whose validity you can and should critique.
  • In Section B planning, mentioning how you would check validity (e.g. correlating with an existing measure for concurrent validity) shows methodological sophistication.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Application to everyday life — ecological validity determines whether findings can actually be used in real clinical or health settings, so it is central to usefulness.
  • Cultural differences — norms built on one culture may not be valid for others, an example of imposed etic and a threat to construct validity across cultures.
  • Reductionism vs holism — reducing a rich construct like depression to a single test score raises questions about construct validity and oversimplification.
  • Nomothetic vs idiographic — psychometric tests are nomothetic tools comparing individuals to norms, which may miss idiographic, individual meaning.

Hypotheses: null, one-tailed, two-tailed

Key terms

  • Hypothesis: a precise, testable statement predicting the outcome of a study. It must be operationalised so it can actually be measured.
  • Null hypothesis (H0): the statement that there is no effect or no relationship, and that any difference or correlation found is due to chance. It is what you test against.
  • Alternative / experimental hypothesis (H1): the statement that there IS an effect or relationship. It is called the experimental hypothesis in experiments and the alternative hypothesis in correlations.
  • Directional (one-tailed) hypothesis: predicts the direction of the effect (which way it will go).
  • Non-directional (two-tailed) hypothesis: predicts that there will be an effect or difference but not its direction.
  • Operationalisation: defining the variables in the hypothesis in specific, measurable terms.

The null hypothesis explained

  • Statistical testing works by assuming the null hypothesis is true and then checking whether the data are unlikely enough (usually p < 0.05) to reject it.
  • You never 'prove' the alternative hypothesis; you either reject the null (accept the alternative) or fail to reject the null. This cautious logic guards against claiming effects that are really chance.
  • Example null: 'There is no difference in recall scores between participants who revise with music and those who revise in silence; any difference is due to chance.'

Directional vs non-directional

  • Use a directional (one-tailed) hypothesis when previous research or strong theory suggests which way the effect will go. Example: 'Participants who revise in silence will recall MORE words than those who revise with music.'
  • Use a non-directional (two-tailed) hypothesis when there is no clear prior evidence for a direction, or findings are mixed. Example: 'There will be a DIFFERENCE in recall between the silence and music conditions.'
  • For correlations: directional example 'There will be a POSITIVE correlation between hours of sleep and exam score'; non-directional example 'There will be a correlation (positive or negative) between hours of sleep and exam score.'

Worked examples

  • Study on caffeine and reaction time. Experimental (directional): 'Participants given caffeine will have FASTER reaction times (ms) than those given a placebo.' Null: 'There will be no difference in reaction time between the caffeine and placebo groups; any difference is due to chance.'
  • Study on stress and illness. Alternative (non-directional): 'There will be a correlation between self-reported stress score and number of illness days.' Null: 'There will be no correlation between stress score and illness days.'
  • Notice each variable is operationalised (reaction time in ms, stress as a score, illness as days), which is required for full marks.

Hypotheses: null, one-tailed, two-tailed — evaluation & recall

When to use each

  • Choose one-tailed only when justified by prior research or theory; choosing it just to sound confident is a mistake because if the effect goes the other way you cannot claim support.
  • Choose two-tailed when the field is uncertain or contradictory; it is the safer, more cautious choice and is often preferred in exam answers unless direction is clearly warranted.
  • The null hypothesis is always non-directional in form (it states no effect), regardless of whether your alternative is one- or two-tailed.

Common mistakes

  • Writing an aim instead of a hypothesis: 'To investigate whether caffeine affects reaction time' is an aim, not a testable prediction. A hypothesis must predict an outcome.
  • Forgetting to operationalise: 'Caffeine improves performance' is too vague; specify the measured DV.
  • Muddling correlation and difference wording: correlations predict a relationship between co-variables, not a difference between groups.
  • Writing a directional null hypothesis: the null always states 'no difference/no relationship,' never a direction.
  • Omitting 'due to chance' from the null when the mark scheme expects it.

Research methods / going deeper

  • In Section B you will often be asked to write a hypothesis; earn marks by making it (a) testable, (b) operationalised, and (c) correctly directional or non-directional with justification.
  • Link the hypothesis to the statistical test logic: rejecting the null at p < 0.05 means less than 5% probability the result is due to chance.
  • Practise converting an aim into both a null and an alternative hypothesis quickly; it is a frequent, easy-to-earn exam task.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Determinism — hypotheses assume behaviour follows lawful, predictable causes that can be stated in advance and tested.
  • Nomothetic approach — hypothesis testing seeks general laws across groups, the hallmark of a nomothetic, scientific psychology.
  • Application to everyday life — well-operationalised hypotheses lead to clear, usable findings that can inform real interventions.
  • Reductionism — reducing a complex prediction to two operationalised variables can oversimplify, but is necessary for testability.

Issues & debates (part 1)

Key terms

  • Issue/debate: a recurring theme that runs across all of psychology and provides a framework for evaluating theories and studies in part (b) essays.
  • The four in this set: application to everyday life; individual vs situational explanations; nature vs nurture; and the use of children in research.
  • Using debates well means not just naming them but applying them to the specific study or topic and drawing a judgement.

Application of psychology to everyday life

  • Definition: the extent to which psychological research and theory can be used practically to benefit society, for example in treatment, policy or improving wellbeing.
  • Why it matters: usefulness is a key measure of a theory's value; research that helps real people has strong justification.
  • Clinical example: CBT for depression comes directly from cognitive theory and is widely and effectively used in therapy, showing high practical application.
  • Health example: research on the health belief model is applied to design campaigns that increase screening uptake and healthier behaviour.

Individual vs situational explanations

  • Definition: whether behaviour is best explained by factors within the person (personality, biology, dispositions) or by the external situation and environment.
  • Why it matters: it shapes how we treat and blame behaviour; over-attributing to the individual can ignore powerful situational forces.
  • Clinical example: is schizophrenia caused by individual factors (dopamine, genetics) or situational ones (stressful family environment, life events)? A full account uses both.
  • Health example: is unhealthy eating due to the individual's willpower and personality, or the situation (food advertising, poverty, availability)? Situational factors are often underestimated.

Nature vs nurture

  • Definition: the debate over whether behaviour arises from innate, genetic/biological factors (nature) or from environment, learning and experience (nurture).
  • Why it matters: it determines whether we target treatment at biology (drugs) or environment (therapy, social change), and most psychologists now favour an interactionist position.
  • Clinical example: depression has a genetic vulnerability (nature) that may be triggered by stressful life events (nurture) — the diathesis-stress model.
  • Health example: addiction may reflect an inherited predisposition (nature) combined with learned associations and social context (nurture).

Use of children in research

  • Definition: the special ethical and methodological considerations when children are participants, because they are vulnerable and cannot fully give informed consent.
  • Why it matters: children cannot always understand or consent, are more easily distressed, and are more suggestible, so researchers must obtain parental consent and protect from harm.
  • Clinical example: studying childhood disorders requires child participants but demands careful safeguarding and parental consent.
  • Health example: research on children's diet or exercise interventions must weigh valuable findings against the duty to protect young participants from harm or pressure.

Issues & debates (part 1) — evaluation & recall

Why these debates strengthen evaluation

  • They convert vague evaluation into structured, examiner-recognised argument. Saying a theory 'takes an interactionist nature-nurture view' is far stronger than 'it's a bit of both.'
  • They let you compare approaches: a biological explanation sits on the 'nature' and 'individual' side, while a behaviourist one sits on 'nurture' and 'situational.'
  • They provide balance: for most debates you can argue both sides and then reach a reasoned conclusion, which top bands reward.

Research methods link

  • Nature-nurture research often uses twin and adoption studies (comparing genetic vs environmental similarity), which you can evaluate for confounds.
  • Individual vs situational is testable by manipulating the situation (as in social experiments) and seeing if 'ordinary' people behave unexpectedly.
  • Use of children raises reliability/validity issues too: children's suggestibility can threaten the validity of self-report data.

Going deeper / exam use — weaving a debate into part (b)

  • Template: (1) name the debate, (2) state which side the theory/study falls on and why, (3) give evidence from the study, (4) give the counter-position, (5) conclude with a judgement.
  • Worked template sentence: 'This study takes a largely [individual/situational] view because [reason with evidence]; however, a [opposing] explanation would argue [point], so a more complete account is [interactionist/balanced conclusion].'

Issues & debates (part 2)

Key terms

  • The four debates in this set: cultural differences; reductionism vs holism; determinism vs free will; and idiographic vs nomothetic.
  • As before, marks come from applying each debate to specific content and reaching a judgement, not from defining it in isolation.
  • Several of these debates cluster together, which lets you build sophisticated, linked arguments.

Cultural differences

  • Definition: the ways psychological findings and behaviour vary across cultures, and the risk of assuming one culture's findings are universal.
  • Ethnocentrism: judging other cultures by the standards of your own, treating your culture as the norm. This biases research and diagnosis.
  • Individualist cultures (e.g. much of the West) value independence and the self; collectivist cultures (e.g. many East Asian societies) value the group and interdependence, which affects behaviour and mental health.
  • Imposed etic: applying a technique or theory developed in one culture to another as if it were universal, when it may not fit. Example: a Western-developed depression measure imposed on a very different culture may misdiagnose.
  • Clinical example: symptoms and expression of mental illness differ culturally, so diagnostic tools built in one culture may lack validity elsewhere. Health example: health beliefs and behaviours vary culturally, so a campaign designed for one culture may fail in another.

Reductionism vs holism

  • Reductionism: explaining complex behaviour by breaking it down to simpler components (e.g. biological reductionism explaining depression purely via neurotransmitters).
  • Holism: explaining behaviour by considering the whole person and all interacting factors together, rather than isolated parts.
  • Why it matters: reductionism enables precise, scientific, testable research but risks oversimplifying; holism is richer but harder to test.
  • Clinical example: reducing schizophrenia to dopamine ignores social and psychological factors. Health example: reducing obesity to calories-in-calories-out ignores emotional, social and economic factors.

Determinism vs free will

  • Determinism: the view that behaviour is caused by factors beyond our control. Biological determinism (genes, brain chemistry), environmental determinism (learning, reinforcement), and psychic determinism (unconscious drives, Freud).
  • Free will: the view that people can choose their behaviour and are responsible for it.
  • Why it matters: determinism supports scientific prediction and treatment but can remove responsibility; free will underpins accountability but is hard to study scientifically.
  • Clinical example: is a disorder biologically determined, or can the person exercise choice in recovery (e.g. engaging with therapy)? Health example: is smoking a determined addiction or a free choice? This affects blame and treatment.

Idiographic vs nomothetic

  • Idiographic: focusing on the individual in depth, capturing unique experience (e.g. detailed case studies).
  • Nomothetic: seeking general laws that apply to everyone, using large samples and quantitative methods.
  • Why it matters: nomothetic research generalises and predicts but can miss individual meaning; idiographic research is rich but hard to generalise.
  • Clinical example: a case study of one patient (idiographic) vs a large diagnostic survey (nomothetic). Health example: an in-depth interview about one person's illness experience vs population health statistics.

  • The reductionism–determinism–nomothetic cluster: reductionist explanations tend to be deterministic (behaviour caused by simple factors) and nomothetic (seeking general laws), which is the classic 'scientific' stance. Biological explanations usually sit in all three at once.

Schizophrenia: diagnosis & symptoms

Key terms

  • Schizophrenia is a severe, chronic psychotic disorder in which a person loses contact with reality; the word means 'split mind', referring to a split between thought, emotion and behaviour, NOT a split personality (a common misconception).
  • Psychosis means a loss of contact with reality, typically involving delusions and/or hallucinations, so the person cannot reliably tell what is real from what is not.
  • A delusion is a fixed false belief held with strong conviction despite clear evidence it is untrue (e.g. believing the TV is sending you personal messages).
  • A hallucination is a false sensory perception experienced without any external stimulus (e.g. hearing voices when no one is speaking); auditory hallucinations are the most common in schizophrenia.

ICD-11 diagnostic criteria

  • ICD-11 requires at least two symptoms from a defined list, at least one of which must be a 'core' symptom (persistent delusions, persistent hallucinations, disorganised thinking, or experiences of influence/passivity such as believing one's thoughts are controlled by outside forces).
  • Symptoms must be present for most of the time for at least one month, ensuring the disturbance is persistent rather than a brief reaction.
  • The symptoms must not be attributable to another health condition (e.g. a brain tumour) or to substance use/withdrawal, so clinicians must rule out organic and drug causes first.
  • ICD-11 has moved away from rigid subtypes (like paranoid or catatonic schizophrenia in older systems) and instead uses symptom specifiers to describe the individual clinical picture.

Positive symptoms (added to normal experience)

  • Positive symptoms are experiences ADDED on top of normal functioning; they are called 'positive' in the mathematical sense of an addition, not because they are good.
  • Delusions are the most prominent positive symptom: bizarre or non-bizarre false beliefs the person is certain of, such as believing they are being followed.
  • Hallucinations, most often hearing voices, feel completely real to the person and may comment on their behaviour or issue commands.
  • Disorganised speech/thought is inferred from speech that jumps illogically between topics ('derailment' or 'loosening of associations'), or in severe cases becomes incomprehensible ('word salad'), reflecting disordered underlying thinking.

Negative symptoms (loss of normal function)

  • Negative symptoms involve a reduction or absence of normal behaviours and emotions, and are often more disabling long-term and harder to treat than positive symptoms.
  • Avolition is a severe lack of motivation and inability to initiate or persist in goal-directed activity, so the person may sit inactive for long periods.
  • Alogia (poverty of speech) is a marked reduction in the amount and content of speech; answers become brief and empty.
  • Anhedonia is the loss of ability to experience pleasure from activities once enjoyed, and flat (blunted) affect is a reduction in emotional expression shown through the face, voice tone and gestures.

Types of delusion

  • Delusions of persecution: the belief that one is being threatened, spied on, plotted against or harmed by others; these are the most common type and are central to paranoia.
  • Delusions of grandeur: an inflated belief in one's own power, importance or identity, e.g. believing oneself to be a famous historical figure or to have special powers.
  • Delusions of reference: the belief that neutral external events carry special personal meaning, e.g. thinking newspaper headlines or song lyrics are secret messages aimed at you.
  • Delusions of control: the belief that one's thoughts, feelings or actions are being controlled by an outside force (also called passivity experiences), e.g. feeling a machine is inserting thoughts into your mind.
⭐ Key study — Freeman et al. (2003) — Can virtual reality be used to investigate persecutory ideation?

Aim: To investigate whether persecutory (paranoid) ideation could be studied experimentally in a controlled, standardised setting using virtual reality (VR), and to identify factors that predict who has such thoughts. VR was chosen because it lets every participant be exposed to exactly the same neutral social environment, removing the confound that in real life paranoid people may genuinely be treated differently.

Method: A pilot experimental study. The sample was 24 non-clinical adult members of the general public who spanned the range of paranoid tendencies (check book for exact N and demographics). Participants first completed questionnaire measures of paranoia, anxiety, and interpersonal sensitivity. They then entered a VR scene depicting a neutral environment (a library) populated by computer-generated characters (avatars) that were deliberately programmed to behave in a neutral, ambiguous way (neither hostile nor especially friendly). After immersion, participants reported their thoughts and feelings about the avatars, which were then rated for persecutory content.

Results: Most appraisals of the avatars were neutral or positive, but a proportion of participants did form persecutory interpretations of the neutral characters, e.g. believing the avatars were hostile, staring, or talking about them. Participants also attributed mental states to the avatars, showing they reacted to them as if they were real social beings. Persecutory thinking in VR was associated with higher levels of anxiety, interpersonal sensitivity and pre-existing paranoid tendencies (check book for exact figures).

Conclusion: Persecutory ideation can be studied experimentally using VR because paranoid interpretations arise even when the social environment is identical and objectively neutral for everyone. This supports a cognitive view that paranoia comes from how ambiguous social information is interpreted, and shows VR is a valid, ethical research tool for studying delusional thinking.

Schizophrenia: diagnosis & symptoms — evaluation & recall

Evaluating the diagnostic criteria

  • Strength — reliability: standardised systems like ICD-11 give clinicians a shared checklist, improving inter-rater reliability so different psychiatrists are more likely to reach the same diagnosis for the same patient.
  • Weakness — validity and symptom overlap: many symptoms of schizophrenia (e.g. flat affect, avolition, delusions) overlap with severe depression, bipolar disorder and schizoaffective disorder, which can lead to misdiagnosis and questions co-morbidity raises about whether these are truly distinct disorders.
  • Weakness — cultural bias: certain experiences (e.g. hearing the voice of an ancestor) may be normal within some cultures but pathologised by Western diagnostic manuals, risking over-diagnosis in minority groups.
  • Strength of positive/negative distinction: separating positive and negative symptoms is clinically useful because they respond differently to drugs — antipsychotics reduce positive symptoms far more effectively than negative ones.

Research methods

  • Freeman used a controlled experiment via VR, giving high internal validity and control over the independent variable (the identical neutral scene) — a real strength, as everyone genuinely received the same social input.
  • However, using a non-clinical sample means we must be cautious generalising to people with diagnosed schizophrenia, so the study measures paranoid ideation on a continuum rather than clinical delusions.
  • VR raises ecological validity questions: reactions to cartoon-like avatars may not fully match reactions to real people, though participants did treat avatars as social agents.
  • Diagnosis research often relies on self-report and clinical interview, which can be affected by social desirability and by the fact that people with delusions may lack insight into their own condition.

Going deeper / exam use

  • Use the positive vs negative symptom distinction as a ready-made structure for a 'describe the symptoms' answer — define each category, then give named examples with a sentence of explanation each.
  • Freeman is your go-to key study for both symptoms (persecutory delusions) and, later, the cognitive explanation, so link it across topics to show synoptic understanding.
  • When evaluating diagnosis, always pair a reliability point with a validity point — examiners reward showing you understand these are different concepts.
  • Remember to specify ICD-11 terminology rather than older DSM subtypes, since the specification uses ICD.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Nature vs nurture: schizophrenia's clear genetic component (nature) sits alongside evidence that stress and life experience (nurture) trigger onset, so diagnosis captures a condition best explained by an interaction of both.
  • Free will vs determinism: symptoms such as delusions of control imply the person experiences their own behaviour as determined by outside forces, and the biological basis of the disorder supports a determinist view of the sufferer's actions.
  • Idiographic vs nomothetic: ICD-11's use of symptom specifiers rather than fixed subtypes is more idiographic, describing each patient's unique clinical picture, while the standardised checklist itself is nomothetic.
  • Usefulness of psychological research: Freeman's VR method shows how research can produce ethical, controlled tools to study distressing experiences that could not be induced directly, informing later VR therapies.

Schizophrenia: explanations

Key terms

  • Concordance rate is the probability that if one twin (or relative) has a disorder, the other also has it; it is used to estimate the genetic contribution to a disorder.
  • Polygenic means a trait or disorder is influenced by many genes each of small effect, rather than a single gene — schizophrenia is now understood to be polygenic.
  • The diathesis-stress model proposes that a person inherits a vulnerability (diathesis) to schizophrenia, but the disorder only develops if that vulnerability is triggered by environmental stress.
  • A neurotransmitter is a chemical messenger that carries signals between neurons across the synapse; dopamine is the neurotransmitter central to the biochemical explanation of schizophrenia.

Biological explanation 1: genetics

  • Twin studies compare identical (monozygotic, MZ, 100% shared genes) with non-identical (dizygotic, DZ, ~50% shared genes) twins; concordance is much higher in MZ than DZ pairs, indicating a strong genetic influence (Gottesman's family studies report roughly 48% MZ vs 17% DZ — check book).
  • Family studies show that the closer the genetic relationship to someone with schizophrenia, the greater one's own risk: a child of two affected parents has a far higher lifetime risk than a distant cousin.
  • Crucially, MZ concordance is well below 100%, which proves genes alone cannot cause schizophrenia — the environment must also play a part.
  • Because no single 'schizophrenia gene' exists, the disorder is regarded as polygenic, with many candidate genes each slightly raising risk; this fits the diathesis-stress idea that inherited vulnerability plus environmental stress (e.g. urban living, cannabis use, family conflict, trauma) together produce onset.

Biological explanation 2: the dopamine hypothesis

  • The ORIGINAL dopamine hypothesis proposed that schizophrenia is caused by an excess of dopamine or by an over-sensitivity of dopamine receptors, leading to too much dopamine activity in the brain.
  • This was supported by two drug observations: antipsychotic drugs that block dopamine receptors reduce positive symptoms, while amphetamines and L-dopa, which increase dopamine, can produce schizophrenia-like symptoms in healthy people.
  • The REVISED dopamine hypothesis is more precise about location: it links positive symptoms (delusions, hallucinations) to EXCESS dopamine activity in the mesolimbic pathway (hyperdopaminergia).
  • It links negative and cognitive symptoms to a DEFICIT of dopamine activity in the prefrontal cortex (hypodopaminergia), which better explains why one disorder shows both symptom types and why drugs that only block dopamine fail to fix negative symptoms.

Cognitive explanation

  • The cognitive explanation argues schizophrenia results from faulty (dysfunctional) information processing, so symptoms arise from the way the person thinks about and interprets their experiences.
  • Attentional bias means sufferers pay disproportionate attention to threatening information, which can feed persecutory delusions by making the world seem hostile (as Freeman's VR work suggests).
  • 'Jumping to conclusions' (JTC) is a reasoning bias in which people with delusions gather less evidence before reaching a firm conclusion, so they accept delusional beliefs too readily (tested using bead-drawing 'probabilistic reasoning' tasks).
  • Frith proposed that schizophrenia involves a failure of metacognition/central control — an inability to monitor one's own intentions and distinguish self-generated thoughts from external events; this neatly explains why inner speech is misattributed to an external voice (hallucination) and why the person feels their actions are controlled by others (passivity).

Schizophrenia: explanations — evaluation & recall

Evaluating the genetic explanation

  • Strength — consistent evidence: twin, family and adoption studies reliably show higher risk with closer genetic relatedness, and adoption studies (children raised away from affected biological parents) help separate genes from shared environment.
  • Weakness — genes are not sufficient: MZ concordance below 100% shows environment matters, so a purely genetic account is incomplete; the diathesis-stress model is more credible.
  • Confound in twin studies: MZ twins are often treated more similarly and share a more identical environment than DZ twins, so higher MZ concordance may partly reflect shared experiences, not just shared genes.
  • The polygenic view fits modern genome-wide research but makes prediction difficult, as no single gene reliably identifies who will develop the disorder.

Evaluating the dopamine and cognitive explanations

  • Strength of dopamine hypothesis: strong pharmacological support — drugs that lower dopamine reduce symptoms and drugs that raise it induce them, showing a causal link.
  • Weakness — cause or effect: high dopamine activity may be a result of schizophrenia rather than its cause, and newer atypical drugs act on serotonin too, suggesting dopamine is not the whole story.
  • Strength of cognitive explanation: it explains the actual content of symptoms (why someone hears a voice or feels persecuted) which biology alone does not, and it directly informs CBT for schizophrenia.
  • The explanations are complementary, not competing: the cognitive account describes the psychological mechanism, while the dopamine account may describe its neural substrate — a biopsychosocial view is strongest.

Research methods

  • Genetic evidence comes largely from correlational twin, family and adoption studies, so it cannot establish causation and is vulnerable to shared-environment confounds.
  • The dopamine hypothesis draws on drug studies and post-mortem/brain-scan data; brain scans of living patients are correlational, again limiting causal claims.
  • Cognitive biases like JTC are tested with controlled lab tasks (e.g. the beads task), giving good control but sometimes low ecological validity relative to real delusional thinking.
  • Much biological research uses animal models for dopamine pathways, raising questions about generalising to complex human cognition.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Nature vs nurture: genetics supply the diathesis (nature) but the diathesis-stress model shows environmental stress (nurture) is needed to trigger onset, making this a textbook interactionist debate.
  • Reductionism vs holism: the dopamine hypothesis is biologically reductionist, reducing a complex disorder to one neurotransmitter, whereas the diathesis-stress and cognitive accounts are more holistic.
  • Determinism: the biological explanations are strongly deterministic (genes and biochemistry cause the disorder), which has implications for responsibility and for optimism about recovery.
  • Usefulness: the dopamine hypothesis is highly useful because it directly justifies antipsychotic drug treatment, while the cognitive explanation is useful because it underpins CBT.

Schizophrenia: treatment & management

Key terms

  • An antipsychotic is a drug used to reduce the symptoms of psychosis, primarily by acting on dopamine (and, for newer drugs, serotonin) neurotransmission.
  • A typical (first-generation) antipsychotic is an older drug (e.g. chlorpromazine, haloperidol) that works mainly by blocking dopamine receptors.
  • An atypical (second-generation) antipsychotic is a newer drug (e.g. clozapine, risperidone) that blocks dopamine but also acts on serotonin receptors and tends to have fewer movement side-effects.
  • ECT (electroconvulsive therapy) is a treatment in which a controlled electric current is passed through the brain under anaesthetic to induce a brief seizure; it is now rarely used for schizophrenia and reserved for severe, treatment-resistant or catatonic cases.

Typical antipsychotics

  • Typical antipsychotics work as dopamine antagonists: they bind to and block D2 dopamine receptors, reducing dopamine activity and thereby dampening positive symptoms like hallucinations and delusions.
  • They are effective for positive symptoms but do little for negative symptoms, consistent with the revised dopamine hypothesis (negative symptoms involve too LITTLE prefrontal dopamine).
  • Because they block dopamine indiscriminately across the brain, they cause extrapyramidal side-effects: Parkinsonian tremors, stiffness, and restlessness.
  • A serious long-term risk is tardive dyskinesia — involuntary repetitive movements of the face and mouth that can become permanent, which is a major reason patients stop taking them (poor adherence).

Atypical antipsychotics

  • Atypical antipsychotics also block dopamine receptors but bind more loosely and additionally act on serotonin receptors, which is thought to give some benefit for negative and cognitive symptoms as well as positive ones.
  • They generally cause fewer movement (extrapyramidal) side-effects, improving tolerability and adherence.
  • However, they carry their own risks: clozapine can cause agranulocytosis (a dangerous drop in white blood cells) requiring regular blood tests, and many cause significant weight gain and metabolic problems (diabetes risk).
  • Clozapine is often reserved for treatment-resistant cases because, despite its blood risks, it can help patients who do not respond to other drugs.

CBT for schizophrenia and ECT

  • CBT for schizophrenia (CBTp) does not aim to cure the disorder but to help patients cope with and reappraise their symptoms, reducing distress and improving functioning.
  • The therapist builds a trusting relationship, then gently helps the patient explore the evidence for delusional beliefs and consider alternative, less threatening interpretations of their experiences (e.g. that a voice may be their own thought).
  • Coping strategies (e.g. distraction, reality-testing) are taught so the person can manage residual symptoms; CBT is used alongside, not instead of, medication.
  • ECT is now a last-resort management option, used mainly for severe catatonia or when drugs and therapy have failed; its mechanism in schizophrenia is not well understood and it carries memory-loss side-effects.
⭐ Key study — Sensky et al. (2000) — CBT for persistent symptoms in schizophrenia resistant to medication

Aim: To test whether cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) is effective for patients with schizophrenia who have persistent positive and negative symptoms despite taking antipsychotic medication, and to check whether any benefit is due specifically to CBT rather than to the general attention and support a therapist provides.

Method: A randomised controlled trial (RCT). The sample was 90 patients with schizophrenia whose symptoms had not responded adequately to medication. Participants were randomly allocated to one of two conditions: (1) about 19 sessions of CBT over roughly 9 months plus their usual treatment, or (2) a 'befriending' control condition (equal therapist contact spent on neutral supportive conversation about hobbies and everyday topics) plus usual treatment. Befriending controls for the non-specific effects of having a warm, attentive therapist. Symptoms (positive, negative and depressive) were measured before treatment, at the end of treatment, and again at a 9-month follow-up.

Results: Both CBT and befriending produced significant reductions in positive, negative and depressive symptoms by the end of treatment, showing that therapist attention alone had some effect. Crucially, at the 9-month follow-up the CBT group CONTINUED to improve and maintained their gains, whereas the befriending group's improvements were not sustained (check book for exact scores/effect sizes).

Conclusion: CBT is an effective adjunct to medication for treatment-resistant schizophrenia, producing durable improvements that outlast the benefits of general support. The use of a befriending control shows the lasting effect is specific to CBT's active ingredients (reappraising symptoms), not merely to attention, strengthening the case for offering CBT alongside drugs.

Schizophrenia: treatment & management — evaluation & recall

Evaluating drug treatments

  • Strength — effectiveness and practicality: antipsychotics are effective at reducing positive symptoms relatively quickly, are cheap and easy to administer at scale, and enable many patients to live in the community rather than in hospital.
  • Weakness — side-effects and adherence: distressing side-effects (especially tardive dyskinesia with typicals, weight gain/agranulocytosis with atypicals) lead many patients to stop taking their medication, causing relapse.
  • Weakness — treats symptoms not cause: drugs manage symptoms but do not cure the disorder, and they do little for negative symptoms (typicals especially), so relapse is common when medication stops.
  • Ethical issue: because psychotic patients may lack insight, questions arise about informed consent and about drugs being used as a 'chemical straitjacket' for control rather than benefit.

Evaluating CBT and comparing approaches

  • Strength of CBT (from Sensky): its benefits are durable and outlast simple befriending, and it targets the distress and content of symptoms that drugs ignore, improving quality of life.
  • Weakness of CBT: it requires a degree of insight and engagement, so it is less suitable during acute psychotic episodes and works best once medication has stabilised the patient.
  • The best outcomes usually come from combining biological (drug) and psychological (CBT) treatments — drugs reduce florid symptoms so the patient can engage with therapy, and CBT reduces relapse and distress.
  • ECT is hard to justify for schizophrenia given weak evidence and side-effects, and is now used only in tightly restricted circumstances.

Research methods

  • Sensky used an RCT with random allocation and an active control (befriending), giving high internal validity and controlling for placebo/attention effects — a methodological strength.
  • The 9-month follow-up is a key strength because it revealed that the two treatments differed in DURABILITY, which a short study would have missed.
  • Drug efficacy is usually established through double-blind placebo-controlled trials, which reduce experimenter and participant bias, though truly blinding a drug with obvious side-effects is difficult.
  • Sample sizes in such trials can be modest and drop-out is common in this population, which can threaten the reliability and generalisability of findings.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Reductionism vs holism: drug treatment is biologically reductionist (fixing neurotransmitters), whereas combining drugs with CBT is a more holistic biopsychosocial approach.
  • Free will vs determinism: drugs imply a determinist model (chemistry controls the disorder), while CBT restores some agency by giving the patient tools to reinterpret and cope with symptoms.
  • Ethics and social control: compulsory medication and ECT raise the debate about whether treatment serves the patient or society's desire to manage disturbing behaviour, and about consent when insight is impaired.
  • Usefulness of research: Sensky's RCT is directly useful — its evidence supports clinical guidelines recommending CBT alongside medication.

Mood disorders: diagnosis

Key terms

  • A mood (affective) disorder is a mental disorder in which a disturbance of mood is the central feature, ranging from severe low mood (depression) to abnormally elevated mood (mania).
  • Unipolar depression means the person experiences only depressive episodes (one 'pole' of mood), with no history of mania.
  • Bipolar disorder means the person experiences both depressive lows AND manic (or hypomanic) highs — the mood swings between two poles.
  • A manic episode is a distinct period of abnormally elevated, expansive or irritable mood plus increased energy and activity, lasting at least a week and causing serious impairment.

ICD-11 unipolar depression criteria

  • ICD-11 requires a cluster of symptoms present most of the day, nearly every day, for at least two weeks, marking a change from the person's usual functioning.
  • There must be affective symptoms (persistent depressed mood and/or markedly reduced interest or pleasure — anhedonia), which are core to the diagnosis.
  • There must also be cognitive-behavioural symptoms such as reduced concentration, feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt, hopelessness about the future, and recurrent thoughts of death or suicide.
  • Neurovegetative (physical) symptoms are also required, such as disturbed sleep (insomnia or oversleeping), changes in appetite/weight, fatigue/loss of energy, and psychomotor agitation or slowing; the episode must not be better explained by bereavement or substance use.

Bipolar disorder: episodes and subtypes

  • A manic episode involves elevated or irritable mood plus symptoms such as inflated self-esteem/grandiosity, reduced need for sleep, pressured (rapid, hard-to-interrupt) speech, racing thoughts, distractibility, and reckless behaviour (e.g. overspending); it lasts at least a week or requires hospitalisation.
  • Hypomania is a milder, shorter form of mania (at least several days) with similar symptoms but WITHOUT severe impairment or psychosis, so the person can still function.
  • A depressive episode in bipolar looks like unipolar depression (low mood, anhedonia, the symptoms above) but occurs in someone who also has a history of mania/hypomania.
  • Bipolar I is diagnosed if the person has had at least one full manic episode (depressive episodes usually occur but are not strictly required); Bipolar II is diagnosed when the person has had at least one hypomanic episode AND at least one major depressive episode, but never a full manic episode.

Measuring depression: the Beck Depression Inventory (BDI)

  • The BDI is a widely used self-report questionnaire designed to measure the SEVERITY of depression, not to diagnose it on its own.
  • It contains 21 items, each describing a symptom (e.g. sadness, pessimism, guilt, sleep, appetite) with a set of statements scored 0-3 according to how severe that symptom is for the respondent.
  • Item scores are summed to give a total (higher = more severe); banded score ranges indicate minimal, mild, moderate or severe depression, allowing clinicians to track change over time and across treatment.
  • Because it is self-report, it is quick and gives the patient's own perspective, but it depends on honest, insightful responding and can be affected by social desirability.

Mood disorders: diagnosis — evaluation & recall

Evaluating diagnosis and classification

  • Strength — clear thresholds: ICD-11's specified duration (2 weeks for depression, 1 week for mania) and symptom lists improve inter-rater reliability, so clinicians are more likely to agree on a diagnosis.
  • Weakness — where to draw the line: distinguishing clinical depression from normal, understandable sadness (e.g. after loss) is difficult, risking over-diagnosis and the medicalisation of ordinary distress.
  • Strength of the unipolar/bipolar distinction: it matters clinically because they need different treatments — giving antidepressants alone to a bipolar patient can trigger a manic episode, so accurate classification prevents harm.
  • Weakness — under-recognition of bipolar: because patients usually seek help when depressed (not when manic/euphoric), bipolar is often misdiagnosed as unipolar depression, delaying correct treatment for years.

Research methods

  • Diagnosis relies heavily on clinical interview and self-report measures like the BDI, which are subjective and vulnerable to social desirability bias and to the patient's level of insight.
  • The BDI has been extensively tested for reliability (e.g. test-retest and internal consistency) and validity, making it a psychometrically strong tool — but a high score alone is not a diagnosis.
  • Self-report scales allow standardised, quantitative comparison across patients and over time, aiding evaluation of treatment, but they measure severity rather than cause.
  • Cultural validity is a concern: symptom expression varies across cultures (e.g. depression expressed more physically in some cultures), so tools developed in one culture may not transfer well.

  • Reductionism vs holism: reducing mood to a diagnostic checklist and a BDI score is efficient but may miss the holistic, personal meaning of a patient's distress.
  • Nomothetic vs idiographic: standardised criteria and the BDI are nomothetic (comparing people to norms), whereas understanding an individual's specific triggers is idiographic.

  • Usefulness: reliable diagnosis is useful because it guides treatment choice (crucially, the unipolar/bipolar distinction prevents harmful mis-prescribing).

Mood disorders: explanations of depression

Key terms

  • The monoamine hypothesis proposes that depression is caused by low levels or activity of monoamine neurotransmitters, chiefly serotonin and noradrenaline, in the brain.
  • A schema is a mental framework of beliefs and expectations built from experience; in Beck's theory, depressed people hold negative self-schemas that bias how they interpret events.
  • A cognitive distortion is a systematic error in thinking (e.g. overgeneralisation, catastrophising) that maintains a negative, unrealistic view of oneself and the world.
  • Attributional style is the habitual way a person explains the causes of events; a depressive style attributes bad events to internal, stable and global causes ('it's my fault, it always happens, it affects everything').

Biological explanation

  • The monoamine (biochemical) explanation says depression results from too little serotonin and/or noradrenaline activity at the synapse, disrupting mood regulation.
  • The strongest support is pharmacological: antidepressants that raise serotonin/noradrenaline levels relieve depression, whereas drugs that deplete them can induce low mood.
  • Genetic evidence indicates depression runs in families and shows higher concordance in MZ than DZ twins, suggesting an inherited vulnerability, again best framed as diathesis-stress.
  • Genes are thought to influence depression partly by affecting the serotonin system (e.g. serotonin transporter and receptor genes), linking the genetic and biochemical accounts together.

Beck's cognitive theory

  • Beck argued that depression is maintained by faulty thinking, organised around the NEGATIVE COGNITIVE TRIAD: automatic negative thoughts about (1) the self ('I am worthless'), (2) the world/experience ('everything is against me'), and (3) the future ('things will never improve').
  • These thoughts stem from negative self-schemas, often formed in childhood through adverse experiences, which act as templates that bias interpretation of new events toward the negative.
  • Schemas produce cognitive distortions such as overgeneralisation (one failure means total failure), selective abstraction (focusing only on negatives), and catastrophising (assuming the worst outcome).
  • The triad, schemas and distortions form a self-reinforcing cycle: negative interpretation lowers mood, which further activates negative schemas — this cycle is exactly what CBT/cognitive restructuring later targets.

Learned helplessness and attributional style (Seligman)

  • Seligman's learned helplessness began with animal studies: dogs exposed to inescapable shocks later failed to escape even when escape became possible, having 'learned' that their actions were futile.
  • Applied to humans, repeated experience of uncontrollable negative events can teach people that they are helpless, producing the passivity and hopelessness seen in depression.
  • The theory was reformulated to include attributional style: depression is more likely when a person explains bad events using internal ('my fault'), stable ('it will last') and global ('it affects everything') attributions.
  • This explains individual differences — two people facing the same setback respond differently depending on their explanatory style, and a pessimistic style predicts vulnerability to depression.
⭐ Key study — Oruč et al. (1997) — Association analysis of the 5-HT2C receptor and 5-HT transporter genes in bipolar disorder

Aim: To investigate whether specific variations (polymorphisms) in two serotonin-related genes — the 5-HT2C serotonin receptor gene and the serotonin transporter (5-HTT) gene — are associated with bipolar affective disorder, testing the biological/genetic explanation that abnormalities in the serotonin system contribute to mood disorders.

Method: A genetic association (case-control) study. The sample was 42 patients diagnosed with bipolar disorder type I and 40 healthy control participants, all drawn from a Croatian population. DNA was analysed to identify each participant's polymorphisms in the 5-HT2C receptor gene and the 5-HTT gene. The frequency of the different gene variants was then compared between the bipolar patients and the controls to see if particular variants were more common in the patient group. Analyses were also carried out separately by sex.

Results: In the sample as a whole, there were NO statistically significant associations between either gene polymorphism and bipolar disorder. However, when patients were analysed separately by gender, there were trends toward an association in FEMALE patients for both genes (reported as p = 0.051 for the 5-HT2C receptor and p = 0.049 for the serotonin transporter — check book), i.e. right at the borderline of significance.

Conclusion: The results give at most weak, inconclusive support for a role of these serotonin genes in bipolar disorder, and only a possible sex-specific effect in females. This shows mood disorders are unlikely to be caused by any single gene (they are polygenic) and illustrates how difficult it is to pin down genetic contributions — supporting a diathesis-stress rather than a purely genetic account.

Mood disorders: explanations of depression — evaluation & recall

Evaluating the biological explanation

  • Strength — pharmacological support: the effectiveness of antidepressants that raise serotonin/noradrenaline is strong indirect evidence for the monoamine hypothesis.
  • Weakness — the hypothesis is oversimplified: antidepressants raise neurotransmitter levels within hours but mood only improves after weeks, so low monoamines cannot be the whole, immediate cause.
  • Weakness — inconclusive genetics (Oruč): studies like Oruč et al. fail to find clear single-gene associations, showing depression/bipolar are polygenic and not straightforwardly 'in the genes'.
  • Cause or effect: even where biochemical differences exist, they may be a consequence of being depressed (e.g. inactivity, stress) rather than its cause, so correlation is not causation.

Evaluating the cognitive explanations

  • Strength of Beck's theory: it is supported by evidence that depressed people show more negative automatic thoughts and distortions, and it has excellent practical value because it directly produced effective CBT.
  • Weakness — direction of causality: negative thinking may be a SYMPTOM of depression rather than its cause, so it is unclear whether faulty cognition triggers or merely accompanies the disorder.
  • Strength of learned helplessness: it explains individual differences via attributional style and is backed by experimental (if partly animal-based) evidence.
  • Comparison: biological and cognitive accounts are complementary — a biological vulnerability may lower mood, and a negative cognitive/attributional style then maintains and deepens it (a diathesis-stress interaction).

Research methods

  • Oruč used a case-control genetic design, which is efficient but cannot establish causation and can produce false positives/negatives, especially with small samples (n=82) and multiple sub-group comparisons.
  • Splitting the sample by sex after the main analysis is a form of post-hoc sub-group analysis, so the borderline female results should be treated cautiously as they may reflect chance.
  • Cognitive theories are tested using questionnaires and attributional-style measures, which are quick and quantifiable but rely on self-report and can be retrospective.
  • Seligman's foundational evidence used animal experiments, raising both generalisation concerns and ethical issues about inescapable-shock procedures.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Nature vs nurture: the monoamine/genetic view (nature) contrasts with cognitive and learned-helplessness accounts (nurture/experience), with diathesis-stress integrating both.
  • Reductionism vs holism: the monoamine hypothesis is highly reductionist (mood = neurotransmitter levels), whereas cognitive theories offer a more holistic account of thinking patterns and life experience.
  • Determinism vs free will: biological and learned-helplessness accounts are deterministic, but Beck's model allows some free will because thoughts can be identified and deliberately changed (basis of therapy).
  • Usefulness: both explanations are useful — the monoamine hypothesis justifies antidepressants and Beck's theory justifies CBT, so the debate has direct treatment implications.

Mood disorders: treatment

Key terms

  • An antidepressant is a drug used to relieve depression, generally by increasing the availability or activity of monoamine neurotransmitters (serotonin and/or noradrenaline).
  • Reuptake is the process by which a neuron reabsorbs neurotransmitter from the synapse after transmission; blocking reuptake leaves more neurotransmitter active in the synapse.
  • Cognitive restructuring (Beck) is a therapeutic process of identifying, challenging and replacing negative automatic thoughts and distortions with more realistic ones.
  • REBT (Rational Emotive Behaviour Therapy, Ellis) is a form of cognitive therapy that challenges the irrational BELIEFS a person holds about events, using the ABCDE framework.

Antidepressant drugs and their mechanisms

  • Tricyclics (e.g. imipramine) block the reuptake of both serotonin and noradrenaline, increasing their levels in the synapse; they are effective but have many side-effects (drowsiness, dry mouth, and dangerous toxicity in overdose).
  • MAOIs (monoamine oxidase inhibitors) block the enzyme monoamine oxidase, which normally breaks down monoamines, so more neurotransmitter remains available; they are effective but risky because combining them with certain foods (e.g. aged cheese, containing tyramine) can cause a dangerous rise in blood pressure.
  • SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, e.g. fluoxetine/Prozac) selectively block the reuptake of serotonin only, raising synaptic serotonin; they are now first-line because they have fewer, milder side-effects and are safer in overdose.
  • A key issue for all antidepressants is the delay of several weeks before mood improves, plus side-effects, discontinuation effects and debate over how much of the benefit exceeds placebo.

Beck's cognitive restructuring

  • Cognitive restructuring aims to break the depressive cycle by changing the faulty thinking (the negative triad, schemas and distortions) identified in Beck's theory.
  • The therapist first helps the client identify their negative automatic thoughts, often using a thought diary to record situations, thoughts and feelings.
  • The client is treated as a 'scientist' who tests these thoughts against evidence ('collaborative empiricism'), and may be set behavioural 'homework' (hypothesis-testing) to gather real-world evidence that disconfirms negative beliefs.
  • Over time, unrealistic negative thoughts are replaced with more balanced, realistic ones, which lifts mood and weakens the underlying negative schemas.

Ellis's REBT and the ABCDE model

  • Ellis argued that emotional disturbance is caused not by events themselves but by the IRRATIONAL BELIEFS we hold about them, so therapy targets those beliefs directly.
  • The ABC part describes the problem: A = Activating event (something happens), B = Belief about it (often irrational, e.g. 'I must be liked by everyone'), C = Consequence (the resulting emotion/behaviour, e.g. depression).
  • The DE part is the therapeutic addition: D = Disputing the irrational belief (the therapist vigorously challenges it, e.g. 'Where is the evidence you MUST be liked by everyone?'), leading to E = Effect (a new, rational belief and healthier emotional consequence).
  • REBT is more confrontational than Beck's approach and focuses on 'musturbatory' thinking (rigid demands like 'must', 'should', 'ought'), aiming to make beliefs flexible and realistic.

Mood disorders: treatment — evaluation & recall

Evaluating drug treatments

  • Strength — effective and convenient: antidepressants (especially SSRIs) are effective for many patients, easy to take, cheap and require little effort from the patient, making them scalable.
  • Weakness — side-effects and delay: all antidepressants have side-effects and take weeks to work, and the tyramine/food interaction makes MAOIs particularly hazardous, reducing adherence.
  • Weakness — treats symptoms, not causes: drugs address biochemistry but not the negative thinking or life circumstances behind depression, so relapse is common once the drug stops.
  • Debate over effectiveness vs placebo: some analyses suggest much of antidepressants' benefit (especially in mild depression) overlaps with placebo, questioning how large the true drug effect is.

Evaluating cognitive therapies and comparisons

  • Strength of CBT/REBT: by tackling the underlying faulty cognitions, they can produce longer-lasting benefits and lower relapse than drugs, and they empower the patient with transferable coping skills.
  • Weakness — effort and suitability: they require motivation, verbal ability and effort, so they are less suitable for severely depressed patients who lack energy or insight; they also take time and a skilled therapist.
  • Beck vs Ellis: Beck's collaborative, evidence-testing style is gentler and often better tolerated, whereas Ellis's confrontational disputing can be effective but may feel harsh to a vulnerable client.
  • Combined approach: many patients do best with drugs to lift mood enough to engage, plus CBT/REBT to change thinking and reduce relapse — a biopsychosocial best-of-both strategy.

Research methods

  • Drug effectiveness is tested in double-blind placebo-controlled RCTs, which reduce bias, though side-effects can 'break' the blind by revealing who received the active drug.
  • Therapy outcome studies compare CBT/REBT against control or drug conditions, often using standardised measures like the BDI to quantify change objectively.
  • Comparing therapy to drugs is complicated because therapy cannot easily be double-blinded and drop-out rates differ between conditions, affecting internal validity.
  • Long follow-ups are important (as in Sensky for schizophrenia) because a treatment that works short-term may differ from one that prevents relapse.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Reductionism vs holism: drug treatment is biologically reductionist, while CBT/REBT are more holistic, addressing thoughts, beliefs and behaviour.
  • Free will vs determinism: drugs imply mood is determined by chemistry, whereas cognitive therapies assume free will — that people can choose to challenge and change their thinking.
  • Ethics: drugs risk over-medicalising distress and creating dependence, while confrontational REBT raises questions about protecting a vulnerable client from harm during disputing.
  • Usefulness: treatment research is directly useful for clinical guidelines, which typically recommend CBT and/or SSRIs for depression depending on severity.

Impulse control disorders: diagnosis

Key terms

  • An impulse control disorder is a condition characterised by repeated failure to resist an impulse, drive or urge to perform an act that is harmful to the person or others.
  • The defining cycle for these disorders is a build-up of tension or arousal before the act, followed by pleasure, gratification or relief during or after it — this tension-then-relief pattern is their common feature.
  • Kleptomania is the recurrent failure to resist urges to steal objects that are NOT needed for personal use or their monetary value (the theft is about the urge, not the item).
  • Pyromania is the recurrent, deliberate and purposeful fire-setting driven by an urge and fascination with fire, not done for profit, revenge, or as an expression of another disorder.

ICD-11: kleptomania

  • ICD-11 defines kleptomania by a recurrent inability to resist impulses to steal, where the stolen items are not acquired for personal use or monetary gain — the person often gives them away, hoards or discards them.
  • There is a rising sense of TENSION or arousal immediately before committing the theft.
  • There is a feeling of PLEASURE, gratification or RELIEF at the time of, or immediately after, the theft — this reinforces the behaviour.
  • The stealing is not better explained by another condition (e.g. conduct disorder, mania, or antisocial personality) and is not done out of anger, revenge, or in response to a delusion/hallucination; the person typically feels guilt or shame afterwards.

ICD-11: pyromania

  • Pyromania is defined by a recurrent failure to resist impulses to set fires, resulting in multiple acts of, or attempts at, fire-setting.
  • As with kleptomania, there is a build-up of tension or affective arousal before the act, and pleasure, gratification or relief when setting fires or when watching or participating in their aftermath.
  • The person shows a persistent fascination with, interest in, or attraction to fire and related stimuli (e.g. watching fires, the equipment involved).
  • The fire-setting is NOT done for financial gain, to conceal a crime, to express a political/ideological view, for revenge, or as a result of another mental disorder or intoxication — this exclusion is essential to distinguish pyromania from ordinary arson.

ICD-11: gambling disorder and the K-SAS

  • Gambling disorder is a pattern of persistent or recurrent gambling behaviour (online or offline) that the person cannot control and continues despite negative consequences.
  • Its features include impaired control over gambling (frequency, duration, amount), giving gambling increasing priority over other interests and daily activities, and continuing or escalating gambling despite harm to relationships, work or finances.
  • The pattern must be evident over a sustained period (usually at least 12 months, though this can be shortened if all features are present and severe) and causes significant distress or impairment.
  • The Kleptomania Symptom Assessment Scale (K-SAS) is an 11-item self-report questionnaire measuring the severity of kleptomania symptoms — such as the strength and frequency of urges, the associated tension, and the thoughts and behaviours — used to assess severity and to monitor response to treatment.

Impulse control disorders: diagnosis — evaluation & recall

Evaluating diagnosis of impulse control disorders

  • Strength — the shared tension-relief cycle: identifying a common mechanism across kleptomania, pyromania and gambling disorder gives the category conceptual coherence and helps explain how the behaviours are negatively reinforced (relief) and positively reinforced (pleasure).
  • Strength — clear exclusion criteria: requiring that the behaviour is NOT for gain, revenge or due to another disorder improves validity by separating, e.g., pyromania from ordinary arson and kleptomania from ordinary shoplifting.
  • Weakness — reliability and honesty of report: because these behaviours are illegal or shameful, patients may under-report or conceal them, and diagnosis relies heavily on self-report (including the K-SAS), threatening reliability.
  • Weakness — rarity and overlap: kleptomania and pyromania are rare and share features with mood, addictive and personality disorders, making differential diagnosis difficult and raising the question of whether they are truly distinct disorders.

Research methods

  • Diagnosis and severity assessment rely on structured clinical interviews and self-report scales like the K-SAS, which are quick and quantifiable but vulnerable to social desirability and concealment given the stigmatised, often criminal nature of the behaviour.
  • The K-SAS gives a standardised, numerical severity score, allowing clinicians to compare patients and track change during treatment — a strength for reliability and for evaluating interventions.
  • Because these disorders are rare, research often uses small or clinical samples, limiting generalisability and statistical power.
  • Much evidence is correlational or based on case studies, so causal explanations of impulse control disorders remain tentative.

Going deeper / exam use

  • For a 'describe the diagnosis' answer, structure it disorder-by-disorder (kleptomania, pyromania, gambling) and always include the shared tension-before / relief-after cycle plus the key exclusion criteria.
  • Emphasise the exclusions — many marks are lost by describing ordinary theft/arson; the point is that the act is driven by an urge, not by gain or revenge.
  • Use the K-SAS as your named measurement tool, explaining it assesses severity (not just presence) and is used to monitor treatment progress.
  • These disorders link well to the behaviourist idea of reinforcement (relief negatively reinforces, pleasure positively reinforces), which is useful for evaluation and later explanation topics.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Free will vs determinism: the label 'impulse control disorder' implies the behaviour is not fully under voluntary control (determinism), which has major implications for legal and moral responsibility.
  • Idiographic vs nomothetic: standardised ICD-11 criteria and the K-SAS are nomothetic, but understanding an individual's specific triggers and the meaning of their urges is idiographic.
  • Usefulness of psychological research: reliable diagnosis and measures like the K-SAS are useful for planning and evaluating treatment, but the stigma attached limits how readily people seek help.
  • Reductionism vs holism: reducing these disorders to a tension-relief cycle is neat and explanatory, but a holistic view also considers the person's social, emotional and developmental context.

Impulse control disorders: explanations

What impulse control disorders are

  • Impulse control disorders (ICDs) are conditions in which a person repeatedly fails to resist an urge or drive to perform an act that is harmful to themselves or others. The person typically feels rising tension or arousal before the act, pleasure or relief during it, and sometimes guilt afterwards.
  • In ICD-11 the main examples include gambling disorder, kleptomania (stealing without financial need), pyromania (fire-setting), and intermittent explosive disorder. The syllabus focuses heavily on gambling as the exemplar because it has the richest research base.
  • The three levels of explanation you must be able to describe and evaluate are biological, behavioural, and cognitive. A strong answer treats these as complementary rather than mutually exclusive, because each explains a different piece of the puzzle (why the brain finds it rewarding, how the behaviour is learned and maintained, and why faulty thinking keeps the person gambling).

Biological explanation: dopamine and the reward system

  • The mesolimbic dopamine pathway (from the ventral tegmental area to the nucleus accumbens) is the brain's reward circuit. It normally releases dopamine when we do survival-relevant things like eating, producing a feeling of pleasure that reinforces the behaviour.
  • In gambling disorder this reward system is thought to be dysregulated. Winning money (and even the anticipation of a possible win) triggers dopamine release, which is experienced as a 'high'. This creates a powerful drive to repeat the behaviour to chase that neurochemical reward.
  • Over time tolerance may develop: the gambler needs to bet larger amounts or take bigger risks to achieve the same dopamine-driven excitement, mirroring the pattern seen in substance addictions. This is why gambling disorder is now classed alongside addictive behaviours rather than as a pure obsessive-compulsive condition.
  • Evidence: drugs that increase dopamine (e.g. dopamine agonists used to treat Parkinson's disease) have been observed to trigger sudden compulsive gambling in some patients, strongly implicating dopamine. Individual differences in dopamine receptor genes may explain why some people are more vulnerable.

Behavioural explanation: reinforcement and the near-miss

  • Operant conditioning explains gambling through positive reinforcement: a win delivers money and excitement (a reward), which increases the probability of gambling again. The behaviour is 'stamped in' by its consequences.
  • Crucially, wins occur on a variable (partial) reinforcement schedule: the reward is unpredictable in timing and size. Variable-ratio schedules produce very high, persistent response rates that are extremely resistant to extinction, because the gambler can never be sure the next bet will not be the winner. This is exactly how slot machines are engineered.
  • The near-miss effect: outcomes that fall just short of a win (e.g. two jackpot symbols plus a third one position off) are experienced almost like wins. They increase arousal and encourage continued play even though objectively they are losses. The gambler misinterprets 'nearly winning' as evidence that a real win is imminent.
  • Together these mean losses do not simply extinguish gambling; the intermittent wins and frequent near-misses keep the behaviour going far longer than a purely rational cost-benefit analysis would predict.

Cognitive explanation: feeling-state theory and distortions

  • Miller's feeling-state theory proposes that an intense positive feeling (e.g. the euphoria of a big early win) becomes 'locked' to the specific behaviour and situation that produced it, forming a feeling-state. Later, cues linked to that state trigger a compulsive drive to gamble in order to re-experience the feeling, even against the person's better judgement.
  • Cognitive distortions are faulty, irrational beliefs about gambling. The illusion of control is the belief that one's own actions (choosing numbers, blowing on dice, using a 'lucky' machine) can influence what is actually a random outcome.
  • The gambler's fallacy is the mistaken belief that past random events change the probability of future ones — e.g. 'red has come up five times so black is now due'. In truly random events each outcome is independent, so this reasoning is false but very persuasive.
  • Other distortions include selective recall (vividly remembering wins but forgetting or minimising losses) and superstitious beliefs. These distortions maintain gambling by making the gambler overestimate their chances of winning.

Key terms

  • Impulse control disorder: repeated failure to resist an urge to perform a self- or other-harming act, with pre-act tension and act-related relief.
  • Mesolimbic dopamine pathway: brain reward circuit whose dopamine release reinforces pleasurable behaviour.
  • Variable (partial) reinforcement schedule: unpredictable reward pattern producing highly persistent, extinction-resistant behaviour.
  • Near-miss: a losing outcome close to a win that is misread as encouraging and increases arousal.
  • Feeling-state (Miller): a positive emotion fused to a specific behaviour that later triggers a compulsion to repeat it.
  • Illusion of control: false belief that one can influence random outcomes.
  • Gambler's fallacy: false belief that past outcomes alter the odds of future independent random events.

Impulse control disorders: explanations — evaluation & recall

Evaluating the biological explanation

  • Strength: it is supported by strong biological evidence, such as dopamine agonist medication triggering compulsive gambling in Parkinson's patients, and by brain-imaging showing reward-system activation during gambling. This gives the explanation scientific credibility.
  • Strength: it usefully accounts for why gambling disorder is so hard to stop and why it resembles substance addiction, which justifies biological treatments such as opioid antagonists (Day 17).
  • Weakness: it is reductionist — reducing a complex behaviour shaped by upbringing, advertising, and thinking style to neurochemistry alone. It cannot easily explain why most people who gamble never become addicted despite having the same dopamine systems.
  • Weakness: much evidence is correlational. Differences in dopamine activity between gamblers and non-gamblers do not prove dopamine causes the disorder; heavy gambling might itself change the reward system (a cause-effect direction problem).

Evaluating the behavioural and cognitive explanations

  • Behavioural strength: variable reinforcement and the near-miss effect are supported by controlled experimental and observational work and have real-world validity — they explain the deliberate design of slot machines and online betting.
  • Behavioural weakness: it struggles to explain why some people persist through enormous losses while others quit; a purely learning-based account underplays individual differences in cognition and biology.
  • Cognitive strength: distortions can be directly measured (e.g. via the 'thinking-aloud' technique, where gamblers verbalise their thoughts while playing and reveal many irrational statements) and are the direct target of successful cognitive treatments.
  • Cognitive weakness: it is hard to establish whether distortions cause problem gambling or are a by-product of it, and feeling-state theory is difficult to test scientifically because feeling-states are internal and inferred.

Research methods

  • The 'thinking-aloud' method is a key technique here: participants say every thought out loud while gambling, giving rich qualitative data on distortions. It has high ecological validity but is open to observer bias in coding and may itself change behaviour (reactivity).
  • Much biological evidence comes from correlational and case-study designs (e.g. Parkinson's patients), which cannot establish cause and effect and may not generalise.
  • Near-miss and reinforcement effects are often studied experimentally in laboratories using simulated slot machines, giving good control but reduced realism because participants gamble with limited or no real money.

Going deeper / exam use

  • The best exam answers explicitly link the three explanations: biology explains the vulnerable reward system, behaviourism explains how the environment trains and maintains the habit, and cognition explains the beliefs that rationalise continuing. This 'diathesis-stress'-style integration scores highly on evaluation marks.
  • Always connect explanation to treatment (Day 17): the biological account justifies naltrexone, the behavioural account justifies imaginal desensitisation and covert sensitisation, and the cognitive account justifies CBT that challenges distortions.
  • A useful applied example: online and slot-machine gambling deliberately exploits every mechanism at once (fast variable reinforcement, engineered near-misses, flashing lights that boost dopamine, and 'lucky' features that feed the illusion of control).
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Nature vs nurture: biological (dopamine, genes) versus behavioural/cognitive (learning, distortions) accounts; the strongest position is an interactionist one where an inherited vulnerable reward system is triggered by learning and faulty thinking.
  • Reductionism vs holism: the dopamine explanation is reductionist (behaviour = neurochemistry), whereas combining all three levels gives a more holistic and complete account.
  • Determinism vs free will: biological and behavioural explanations imply the gambler is driven by forces beyond their control, raising questions about personal responsibility for gambling debts and harm.
  • Application to everyday life: understanding near-misses and variable reinforcement can justify real-world policy such as regulating slot-machine design and gambling advertising.

Impulse control disorders: treatment

Overview of treatment approaches

  • Treatments for gambling disorder map onto the three explanations from Day 16. Biological treatment uses medication (opioid antagonists) to dampen the reward. Behavioural treatments (covert sensitisation and imaginal desensitisation) use learning principles to weaken the urge. Cognitive-behavioural therapy challenges distortions and builds coping skills.
  • Because gambling has biological, learned, and cognitive components, treatment is often most effective when approaches are combined — for example medication to reduce urges plus therapy to address thinking and behaviour.

Biological treatment: opioid antagonists

  • Naltrexone and nalmefene are opioid (opiate) antagonists: they block opioid receptors in the brain. Because the opioid system modulates dopamine release in the reward pathway, blocking it reduces the pleasurable 'high' and the excitement of gambling and betting.
  • By reducing the reward and the intensity of gambling urges, the medication makes it easier for the person to resist the impulse and stay in psychological treatment. It targets the biological mechanism directly rather than the thinking or the learned habit.
  • These drugs were originally used for alcohol and opioid dependence, which reflects the shared reward-system basis of addictive behaviours. Side effects (e.g. nausea, dizziness, liver-function concerns) mean medical monitoring is needed, and the drug treats symptoms rather than underlying causes.

Covert sensitisation (Glover, 1985)

  • Covert sensitisation is a behavioural (aversion) therapy that works through classical conditioning done entirely in imagination ('covert' = hidden/internal). The aim is to associate gambling with an unpleasant consequence so the urge is reduced.
  • The client relaxes and then vividly imagines gambling, and at the peak of the imagined scene pairs it with an aversive image (e.g. feeling intensely sick, vomiting, or the shame of a partner discovering their losses). Repeated pairing makes the previously attractive gambling cues trigger disgust or anxiety instead of excitement.
  • Glover (1985) reported using this successfully with a compulsive gambler, illustrating how imagined aversive associations can suppress the drive to gamble. Because it is imaginal, it avoids the ethical and practical problems of using real aversive stimuli (like electric shocks).

Imaginal desensitisation (Blaszczynski & Nower, 2003)

  • Imaginal desensitisation combines relaxation training with imagined exposure to gambling situations. Unlike covert sensitisation, the goal is NOT to make gambling aversive but to reduce the arousal and tension that drive the urge, so the client can encounter gambling cues without being compelled to act.
  • The client is trained in progressive muscle relaxation, then imagines a series of gambling scenarios (approaching a betting shop, being handed money) while staying relaxed. Because relaxation is incompatible with the tense, aroused state that precedes gambling (reciprocal inhibition), the trigger cues gradually lose their power to produce the compulsive urge.
  • Blaszczynski and Nower (2003) advocated this approach and reported it as effective in reducing gambling urges, framing pathological gambling partly as an impulse driven by heightened arousal that relaxation can counteract.

Key terms

  • Opioid antagonist: a drug (naltrexone, nalmefene) that blocks opioid receptors, indirectly lowering reward-related dopamine and reducing gambling urges.
  • Covert sensitisation: imagined pairing of the target behaviour with an aversive consequence to reduce its appeal (aversion therapy in imagination).
  • Imaginal desensitisation: relaxation paired with imagined gambling scenes to reduce arousal-driven urges (based on reciprocal inhibition).
  • Reciprocal inhibition: the principle that one cannot feel relaxed and aroused/anxious at the same time; relaxation displaces the urge.
  • Placebo response: improvement due to expectation of benefit rather than the active drug.
⭐ Key study — Grant, Kim, Hollander & Potenza (2008) — Predicting response to opiate antagonists and placebo in the treatment of pathological gambling

Aim: To identify which patient characteristics predict a good response to opiate (opioid) antagonist medication, and separately which predict a placebo response, in the treatment of pathological gambling — in order to help target the drug at patients most likely to benefit.

Method: Design: a secondary (pooled) analysis of data from two double-blind, placebo-controlled drug trials — one of nalmefene (16 weeks) and one of naltrexone (18 weeks). Sample: 284 people meeting DSM-IV criteria for pathological gambling drawn from these trials. Procedure: participants were randomly allocated to active opioid-antagonist medication or placebo. Gambling severity and urges were measured (e.g. using the PG-YBOCS, a gambling-adapted Yale-Brown scale). Patient characteristics — notably family history of alcoholism, age, and intensity of gambling urges — were statistically analysed as possible predictors of treatment outcome.

Results: A family history of alcoholism was the key predictor of a positive response to opiate antagonists: patients with alcoholic relatives responded better to the drug. There was also a trend for stronger baseline gambling urges to predict a better response at higher doses. For the placebo condition, younger age predicted a placebo response.

Conclusion: Opioid antagonists are especially effective for pathological gamblers with a family history of alcoholism, suggesting a shared inherited reward-system/opioid vulnerability across addictive behaviours. Knowing these predictors allows more personalised prescribing (targeting the drug where it works best) and highlights that some improvement in trials reflects placebo effects, particularly in younger patients.

Impulse control disorders: treatment — evaluation & recall

Evaluating biological treatment

  • Strength: opioid antagonists are supported by controlled, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials (as used by Grant et al.), giving high-quality, objective evidence of effectiveness that rules out expectation effects.
  • Strength: because it directly reduces urges, medication can be fast-acting and can help a person engage with psychological therapy that requires them to resist gambling.
  • Weakness: it treats the symptom (the urge) not the underlying distorted thinking or the learned habit, so relapse is likely if the drug is stopped. Side effects and the need for medical monitoring reduce compliance.
  • Weakness: Grant et al. show it does not work equally for everyone — patients without a family history of alcoholism respond less well — so it is not a universal solution.

Evaluating the behavioural therapies

  • Strength: covert sensitisation and imaginal desensitisation are done in imagination, so they are practical, low-cost, avoid real aversive stimuli, and raise fewer ethical problems than using actual shocks or drugs to induce nausea.
  • Strength: imaginal desensitisation targets the arousal that triggers gambling, giving the client a portable coping skill (relaxation) they can use when real urges arise.
  • Weakness: much of the supporting evidence comes from small samples and individual case reports (e.g. Glover 1985), which limits how far results generalise and how confidently effectiveness can be claimed.
  • Weakness: aversion-based approaches like covert sensitisation can be uncomfortable and may have high drop-out; effects may not transfer well from imagined scenarios to real casinos or betting apps.

Research methods

  • Grant et al. used a double-blind, placebo-controlled, randomised design — the gold standard for testing drugs — which controls for placebo effects and experimenter bias, giving strong internal validity.
  • It was a pooled/secondary analysis of two trials, which increases sample size and statistical power but combines slightly different drugs and durations, so the groups are not perfectly comparable.
  • Behavioural therapy evidence often relies on case studies, which give rich detail but poor generalisability and no control group, making cause and effect harder to establish.

Going deeper / exam use

  • Compare and contrast the two behavioural therapies precisely: both use imagination and relaxation, but covert sensitisation aims to make gambling aversive (a negative association) whereas imaginal desensitisation aims to make the person calm around gambling cues (removing the arousal). Getting this distinction right is a common exam discriminator.
  • Use Grant et al. to make an evaluation point about individual differences and personalised medicine: the same drug helps some patients (those with alcoholic relatives) far more than others.
  • A model conclusion argues for combined treatment — medication to lower urges, plus behavioural or cognitive therapy to address the learned habit and distorted thinking — because no single approach addresses all causes.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Reductionism vs holism: drug treatment is reductionist (treating urges as brain chemistry) whereas combining it with therapy is more holistic.
  • Free will vs determinism: medication implies behaviour is biologically determined, raising the question of whether the gambler can simply choose to stop.
  • Individual vs situational: Grant et al. highlight individual (genetic/family-history) differences in who benefits, arguing against a one-size-fits-all treatment.
  • Nature of evidence: double-blind trials (Grant) versus case studies (Glover) illustrate the trade-off between scientific rigour and rich, realistic detail.

Anxiety & fear-related disorders: diagnosis

The category and how ICD-11 organises it

  • Anxiety and fear-related disorders share the core feature of excessive fear and anxiety together with related behavioural disturbances, where the fear is out of proportion to any actual danger and is persistent (typically several months or more).
  • Fear is the response to a perceived immediate threat (present-focused), whereas anxiety is anticipation of a future threat (future-focused). ICD-11 distinguishes disorders largely by what triggers the fear/anxiety.
  • The three you must know are generalised anxiety disorder (no specific focus), agoraphobia (situations from which escape is hard), and specific phobia (a particular object or situation), including the special case of blood-injection-injury phobia.

Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD) and agoraphobia

  • GAD in ICD-11 is marked by general apprehension or worry that is not restricted to any particular situation ('free-floating' anxiety), persisting for at least several months on more days than not. It is accompanied by symptoms such as muscle tension, restlessness, difficulty concentrating, irritability, and sleep disturbance.
  • The worry in GAD is excessive and difficult to control, and it typically covers everyday matters such as family, health, finances, or work.
  • Agoraphobia is marked fear or anxiety about multiple situations where escape might be difficult or help unavailable if panic-like symptoms occur — e.g. crowds, public transport, queues, or being outside the home alone. The situations are avoided, endured with intense fear, or only faced with a companion, and the pattern lasts at least several months and impairs functioning.

Specific phobia and blood-injection-injury (BII) phobia

  • A specific phobia is a marked, excessive, persistent fear of a particular object or situation (e.g. spiders, heights, flying) that is out of proportion to real danger. The stimulus is actively avoided or endured with intense fear, and the reaction is often immediate.
  • Blood-injection-injury (BII) phobia is a specific-phobia subtype triggered by the sight of blood, injury, injections, or medical procedures. It is distinctive because of its unusual physiological pattern.
  • Most phobias produce a single-phase sympathetic response (heart rate and blood pressure rise — 'fight or flight'). BII phobia produces a diphasic (two-phase) vasovagal response: an initial brief rise in heart rate and blood pressure, followed by a sudden sharp drop, which reduces blood flow to the brain and causes fainting (vasovagal syncope).
  • This fainting response is clinically important because it explains why the standard treatment (relaxation-based systematic desensitisation) is unsuitable for BII phobia — relaxing lowers blood pressure further and makes fainting more likely (see Day 20).

Measures: GAD-7 and the BIPI

  • The GAD-7 is a 7-item self-report questionnaire measuring the severity of generalised anxiety symptoms over the past two weeks. Each item is scored 0-3, giving a total of 0-21, with higher scores indicating more severe anxiety and cut-off bands for mild, moderate, and severe. It is quick, standardised, and widely used for screening and tracking change.
  • The Blood Injection Phobia Inventory (BIPI; Mas et al., 2010) is a self-report inventory specifically for BII phobia. It presents blood/injection/injury situations and asks respondents to rate their cognitive, physiological (including fainting), and behavioural reactions, capturing the distinctive vasovagal component that general phobia measures miss.
  • Standardised measures like these improve reliability (consistent scoring across clinicians), allow diagnosis against agreed cut-offs, and let clinicians track improvement objectively during treatment.

Key terms

  • Fear vs anxiety: fear = response to immediate perceived threat; anxiety = anticipation of future threat.
  • Generalised anxiety disorder: persistent, uncontrollable 'free-floating' worry not tied to one situation, with physical tension symptoms.
  • Agoraphobia: fear/avoidance of situations where escape or help would be difficult.
  • Specific phobia: excessive, persistent fear of a particular object or situation, actively avoided.
  • Diphasic (vasovagal) response: two-phase physiological reaction in BII phobia — arousal then a sudden blood-pressure drop causing fainting.
  • GAD-7: 7-item self-report scale (0-21) for generalised anxiety severity.
  • BIPI (Mas et al., 2010): self-report inventory tailored to blood-injection-injury phobia.

Anxiety & fear-related disorders: diagnosis — evaluation & recall

Evaluating classification of these disorders

  • Strength: ICD-11 gives clear, agreed criteria (specific triggers, duration of at least several months, impaired functioning), which improves inter-rater reliability so different clinicians reach the same diagnosis.
  • Strength: distinguishing subtypes such as BII phobia has real clinical value because it changes treatment (applied tension rather than relaxation), showing the diagnosis is not merely labelling.
  • Weakness: high comorbidity and symptom overlap (e.g. GAD frequently co-occurs with depression and with other anxiety disorders) can make boundaries fuzzy and diagnosis less clear-cut.
  • Weakness: the line between a 'normal' worry/fear and a diagnosable disorder relies on judgements of 'excessive' and 'out of proportion', which can be subjective and culturally influenced.

Evaluating the measures (validity and reliability)

  • Strength: self-report scales like the GAD-7 and BIPI are quick, cheap, standardised, and produce quantitative scores that allow screening, comparison, and tracking of change — good for reliability.
  • The BIPI improves validity for BII phobia specifically because it measures the fainting/vasovagal reaction that generic anxiety scales ignore, so it captures what it is meant to capture.
  • Weakness: self-report is vulnerable to social desirability bias and demand characteristics — people may under- or over-report symptoms — and requires honest insight, which reduces validity.
  • Weakness: cut-off scores are somewhat arbitrary, so someone just below threshold may still be significantly impaired; measures should support, not replace, clinical judgement.

Research methods

  • Diagnostic measures are typically validated by correlating them with established scales or with clinician diagnosis (concurrent/criterion validity) and by test-retest reliability (does the same person score similarly on two occasions?).
  • Self-report questionnaires give quantitative, easily analysed data and large samples, but sacrifice depth and are only as valid as the respondent's honesty and insight.
  • The Mas et al. (2010) BIPI was developed and validated on samples of people with and without BII fear to check it discriminates between groups — an example of establishing a measure's validity.

Going deeper / exam use

  • The single most examinable fact in this topic is the diphasic vasovagal response and why it makes BII phobia special — link it forward to why relaxation-based treatment is unsuitable (Day 20).
  • Be ready to compare GAD (no specific focus, chronic worry) with specific phobia (clear focus, immediate reaction) to show you understand how ICD-11 differentiates within the category.
  • When discussing measures, always name the disorder they belong to (GAD-7 for GAD; BIPI for BII phobia) and give one reliability and one validity point — examiners reward this precision.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Cultural and individual differences: judgements of what counts as 'excessive' fear vary across cultures, so classification and measures may not be equally valid everywhere.
  • Usefulness of psychology: reliable diagnosis and standardised measures (GAD-7, BIPI) enable appropriate treatment and service planning.
  • Reliability vs validity: standardised self-report scales are reliable and easy to use but their validity is threatened by social desirability and insight.
  • Nature of the diphasic response: BII phobia illustrates how a single category (specific phobia) can contain physiologically distinct subtypes needing different treatment.

Fear-related disorders: explanations

Biological explanation: genetics and preparedness

  • The genetic view is that a vulnerability to developing phobias and anxiety is partly inherited. Twin and family studies show phobias run in families, and specific subtypes cluster: Öst (1992) found that blood-injection-injury phobia in particular tends to run in families, with a high proportion of BII phobics having a first-degree relative with the same phobia — consistent with an inherited predisposition (possibly to the fainting/vasovagal reaction).
  • Preparedness (Seligman) is an evolutionary idea: we are biologically prepared to acquire fears rapidly to things that threatened our ancestors' survival — snakes, spiders, heights, blood, small spaces. Such fears are learned quickly, are hard to extinguish, and are far more common than fears of modern dangers like cars or electricity.
  • Preparedness explains why phobia objects are non-random: it is not chance that phobias cluster around ancient dangers. It bridges biology and learning — evolution 'pre-loads' certain associations that experience then triggers.

Behavioural explanation: two-process model

  • Mowrer's two-process model explains how phobias are both acquired and maintained. Process 1 (acquisition) is classical conditioning: a neutral stimulus is paired with something that naturally causes fear and comes to trigger fear itself.
  • Process 2 (maintenance) is operant conditioning through avoidance: once the phobia exists, the person avoids the feared object; avoidance reduces their anxiety, and this relief is a reward (negative reinforcement) that strengthens the avoidance. Because they keep avoiding, they never learn that the object is actually harmless, so the fear persists.
  • This two-process account is powerful because it explains both where phobias come from (a learning event) and why they do not simply fade away over time (avoidance blocks natural extinction). It also directly justifies exposure-based treatments, which prevent avoidance so extinction can finally occur (Day 20).

Psychodynamic explanation: displacement

  • The psychodynamic view (Freud) is that a phobia is not really about the feared object at all; it is a symbol. Unconscious anxiety, often from a repressed conflict, is displaced (transferred) onto an external, more manageable object. The person can then avoid the object instead of confronting the true, threatening source of anxiety.
  • Displacement is a defence mechanism: it protects the ego from unbearable internal conflict by shifting the emotion onto a safe substitute target. The phobic object represents the real, hidden source of fear in disguised form.
  • Freud's case of Little Hans (1909) is the classic illustration (see below): Hans's fear of horses was interpreted as displaced fear connected to Oedipal conflict with his father.

Freud (1909) 'Little Hans' in detail

  • Little Hans was a 5-year-old boy with a phobia of horses; he feared that a horse would bite him or fall down, and refused to leave the house. Freud analysed him at a distance via letters from Hans's father.
  • Freud interpreted the phobia through the Oedipus complex: Hans unconsciously desired his mother and saw his father as a rival, fearing his father would castrate/punish him. This forbidden fear was too threatening, so it was displaced onto horses — the horse's black muzzle and blinkers supposedly symbolising the father's moustache and glasses; a horse falling symbolising the (wished-for and feared) removal of the father.
  • For Freud, the case supported the idea that phobias are displaced expressions of unconscious conflict. Critics argue the evidence is a single case, interpreted subjectively by a father who already believed Freud's theory, and can be explained more simply by classical conditioning (Hans had been frightened when a horse pulling a cart collapsed in the street).

Key terms

  • Preparedness: an evolved readiness to acquire fears quickly to ancestral dangers (snakes, blood, heights).
  • Two-process model: classical conditioning acquires a phobia; operant conditioning (negative reinforcement via avoidance) maintains it.
  • Negative reinforcement: strengthening a behaviour by removing an unpleasant state (here, anxiety relief from avoidance).
  • Displacement: a defence mechanism transferring anxiety from its true source onto a safer substitute object.
  • Neutral/unconditioned/conditioned stimulus (NS/UCS/CS): the learning-theory elements shown in the Little Albert study below.
⭐ Key study — Watson & Rayner (1920) — 'Little Albert': conditioned emotional reactions

Aim: To demonstrate that an emotional response, specifically fear, can be classically conditioned in a human infant by pairing a previously neutral stimulus with a naturally fear-inducing stimulus — and to see whether the fear would generalise to similar objects.

Method: Design: a controlled laboratory experiment / single-case study on one infant, 'Albert B', around 9-11 months old. Procedure: baseline tests showed Albert was not afraid of a white rat, a rabbit, a dog, cotton wool, or masks (the neutral stimuli). To condition fear, whenever Albert reached for the white rat (neutral stimulus, NS), the experimenters struck a steel bar behind his head with a hammer, making a sudden loud noise (unconditioned stimulus, UCS) that naturally produced fear/crying (unconditioned response, UCR). This pairing was repeated several times.

Results: After the pairings, the white rat alone (now the conditioned stimulus, CS) made Albert cry and try to crawl away (conditioned response, CR) — even without the noise. The fear also generalised: Albert showed fear of other white, furry things he had not been conditioned to, such as a rabbit, a dog, a fur coat, cotton wool, and a Santa Claus mask.

Conclusion: Fear responses (phobias) can be learned through classical conditioning, and a learned fear can generalise to similar stimuli. This supports the behavioural explanation that phobias are acquired by association rather than being innate or purely symbolic. (Note the serious ethical problems: Albert was distressed and, notoriously, the fear was reportedly not deconditioned before he left the study.)

Fear-related disorders: explanations — evaluation & recall

Evaluating the biological explanation

  • Strength: the clustering of phobias around evolutionarily dangerous objects (rather than modern hazards) strongly supports preparedness, and family data such as Öst (1992) on BII phobia support a genetic component.
  • Strength: it explains why exposure does not always cure phobias easily and why some fears are acquired with a single frightening experience while others never form.
  • Weakness: it is reductionist and cannot fully explain phobias of clearly modern objects; nor does it explain why, within a family sharing genes, only some members develop a phobia — pointing to environmental triggers too.
  • Weakness: family resemblance may reflect shared environment and observational learning, not just genes, so 'runs in families' does not prove inheritance.

Evaluating behavioural and psychodynamic explanations

  • Behavioural strength: it is testable and supported by controlled evidence (Watson & Rayner), and it leads directly to effective exposure treatments — a major practical success that supports the theory.
  • Behavioural weakness: many people with phobias cannot recall any conditioning event, and many who experience trauma (e.g. a dog bite) never develop a phobia, so classical conditioning is not the whole story (preparedness helps fill the gap).
  • Psychodynamic strength: it attempts to explain phobias that seem to have no learning history and highlights the possible role of unconscious factors.
  • Psychodynamic weakness: it rests on subjective interpretation and untestable unconscious concepts. Little Hans is a single, biased case that can be re-explained by simple conditioning, so it is scientifically weak.

Research methods

  • Watson & Rayner was a controlled laboratory single-case experiment: strong control over variables and clear demonstration of cause and effect, but a sample of one severely limits generalisability, and it is a landmark example of an unethical study (harm, no protection from psychological damage).
  • Little Hans is a case study using data reported by a biased third party (the father), giving rich qualitative detail but very poor objectivity, reliability, and generalisability.
  • Öst (1992) uses correlational family/twin-type data, which shows association (phobias run in families) but cannot separate genes from shared environment or prove causation.

Going deeper / exam use

  • For Little Albert, be able to label the conditioning precisely: NS = white rat; UCS = loud noise; UCR = fear to the noise; CS = white rat (after pairing); CR = fear to the rat; plus generalisation to other furry objects. Examiners frequently ask you to identify these.
  • A high-level answer combines explanations: preparedness (biology) makes certain associations easy to form, classical conditioning (behavioural) actually forms them, and avoidance (behavioural) maintains them — an integrated, diathesis-stress account.
  • Use Little Hans as a critical example: contrast the psychodynamic interpretation with the simpler behavioural one (the collapsing cart-horse) to show evaluation of competing explanations.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Nature vs nurture: genetics/preparedness (nature) versus classical conditioning and displacement (nurture); an interactionist view combines an inherited vulnerability with a learning trigger.
  • Ethics in research: Watson & Rayner raises serious issues of harm and lack of protection, weighed against the scientific value of demonstrating conditioned fear.
  • Scientific vs unfalsifiable explanations: behavioural theory is testable, whereas psychodynamic displacement relies on unfalsifiable unconscious concepts.
  • Determinism: both conditioning and unconscious-conflict accounts are deterministic, portraying the phobia as caused by forces outside the person's conscious control.

Anxiety & fear: treatment

Systematic desensitisation (SD): the principles

  • Systematic desensitisation is a behavioural therapy based on counter-conditioning: a phobia is unlearned by gradually replacing the fear response to the object with a relaxation response instead. It applies the two-process idea in reverse — exposure without avoidance allows extinction.
  • It rests on reciprocal inhibition: you cannot feel deeply relaxed and intensely anxious at the same time, so if the person stays relaxed while facing the feared stimulus, the relaxation 'inhibits' and eventually replaces the fear.
  • There are three stages. (1) Relaxation training — the client learns a deep relaxation technique such as progressive muscle relaxation or controlled breathing. (2) Constructing a fear hierarchy — a ranked list of feared situations from least to most frightening (e.g. picture of a spider up to holding a spider). (3) Gradual exposure — while remaining relaxed, the client works up the hierarchy one step at a time, only moving up when calm at the current step.
  • Exposure can be in vitro (imagined) or in vivo (real, live exposure). In vivo exposure is generally more effective because it transfers better to real life, though in vitro is safer and easier for some stimuli.

Applied tension for BII phobia (and why relaxation is unsuitable)

  • Blood-injection-injury phobia is the exception where standard relaxation-based SD is inappropriate. Because BII phobia produces a diphasic vasovagal response — a brief rise then a sharp DROP in heart rate and blood pressure leading to fainting — teaching the client to relax would lower blood pressure even further and make fainting MORE likely, worsening the problem.
  • Applied tension (Öst) does the opposite of relaxation. The client is taught to repeatedly tense the large muscle groups of the body (arms, chest, legs) for about 10-15 seconds, release briefly, and repeat. This raises blood pressure and increases blood flow to the brain, preventing the drop that causes fainting.
  • Once the client can reliably prevent fainting using applied tension, they combine it with graded exposure to blood/injection/injury cues (like a hierarchy), so they can face the feared stimulus without passing out. Preventing the faint removes the very consequence that reinforced the fear and avoidance.

Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)

  • CBT treats phobias by tackling both faulty thinking and behaviour. The cognitive part identifies and challenges catastrophic or irrational beliefs about the feared object (e.g. 'I will faint and die', 'the injection will cause terrible harm') and replaces them with more realistic appraisals.
  • The behavioural part typically includes graded exposure and, for BII phobia, applied tension, so the client tests their new beliefs against reality and builds mastery. Homework and self-monitoring between sessions consolidate the learning.
  • CBT is usually structured and time-limited (often a set number of manualised sessions), which makes it practical and evaluable — as in Chapman & DeLapp's manualised programme below.

Key terms

  • Systematic desensitisation: counter-conditioning therapy using relaxation, a fear hierarchy, and graded exposure to unlearn a phobia.
  • Reciprocal inhibition: relaxation and anxiety cannot coexist, so relaxation displaces fear.
  • Fear hierarchy: ranked list of feared situations from least to most anxiety-provoking.
  • In vitro vs in vivo: imagined exposure vs real, live exposure.
  • Applied tension: tensing muscles to raise blood pressure and prevent fainting in BII phobia — used instead of relaxation.
  • CBT: therapy combining cognitive restructuring of irrational beliefs with behavioural exposure.
⭐ Key study — Chapman & DeLapp (2013) — Nine-session treatment of a blood-injection-injury phobia with manualised CBT (adult case example)

Aim: To demonstrate how a structured, manualised cognitive behavioural therapy incorporating applied muscle tension can effectively treat the multiple components (cognitive, physiological, and behavioural) of blood-injection-injury phobia in an adult.

Method: Design: an individual case study of an adult male client with BII phobia treated over approximately nine sessions of manualised CBT. Procedure: treatment combined psychoeducation about the phobia and the fainting response, cognitive restructuring to challenge catastrophic beliefs, training in applied muscle tension to prevent vasovagal fainting, and graded exposure up a fear hierarchy. Anxiety during exposure was monitored using SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress Scale) ratings. Exposure progressed from watching videos of blood tests, through observing real blood donations at a blood bank, to the client performing a blood-pressure test on himself.

Results: SUDS ratings fell markedly within and across exposure tasks as applied tension controlled the physiological reaction (for example, distress ratings during self-administered blood-pressure testing dropped from around 45 down to 5 across attempts). The client was able to complete increasingly demanding blood/injection tasks without fainting.

Conclusion: Manualised CBT combining cognitive restructuring, applied tension, and graded exposure is an effective and structured treatment for BII phobia, directly addressing the fainting problem that makes relaxation-based approaches unsuitable. Being manualised, it can be replicated and delivered consistently by different therapists.

Anxiety & fear: treatment — evaluation & recall

Evaluating systematic desensitisation

  • Strength: SD is effective for many specific phobias and is gentler and more acceptable to clients than flooding, giving lower drop-out and no need for medication or its side effects.
  • Strength: in vivo SD produces changes that transfer well to real life, and the approach is based on well-established learning principles, giving it a clear theoretical rationale.
  • Weakness: it treats the symptom (the fear response) not any underlying cause, so symptom substitution or relapse is possible, and it is less suited to complex or free-floating anxiety like GAD.
  • Crucial weakness/exception: it is unsuitable for BII phobia because its relaxation component would increase fainting — which is exactly why applied tension exists.

Evaluating applied tension and CBT

  • Applied tension strength: it is specifically designed for the diphasic vasovagal response, tackling the fainting that other therapies ignore; it gives the client a portable, learnable coping skill.
  • CBT strength: by addressing thoughts as well as behaviour, CBT can produce more durable change and treat the beliefs that maintain the phobia; being manualised (Chapman & DeLapp) makes it replicable and easy to evaluate.
  • Weakness: CBT requires effort, insight, and motivation, and exposure is distressing, so drop-out can be high; it may be less suitable for clients who cannot engage cognitively.
  • Weakness of the evidence: Chapman & DeLapp is a single case study, so although it shows the treatment CAN work, it cannot establish general effectiveness or rule out that this particular client would have improved anyway.

Research methods

  • Chapman & DeLapp is a case study using SUDS self-report to track distress: rich, detailed, and shows the process step by step, but with N=1 and no control group it has very limited generalisability and no way to prove the therapy (rather than time or expectation) caused improvement.
  • SUDS is a simple quantitative self-report (0-100) that allows within-person change to be tracked, but it is subjective and open to demand characteristics.
  • Stronger evidence for these therapies comes from randomised controlled trials comparing SD/CBT with control conditions; case studies are best seen as illustrative and hypothesis-generating.

Going deeper / exam use

  • The examiner's favourite link: explain WHY relaxation is unsuitable for BII phobia (it lowers blood pressure, worsening the vasovagal faint) and WHY applied tension is used instead (it raises blood pressure). This connects diagnosis (Day 18) to treatment.
  • Compare SD and CBT: both use graded exposure, but CBT adds explicit cognitive restructuring of irrational beliefs, potentially giving more lasting change for phobias driven by catastrophic thoughts.
  • Use Chapman & DeLapp as concrete evidence for the applied-tension-plus-CBT combination, but always caveat with the case-study limitation to score evaluation marks.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Reductionism vs holism: SD reduces a phobia to a learned response, whereas CBT takes a fuller view including cognition.
  • Idiographic vs nomothetic: Chapman & DeLapp's case study is idiographic (rich detail on one person) versus RCTs that seek general laws.
  • Application to everyday life: understanding the vasovagal response has direct practical value in tailoring treatment (applied tension) for BII patients.
  • Ethics: exposure therapies deliberately cause short-term distress, which must be justified by long-term benefit and informed consent.

OCD: diagnosis

What OCD is and how ICD-11 defines it

  • Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is characterised by persistent obsessions and/or compulsions. In ICD-11, OCD is the anchor condition of the 'obsessive-compulsive and related disorders' grouping.
  • Obsessions are repetitive, intrusive, unwanted thoughts, images, or urges that cause marked anxiety or distress. The person experiences them as intrusive and usually tries to ignore, suppress, or neutralise them. Common themes are contamination, harm, symmetry, and forbidden thoughts.
  • Compulsions (rituals) are repetitive behaviours or mental acts that the person feels driven to perform in response to an obsession, or according to rigid rules, in order to reduce the anxiety or to prevent a feared event. The behaviour is either not realistically connected to what it is meant to prevent, or is clearly excessive.
  • For diagnosis, the symptoms are time-consuming (a common benchmark is more than about an hour a day) or cause significant distress or impairment in daily functioning, and are persistent.

The obsession-compulsion cycle and insight

  • OCD typically runs as a vicious cycle: an obsession (e.g. 'my hands are contaminated') triggers anxiety, the compulsion (washing) temporarily reduces that anxiety, and this relief reinforces the compulsion so the cycle repeats and often worsens over time.
  • Insight is important in ICD-11: it refers to how far the person recognises that their obsessive beliefs are not (or may not be) true. Most people with OCD have fair-to-good insight — they know their fears are exaggerated but still feel compelled to act — while a minority have poor or absent insight, which affects severity and treatment.
  • This cycle links diagnosis to explanation (the compulsion is negatively reinforced by anxiety reduction — Day 22) and treatment (breaking the cycle by preventing the compulsion — Day 23).

Common presentations

  • Contamination/washing: obsessive fear of germs, dirt, or illness leading to compulsive, often ritualised hand-washing, cleaning, or avoidance of 'contaminated' places.
  • Checking: obsessive doubt that something bad will happen (e.g. a fire, a break-in) leading to repeated checking of locks, appliances, or switches, often a set number of times.
  • Symmetry and ordering: a need for things to be 'just right', symmetrical, or in a particular order, with distress if the arrangement is disturbed; compulsions include arranging, counting, or repeating actions until they feel correct.
  • These presentations show that OCD content varies but the underlying obsession-anxiety-compulsion-relief structure is the same.

Rapoport (1989) 'The boy who couldn't stop washing' — Charles

  • Rapoport's clinical account of 'Charles' is a vivid case illustrating contamination OCD. Charles was a teenager consumed by fears of contamination who spent hours each day washing and showering, to the point that it took over his life and he could barely function or leave home.
  • His compulsive washing gave only brief relief before the obsessive thoughts returned, demonstrating the self-perpetuating obsession-compulsion cycle and how time-consuming and disabling severe OCD becomes.
  • The case is used to show real presentation, the distress and impairment criteria, and the failure of the person's own efforts to control the rituals — and Rapoport used such cases to argue for a biological basis and drug treatment.

Key terms and measures

  • Obsession: recurrent, intrusive, unwanted thought/image/urge causing anxiety.
  • Compulsion: repetitive behaviour or mental act performed to reduce anxiety or prevent a feared outcome.
  • Insight: the degree to which the person recognises their obsessive beliefs are unrealistic.
  • MOCI (Maudsley Obsessional-Compulsive Inventory): a self-report questionnaire with true/false items assessing OCD symptoms, particularly checking and cleaning subtypes; quick to administer.
  • Y-BOCS (Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale): a clinician-administered, semi-structured interview that separately rates the severity of obsessions and compulsions (time spent, interference, distress, resistance, control), producing a total severity score widely used to diagnose and to track change during treatment.

OCD: diagnosis — evaluation & recall

Evaluating classification of OCD

  • Strength: ICD-11 provides clear operational criteria (obsessions/compulsions, time-consuming, distress/impairment, level of insight), improving reliability so clinicians agree on diagnoses.
  • Strength: recognising insight as a specifier is clinically useful because low insight predicts a poorer response to standard therapy, so the diagnosis guides treatment.
  • Weakness: OCD symptoms overlap with other conditions (e.g. GAD, hoarding, body-focused disorders) and OCD is highly comorbid with depression, which can blur diagnostic boundaries.
  • Weakness: deciding when 'careful' or 'tidy' behaviour becomes a disorder relies on the distress/impairment judgement, which has a subjective element and can be culturally shaped (what counts as excessive washing or checking).

Evaluating the measures (MOCI and Y-BOCS)

  • The Y-BOCS is a strength because it is a standardised, clinician-rated interview that scores obsessions and compulsions separately and is sensitive to change, making it excellent for measuring treatment outcome (it is the standard outcome measure in OCD trials, including Lovell et al. — Day 23).
  • The MOCI is quick and easy as a self-report screen, but it mainly captures certain subtypes (washing/checking) and, being self-report, is open to social desirability and limited insight, reducing validity.
  • Clinician-administered measures (Y-BOCS) tend to have higher validity than pure self-report but are more time-consuming and require trained raters, and inter-rater reliability must be checked.

Research methods

  • Rapoport's 'Charles' is a case study: rich, detailed, high in ecological validity and useful for illustrating rare or severe presentations, but low in generalisability and reliant on subjective clinical interpretation.
  • Diagnostic measures are validated through reliability checks (test-retest, inter-rater for the Y-BOCS) and validity checks (correlating with clinical diagnosis or other measures).
  • The distinction between self-report (MOCI) and clinician-rated interview (Y-BOCS) is a classic evaluation point about the trade-off between speed/ease and validity/depth.

Going deeper / exam use

  • Always define obsessions and compulsions separately and precisely, then link them through the obsession-anxiety-compulsion-relief cycle — this cycle recurs in explanation (negative reinforcement) and treatment (ERP).
  • Know the two measures by type: MOCI = self-report questionnaire; Y-BOCS = clinician-administered interview rating severity. Being able to state one strength and one weakness of each is commonly rewarded.
  • Use Charles (Rapoport) as concrete evidence of the 'time-consuming' and 'impairment' criteria and of insight (he knew washing was excessive but could not stop).
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Cultural and individual differences: what counts as excessive cleaning/checking varies culturally, affecting the validity of the distress/impairment threshold.
  • Reliability vs validity: standardised measures (Y-BOCS) improve reliability, but self-report tools (MOCI) risk lower validity from limited insight and social desirability.
  • Idiographic vs nomothetic: Rapoport's case study is idiographic and detailed, whereas standardised scales aim for nomothetic comparison.
  • Usefulness of psychology: reliable diagnosis and severity scoring enable appropriate, measurable treatment and research.

OCD: explanations

Biological explanation 1: biochemistry (serotonin and the worry circuit)

  • The biochemical account proposes that OCD involves abnormally low or dysregulated serotonin activity. Serotonin is a neurotransmitter involved in mood and in regulating behaviour; if serotonin transmission is impaired, thoughts and behaviours may not be properly 'switched off', producing repetitive obsessions and compulsions.
  • The strongest support is that SSRIs (drugs that increase serotonin availability) reduce OCD symptoms, implying serotonin is involved (see Day 23).
  • The account also implicates a specific brain network — the orbitofrontal-caudate 'worry circuit'. The orbitofrontal cortex detects when something is 'wrong' (e.g. contamination) and sends worry signals; the caudate nucleus (in the basal ganglia) normally filters and suppresses these signals; the thalamus relays them onward. In OCD the caudate is thought to be underactive, so it fails to filter out the worry signals, which loop repeatedly between the orbitofrontal cortex and thalamus — experienced as persistent obsessions and an urge to perform compulsions.
  • Brain-imaging studies showing heightened activity in this circuit in OCD patients (which reduces after successful treatment) support the worry-circuit model.

Biological explanation 2: genetics

  • OCD runs in families and shows higher concordance in identical (MZ) than non-identical (DZ) twins, suggesting an inherited vulnerability. First-degree relatives of people with OCD have a raised risk of developing it.
  • The genetic account is diathesis-stress: people may inherit a predisposition (e.g. genes affecting serotonin function or the worry circuit) that is then triggered by environmental stress or experience. No single 'OCD gene' has been found; many genes each contribute a small effect (polygenic).
  • Genetics complements the biochemical explanation because the inherited vulnerability is thought to act partly through serotonin systems and the neural worry circuit.

Cognitive explanation

  • The cognitive account argues that intrusive thoughts are actually normal — most people have unwanted thoughts occasionally. What makes them pathological in OCD is how the person interprets and appraises them.
  • Inflated responsibility: the person believes they are personally responsible for preventing harm to themselves or others, so an intrusive thought feels like a duty to act (e.g. 'if I don't check, and there's a fire, it will be my fault').
  • Thought-action fusion (TAF): the belief that thinking something is morally equivalent to doing it, or that thinking about a bad event makes it more likely to happen. This makes intrusive thoughts unbearably threatening.
  • These thinking errors cause the person to try to neutralise the thought through a compulsion; the compulsion briefly reduces anxiety, reinforcing the faulty belief that it was necessary — maintaining OCD.

Behavioural and psychodynamic explanations

  • Behavioural: OCD is explained through operant conditioning. The compulsion is maintained by negative reinforcement — performing the ritual removes the unpleasant anxiety caused by the obsession, and this relief rewards and strengthens the compulsion, making it more likely to recur. The behaviour persists because avoidance/ritual prevents the person from ever learning the feared outcome would not happen.
  • Psychodynamic: Freud viewed OCD as arising from unconscious conflict, often traced to fixation at the anal stage. Obsessions and compulsions are seen as defence mechanisms (e.g. undoing, isolation, reaction formation) that keep repressed impulses out of awareness; the ritual symbolically controls forbidden urges. This explanation is criticised as unfalsifiable and lacking scientific support.

Key terms

  • Serotonin: neurotransmitter implicated in OCD; low/dysregulated activity linked to repetitive thoughts and behaviours.
  • Orbitofrontal-caudate 'worry circuit': orbitofrontal cortex-caudate-thalamus loop that, when the caudate under-filters, produces persistent worry signals.
  • Diathesis-stress: an inherited vulnerability triggered by environmental stress.
  • Inflated responsibility: exaggerated belief that one must prevent harm.
  • Thought-action fusion: belief that thinking something is equivalent to doing it or makes it more likely.
  • Negative reinforcement: compulsions are strengthened because they remove obsession-driven anxiety.

OCD: explanations — evaluation & recall

Evaluating the biological explanation

  • Strength: strong converging evidence — SSRIs reduce symptoms (implicating serotonin), brain-imaging shows worry-circuit overactivity that normalises after treatment, and twin/family data support genetics. This makes it scientific and testable.
  • Strength: it explains why OCD is often chronic and biologically responsive to medication, and it removes blame from the sufferer.
  • Weakness: it is largely correlational — abnormal serotonin or brain activity may be a result of OCD rather than its cause, and 'SSRIs work therefore serotonin causes OCD' is a treatment-aetiology fallacy.
  • Weakness: it is reductionist and cannot fully explain the specific content of obsessions (why contamination vs checking) or why psychological therapy (ERP/CBT) also works well — pointing to cognitive and learning factors.

Evaluating the cognitive, behavioural and psychodynamic explanations

  • Cognitive strength: it explains the specific content and personal meaning of obsessions, is supported by evidence that OCD sufferers show inflated responsibility and TAF, and leads directly to effective CBT.
  • Cognitive/behavioural complementarity: the behavioural account neatly explains why compulsions persist (negative reinforcement) while the cognitive account explains why the obsession is threatening in the first place — together they justify ERP plus CBT.
  • Behavioural weakness: on its own it explains maintenance of compulsions better than the origin of obsessions.
  • Psychodynamic weakness: it relies on unfalsifiable unconscious concepts and has little scientific support, so it is generally regarded as the weakest explanation, though it highlights that early conflict and symbolic meaning might matter.

Research methods

  • Biological evidence uses brain-imaging (e.g. PET/fMRI) — objective and quantitative, showing worry-circuit activity — but scanning studies are correlational and often use small samples.
  • Genetic evidence uses twin and family studies; higher MZ than DZ concordance suggests heritability, but MZ twins also share more environment, so genes and environment are hard to disentangle.
  • Cognitive explanations are tested with questionnaires and experiments measuring beliefs like inflated responsibility and TAF, which allow quantification but rely on self-report and may not prove these beliefs cause OCD rather than accompany it.

Going deeper / exam use

  • Explain the worry circuit as a chain: orbitofrontal cortex raises the alarm, an underactive caudate fails to filter it, the thalamus keeps relaying it, so the worry loops — this level of mechanistic detail scores highly.
  • Beware the treatment-aetiology fallacy: the fact SSRIs help does not prove low serotonin causes OCD. Making this critical point demonstrates strong evaluation.
  • Integrate for top marks: an inherited biological vulnerability (worry circuit/serotonin) may produce intrusive thoughts, which faulty cognition (inflated responsibility, TAF) then misinterprets, and negative reinforcement of compulsions maintains the cycle — a diathesis-stress synthesis linking all levels.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Nature vs nurture: biological (serotonin, genes, worry circuit) versus cognitive/behavioural (appraisals, reinforcement) explanations; a diathesis-stress interactionist position is strongest.
  • Reductionism vs holism: the worry-circuit account is reductionist, whereas combining biology, cognition, and learning is holistic.
  • Determinism vs free will: biological and behavioural accounts are deterministic, implying limited conscious control over obsessions and compulsions.
  • Scientific vs unfalsifiable: biological and cognitive explanations are testable, whereas the psychodynamic account is largely unfalsifiable.

OCD: treatment + Clinical wrap-up

Biological treatment: SSRIs

  • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), such as fluoxetine and sertraline, are the first-line drug treatment for OCD. They work by blocking the reuptake of serotonin into the presynaptic neuron, leaving more serotonin available in the synapse to stimulate the next neuron.
  • By boosting serotonin transmission, SSRIs are thought to help 'quieten' the overactive worry circuit and reduce the frequency and intensity of obsessions and compulsions. Effects build up gradually, often taking several weeks, and OCD may need higher doses and longer than depression does.
  • SSRIs treat symptoms rather than the underlying cause, have side effects (e.g. nausea, insomnia, sexual dysfunction), and symptoms often return if the drug is stopped, so they are frequently combined with psychological therapy.

Exposure and response prevention (ERP)

  • ERP is the core behavioural treatment for OCD and directly breaks the obsession-compulsion cycle. 'Exposure' means deliberately confronting the situation that triggers the obsession (e.g. touching a 'contaminated' door handle); 'response prevention' means NOT performing the usual compulsion (not washing).
  • The key process is habituation: if the person stays in the feared situation without ritualising, their anxiety naturally rises and then falls on its own, and they learn that the feared catastrophe does not happen and that the compulsion is unnecessary. Repeated practice up a hierarchy makes the anxiety response fade.
  • ERP works because it removes the negative reinforcement that maintained the compulsion (Day 22): the ritual no longer 'rescues' the person from anxiety, so the link between obsession and compulsion weakens.
  • Lehmkuhl et al. (2008) reported the successful use of ERP-based CBT in treating OCD (in a case with co-occurring autism spectrum condition), illustrating that exposure with response prevention can be effective and adaptable.

Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)

  • CBT for OCD combines ERP with cognitive restructuring. The cognitive part identifies and challenges the faulty beliefs that fuel OCD — inflated responsibility and thought-action fusion — helping the person reappraise intrusive thoughts as normal and not dangerous.
  • By changing both the appraisal of intrusive thoughts (cognitive) and the ritual behaviour (behavioural via ERP), CBT tackles the cause of the anxiety and the maintaining behaviour together, which can give more durable results than medication alone.
  • CBT is usually structured and time-limited, which makes it practical to deliver and to evaluate — and, as Lovell et al. show below, it can even be delivered effectively by telephone.

Key terms

  • SSRI: drug that blocks serotonin reuptake, increasing synaptic serotonin to reduce OCD symptoms.
  • Exposure and response prevention (ERP): confronting the trigger while refraining from the compulsion.
  • Habituation: the natural decline of anxiety when a feared situation is faced without ritualising.
  • Cognitive restructuring: identifying and challenging faulty beliefs (inflated responsibility, TAF).
  • Non-inferiority trial: a study designed to show a new/cheaper treatment is not meaningfully worse than the standard one.
⭐ Key study — Lovell et al. (2006) — Telephone-administered CBT for OCD: a randomised controlled non-inferiority trial

Aim: To test whether cognitive behaviour therapy (with exposure and response prevention) delivered by telephone is as effective as the same therapy delivered face to face for treating OCD — i.e. a non-inferiority test aimed at improving access to treatment.

Method: Design: a randomised controlled non-inferiority trial. Sample: 72 patients with OCD, recruited from psychology outpatient departments in the UK. Procedure: patients were randomly allocated to receive CBT (including exposure and response prevention) either by telephone or face to face, over 10 weekly sessions. Outcomes were measured with the Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale (Y-BOCS) as the main measure, plus the Beck Depression Inventory and a client satisfaction questionnaire, assessed at the end of treatment and at follow-up.

Results: Telephone-delivered CBT produced improvements in OCD symptoms (Y-BOCS) that were not significantly worse than face-to-face CBT — the two were clinically equivalent (non-inferior). Patient satisfaction was high and similar in both groups, and telephone sessions were shorter, saving substantial therapist time.

Conclusion: CBT for OCD can be delivered effectively by telephone with outcomes comparable to face-to-face therapy, while saving therapist time and improving access for people who cannot easily attend clinics (e.g. severe avoidance, rural location). This supports flexible, cost-effective delivery of an evidence-based treatment.

OCD: treatment + Clinical wrap-up — evaluation & recall

Evaluating the treatments

  • SSRIs strength: supported by controlled trials, easy to administer, effective for many patients, and helpful when severe symptoms prevent engagement in therapy. Weakness: side effects, delayed onset, high relapse when stopped, and they treat symptoms not causes.
  • ERP strength: it directly targets the maintaining mechanism (removing negative reinforcement) and produces durable change through habituation; it is strongly evidence-based. Weakness: it is distressing and demanding, so drop-out can be high and it requires client commitment.
  • CBT strength: by adding cognitive restructuring it addresses the faulty beliefs behind OCD, giving more lasting change than medication alone; it is adaptable (e.g. by telephone, per Lovell). Weakness: it needs insight, motivation, and trained therapists, and may be less suitable for very low-insight patients.
  • Best practice often combines SSRIs (to reduce severe symptoms) with CBT/ERP (to change thinking and behaviour), because the biological and psychological approaches address different parts of the disorder.

Research methods

  • Lovell et al. used a randomised controlled trial, the gold-standard design: random allocation controls for individual differences and allows a fair comparison, giving high internal validity, and the Y-BOCS gives an objective, standardised outcome measure.
  • It was specifically a non-inferiority trial — designed not to show telephone CBT is BETTER, but that it is not meaningfully WORSE than face-to-face therapy; understanding this design distinction is a strong evaluation point.
  • Strengths include real patients and a clinically relevant question (access/cost); limitations include a modest sample (72) and the fact that self-selected patients willing to try telephone therapy may not represent all OCD sufferers.

  • Watson & Rayner (1920) — fear-related disorders (explanation): 'Little Albert' controlled single-case experiment; a fear of a white rat (CS) was classically conditioned by pairing it with a loud noise (UCS), and the fear generalised to other furry objects. Supports conditioning of phobias (highly unethical).
  • Chapman & DeLapp (2013) — fear-related disorders (treatment): nine-session manualised CBT case study of an adult with BII phobia; combined cognitive restructuring, applied tension, and graded exposure, with SUDS distress ratings falling steeply and no fainting. Shows why applied tension replaces relaxation in BII phobia.
  • Lovell et al. (2006) — OCD (treatment): RCT non-inferiority trial; 72 OCD patients received 10 weekly CBT/ERP sessions by telephone or face to face, with equivalent Y-BOCS outcomes and high satisfaction. Shows CBT can be delivered flexibly and cost-effectively.
  • (Diagnosis case examples to know alongside these: Rapoport's 'Charles' for OCD contamination/washing, and Freud's 'Little Hans' for the psychodynamic explanation of phobias.)

Going deeper / exam use

  • Link treatment to explanation explicitly: SSRIs follow from the serotonin/worry-circuit account; ERP follows from the behavioural (negative reinforcement) account; cognitive restructuring follows from the cognitive (inflated responsibility, TAF) account. This mapping earns marks.
  • Use 'habituation' and 'removal of negative reinforcement' as the two mechanisms that explain WHY ERP works — precise mechanism naming is rewarded.
  • Use Lovell et al. both as evidence CBT is effective and as an evaluation vehicle for the non-inferiority RCT design and issues of access, cost, and generalisability.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Reductionism vs holism: SSRIs are reductionist (serotonin), CBT/ERP more holistic; combined treatment is the most complete.
  • Free will vs determinism: drug treatment implies biological determinism, whereas CBT assumes the person can change how they appraise their thoughts.
  • Application to everyday life: Lovell et al. show psychology improving access to care through telephone delivery, especially valuable for housebound or rural patients.
  • Nature of evidence: RCTs (Lovell) versus case studies (Lehmkuhl, Chapman & DeLapp) illustrate the trade-off between rigorous generalisable evidence and rich individual detail.

Patient-practitioner: interpersonal skills

Why communication matters

  • The consultation is the core of healthcare, but it is short (often under 10 minutes) and emotionally loaded, so how the practitioner communicates strongly shapes three key outcomes: patient SATISFACTION, RECALL of information, and ADHERENCE to treatment.
  • If a patient feels rushed, dismissed or confused, they are less satisfied, remember less of the advice, and are less likely to take medication or return — so good interpersonal skills are not just 'nice', they directly affect health.
  • Communication has two channels working together: NON-VERBAL (body language, appearance) and VERBAL (the words used, especially jargon). Both send messages about competence and warmth.

Non-verbal communication (NVC)

  • NVC is everything communicated without words: eye contact, facial expression, posture, tone of voice, touch, proximity and personal space. It often conveys emotion more powerfully than words and is harder to fake.
  • A practitioner who makes eye contact, faces the patient, leans in and nods signals attention and empathy, increasing trust and satisfaction. A doctor typing on a computer or looking away signals disinterest.
  • Patients also give NVC (grimacing, guarding a body part) which a skilled practitioner reads to aid diagnosis. NVC therefore flows both ways.

Practitioner clothing and appearance — McKinstry & Wang (1991)

  • This study tested whether how a GP DRESSES affects patients' confidence in them and willingness to consult. Appearance is a form of NVC that creates a first impression before a word is spoken.
  • Method: patients in Scottish general practices were shown PHOTOGRAPHS of the same male and female doctors dressed in different styles — e.g. formal (suit/white coat), semi-formal, and casual (jeans, cardigan). Patients rated how happy they would be with that doctor and how much confidence they had.
  • Results: patients generally preferred the FORMALLY dressed doctor (traditional suit, or with a white coat) and expressed most confidence in them; the casually dressed doctor inspired least confidence, especially among older and higher-social-class patients.
  • Conclusion: appearance affects patients' confidence and expectations. A formal, conventional appearance can enhance perceived competence and trust — an important practical message for practitioners, though preferences vary by patient group and setting.

Verbal communication and medical jargon — McKinlay (1975)

  • Verbal communication is the actual words used. A recurring problem is JARGON — technical medical terms (e.g. 'oedema', 'umbilicus', 'breech') that doctors understand but patients often do not.
  • McKinlay studied communication on a maternity ward. He gave staff (doctors) a list of common technical terms and asked which words they thought their patients would understand; he also tested what the patients (working-class new mothers) actually understood.
  • Results: doctors consistently UNDERESTIMATED patients' vocabulary in some ways yet still USED words the patients did not understand. Crucially, doctors often used jargon even when they knew or suspected patients would not grasp it.
  • Interpretation: McKinlay argued jargon is not just accidental. It can function to maintain the doctor's POWER/STATUS, save time, and keep social distance — it is partly about control, not only about being misunderstood. This links communication to the wider doctor-patient power relationship.

Key terms

  • Non-verbal communication: transmitting messages without words, via body language, appearance, tone and touch.
  • Jargon: specialist technical language used within a profession, often not understood by outsiders (patients).
  • Satisfaction: the patient's evaluation of how good the consultation and care were.
  • Recall: how much of the practitioner's information the patient can remember afterwards.
  • Adherence (compliance): the extent to which a patient follows medical advice/treatment.

Evaluating McKinstry & Wang (1991)

  • Strength — high control: using photographs of the SAME doctors in different clothes isolates the clothing variable, so differences in confidence can be attributed to appearance rather than to the individual doctor. This gives good internal validity.
  • Weakness — low ecological validity: rating a photo is not the same as a real consultation, where the doctor's manner, listening and skill quickly matter more than their clothes. The study may overstate how much dress alone counts in practice.
  • Application: supports dress codes and 'smart' uniforms in clinics, but findings are culturally and historically specific — the white coat is now sometimes avoided (infection control, 'white-coat hypertension').

Evaluating McKinlay (1975)

  • Strength — real setting (a maternity ward) gives high ecological validity; it studies genuine communication failures with real consequences for care.
  • Weakness — the sample (working-class maternity patients) limits generalisability, and 'understanding' is hard to measure precisely. The power-play interpretation is inferential rather than directly proven.
  • Its value is conceptual: it reframes jargon from an innocent habit into a possible tool of professional dominance, a useful evaluation point in essays on the doctor-patient relationship.

Research methods

  • McKinstry & Wang used an experimental/survey design with photographs (a form of controlled snapshot), allowing quantitative comparison of confidence ratings but at the cost of realism.
  • McKinlay used a more naturalistic, quasi-experimental comparison of doctors' predictions versus patients' actual comprehension, higher realism but weaker control.
  • Both rely partly on SELF-REPORT (rated confidence, reported understanding), which can be affected by social desirability and demand characteristics.

Going deeper / exam use

  • Tie NVC and verbal skills to OUTCOMES: warmth + plain language → more satisfaction, better recall, better adherence; jargon + cold NVC → the opposite.
  • Use the individual vs situational debate: is good communication a stable trait of some doctors (individual) or a product of training, time pressure and setting (situational)? Evidence that skills can be trained supports the situational side.
  • A strong answer contrasts the two studies: one on appearance (NVC), one on words (verbal), showing you understand both channels of interpersonal skill.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Individual vs situational: are poor communicators simply 'that kind of doctor', or does a rushed, high-status hospital environment push even good doctors toward jargon and poor NVC? Training studies suggest situation and skills matter, so behaviour can change.
  • Application to everyday life: findings justify communication-skills training in medical education and simple practices like plain-language leaflets and warm NVC to boost adherence.
  • Usefulness of research: McKinlay's insight into jargon-as-power is practically useful for reforming consultations, but the effect sizes and cultural specificity of dress studies mean advice must be applied cautiously.
  • Ethnocentrism/culture: preferences for formal dress and norms around eye contact and touch vary by culture, so guidance from UK/US studies may not transfer globally.

Patient-practitioner: diagnosis & style

Making a diagnosis

  • Diagnosis is the process of identifying a patient's condition from symptoms, signs and tests. It relies on DISCLOSURE — the patient telling the practitioner what is wrong — so poor communication or embarrassment (e.g. sexual, mental-health symptoms) leads to missing information and mis-diagnosis.
  • A diagnostic test can produce a FALSE POSITIVE (saying a healthy person has the disease) or a FALSE NEGATIVE (missing a disease that is present). Both have costs and doctors must balance them.
  • Cost of a false positive: unnecessary worry, further invasive tests, possibly harmful treatment, and wasted resources. Cost of a false negative: a real illness goes untreated and can worsen or become fatal — usually the more serious error, so screening tends to err toward over-detection.

Presenting a diagnosis

  • HOW a diagnosis is delivered affects how the patient copes and adheres. It should be clear (little jargon), honest, paced, and paired with a plan, so the patient understands what it means and what to do.
  • Bad news needs sensitivity and support; a blunt or vague delivery can cause distress, denial, or non-adherence. Checking understanding (asking the patient to repeat it back) improves recall.

Practitioner style: doctor-centred vs patient-centred

  • DOCTOR-CENTRED (directing) style: the practitioner controls the consultation, asks closed questions, focuses on the physical problem, and tells the patient what to do. It is efficient and authoritative but can leave patients feeling unheard.
  • PATIENT-CENTRED (sharing) style: the practitioner invites the patient's views, uses open questions, discusses options and shares decisions. It respects autonomy and can improve understanding and long-term engagement.
  • Neither is 'best' for everything: acute, simple physical problems may suit a directing style; chronic conditions and lifestyle change often suit a sharing style. The right style can depend on the patient and the problem.

Key terms

  • Disclosure: the patient revealing symptoms/information the doctor needs to diagnose.
  • False positive: test wrongly indicates disease in a healthy person.
  • False negative: test wrongly indicates health in a diseased person.
  • Doctor-centred (directing) style: doctor-led, closed questions, instructions given.
  • Patient-centred (sharing) style: collaborative, open questions, shared decision-making.
⭐ Key study — Savage & Armstrong (1990) — effect of a GP's consulting style on patient satisfaction

Aim: To compare the effect of a DIRECTING (doctor-centred) versus a SHARING (patient-centred) consulting style on patients' satisfaction with the consultation.

Method: Design: a controlled field experiment (randomised) in an inner-London general practice. Sample: 359 patients attending one GP, randomly allocated to a directing or sharing style for the treatment/advice/prognosis part of the consultation. Procedure: in the DIRECTING condition the GP made firm statements, e.g. 'You are suffering from...', 'It is essential you take this treatment', giving a clear diagnosis and instructions. In the SHARING condition he used phrases such as 'What do you think is wrong?' and 'Would you like a prescription?', inviting the patient's views. Patients completed a satisfaction questionnaire immediately after and again about a week later (response rates ~89% immediately and ~58% at follow-up (check book)).

Results: Patients reported GREATER satisfaction after the DIRECTING style — they rated the doctor's understanding of the problem, the explanation given, and their subjective improvement more highly. The effect was strongest for patients with PHYSICAL problems and those who RECEIVED A PRESCRIPTION.

Conclusion: Consulting style influences satisfaction, and — contrary to the assumption that patient-centred is always better — many patients preferred a confident, directing style, especially for physical complaints. Patients often want authoritative reassurance; the 'best' style depends on the patient and the problem.

Evaluating Savage & Armstrong (1990)

  • Strength — real consultations with real patients and random allocation give a strong mix of ecological validity AND experimental control, so the style comparison is credible.

Research methods

  • Independent measures (each patient got one style) avoids order effects but introduces participant variables — mitigated here by random allocation.
  • Satisfaction measured by self-report questionnaire: quantitative and comparable, but vulnerable to social desirability and demand characteristics.

Misusing health services

Two ways services are misused

  • Health services can be misused by UNDER-use (delaying or avoiding needed care) and by OVER-use/false use (seeking care that is not medically needed, as in Munchausen syndrome and malingering). Both waste resources and harm health.
  • Delay is dangerous because early treatment often determines survival (e.g. cancer, heart attack); understanding WHY people delay lets services intervene.

Delay in seeking treatment — Safer et al. (1979)

  • Safer et al. studied patients at a clinic and identified that total delay is made of three separate STAGES, each driven by different factors.
  • APPRAISAL DELAY: the time from first noticing a symptom to deciding you are ill. It is influenced by the symptom itself — bleeding or pain shortens it; a painless lump lengthens it.
  • ILLNESS DELAY: from deciding you are ill to deciding to seek professional help. Influenced by beliefs, imagining the illness, and whether you think a doctor can help.
  • UTILISATION DELAY: from deciding to seek help to actually going/starting treatment. Influenced by practical costs — money, time off work, fear of the visit, whether the benefit seems worth the effort.
  • Splitting delay into stages is useful because interventions can target the RIGHT stage (e.g. public education for appraisal, easier appointments for utilisation).

Health Belief Model as an alternative explanation

  • The HBM explains delay differently: people weigh perceived SUSCEPTIBILITY and SEVERITY of the illness against the BENEFITS of acting minus the BARRIERS, prompted by CUES TO ACTION.
  • So someone delays if they feel invulnerable, think the problem is minor, or perceive high barriers (cost, fear) — a cost-benefit account of why people misuse/underuse services.

Munchausen syndrome (factitious disorder)

  • Munchausen syndrome = a FACTITIOUS disorder: the person deliberately fakes, exaggerates or self-induces illness. The MOTIVATION is internal — to adopt the SICK ROLE and receive attention and care — NOT external reward.
  • Essential features: intentional production of symptoms, and the primary motivation is to be treated as a patient. Supporting features: dramatic/plausible medical history, extensive knowledge of medical terms, willingness to undergo painful tests/surgery, and moving between hospitals ('hospital hopping') when discovered.

Malingering — the key contrast

  • Malingering is ALSO faking illness, but the motivation is EXTERNAL and rational: to gain money, drugs, time off work, avoiding a court case, etc.
  • The crucial exam distinction is MOTIVATION: Munchausen = internal (psychological need to be a patient); malingering = external tangible reward. Malingering is not itself a mental disorder.

Key terms

  • Appraisal / illness / utilisation delay: the three stages of Safer et al.
  • Factitious disorder: deliberately producing symptoms for the internal reward of the sick role.
  • Malingering: faking illness for external gain.
  • Sick role: the socially recognised status/care given to someone who is ill.
  • Cues to action (HBM): triggers (a symptom, a friend's advice) that prompt help-seeking.

Misusing health services — evaluation & recall

Aleem & Ajarim (1995) — case study of Munchausen syndrome

  • Case: a 22-year-old Saudi Arabian female university student presented repeatedly with painful swellings and abscesses of the breast, abdomen, and groin that resisted treatment and recurred.
  • Discovery: a nurse, tipped off by a room-mate, found needles and a syringe containing faecal matter under the bed — the patient had been SELF-INDUCING the infections. Psychiatric assessment found her defensive, rationalising, with superficial affect and evident stress/conflict but no clear depression.
  • Conclusion: this fits Munchausen syndrome — deliberate self-harm to obtain the sick role. Prognosis was judged poor; long-term psychotherapy was suggested with limited expected benefit. Illustrates how difficult factitious disorder is to detect and treat.

Evaluating the case-study method

  • Strengths: rich, detailed data on a rare disorder that could not be studied experimentally; high ecological validity; can generate hypotheses about causes and management.
  • Weaknesses: a single case cannot be generalised; interpretation relies on the clinicians' judgement (researcher bias); it is not replicable, and causes remain inferred rather than tested.

Evaluating Safer et al. and the HBM

  • Safer's three-stage model is USEFUL and practical, letting services target the specific reason for delay, but it relies on retrospective self-report of when decisions were made, which is imprecise.
  • The HBM adds explanatory power (belief-based) and predicts individual differences, but it downplays emotion (fear, denial) and social/habit factors, and being descriptive it can be hard to falsify.

Research methods & going deeper

  • Contrast methods: Safer used a structured survey/quantitative approach across many patients; Aleem & Ajarim used a qualitative single case — breadth vs depth.
  • Exam use: use Munchausen vs malingering to demonstrate you can compare on MOTIVATION; use Safer's stages to structure an answer on 'why people delay'; offer HBM as an 'alternative explanation' for extra evaluation marks.
  • Individual vs situational: delay reflects both individual beliefs (HBM) and situational barriers (cost, access in utilisation delay).
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Application to everyday life: Safer's stages guide targeted campaigns (e.g. 'check for lumps' tackles appraisal delay; walk-in clinics tackle utilisation delay).
  • Nature vs nurture / individual differences: why one person develops factitious disorder while another malingers points to differing psychological needs versus rational choice.
  • Usefulness of case studies: Aleem & Ajarim is rich but ungeneralisable — good for insight, weak for prediction.
  • Reductionism vs holism: HBM reduces help-seeking to a cost-benefit sum, arguably ignoring emotion, culture and habit.

Adherence: types & reasons for non-adherence

What adherence means and its types

  • Adherence (compliance) = the extent to which a patient's behaviour matches medical advice. NON-adherence takes several forms: not taking medication (or wrong dose/timing), stopping early, not following lifestyle advice, and MISSING APPOINTMENTS.
  • A key distinction is UNINTENTIONAL vs INTENTIONAL non-adherence. Unintentional = the patient wants to comply but forgets, misunderstands instructions, or cannot afford/access the treatment. Intentional = the patient makes a deliberate decision not to follow advice (e.g. dislikes side-effects, disbelieves the diagnosis).

Problems caused by non-adherence

  • RELAPSE: the illness returns or worsens because treatment was incomplete or stopped (e.g. stopping antibiotics early).
  • RESISTANCE: partial or inconsistent use of antibiotics helps bacteria evolve DRUG-RESISTANT strains, a serious public-health harm.
  • WASTE: prescribed but unused medicine, extra consultations, and hospital admissions from preventable deterioration waste large sums of money.
  • These make non-adherence one of the biggest, most expensive problems in healthcare.

Rational non-adherence — Laba et al. (2012)

  • Rational non-adherence is the idea that patients weigh the COSTS and BENEFITS of taking medicine and may sensibly decide NOT to — it is a reasoned choice, not just forgetfulness.
  • Laba et al. (2012) used a DISCRETE-CHOICE EXPERIMENT: participants were repeatedly asked to choose between hypothetical medicines that differed on features such as cost, dosing frequency, side-effects, symptom severity, and how much the drug helped.
  • By seeing which features made people MORE or LESS willing to take a medicine, the researchers quantified the trade-offs. Cost, side-effects, treatment inconvenience (frequent dosing), and beliefs about how much good the medicine does all significantly influenced the willingness to adhere.
  • Conclusion: non-adherence is often a rational, economic decision, so improving it means reducing perceived costs/barriers (cheaper, simpler, fewer side-effects) rather than just 'reminding' patients.

Health Belief Model (HBM) — six components

  • The HBM explains WHY people do or don't follow health advice through beliefs. Perceived SUSCEPTIBILITY: how likely I think I am to get/worsen the illness. Perceived SEVERITY: how serious I think it would be.
  • Perceived BENEFITS: how effective I think the treatment/action is. Perceived BARRIERS: the costs, effort, side-effects or inconvenience of acting.
  • CUES TO ACTION: internal or external triggers (a symptom flaring, a leaflet, a friend's illness) that prompt behaviour. SELF-EFFICACY: my confidence that I can actually carry out the behaviour.
  • Adherence is most likely when susceptibility + severity + benefits + self-efficacy are HIGH and barriers are LOW, and a cue triggers action. This overlaps with rational non-adherence (benefits vs barriers = a cost-benefit judgement).

Key terms

  • Adherence/compliance: following medical advice.
  • Intentional vs unintentional non-adherence: deliberate choice vs forgetting/misunderstanding.
  • Rational non-adherence: a reasoned cost-benefit decision not to follow advice.
  • Discrete-choice experiment: method offering trade-off choices to reveal what people value.
  • Self-efficacy: belief in one's own ability to perform the behaviour.

Adherence: types & reasons for non-adherence — evaluation & recall

Evaluating rational non-adherence / Laba et al.

  • Strength — reframes patients as active decision-makers, not passive/forgetful, which is more respectful and points to practical fixes (reduce cost, side-effects, dosing frequency).
  • Strength — the discrete-choice method is systematic and quantifiable, isolating the influence of each medicine feature.
  • Weakness — choices are HYPOTHETICAL; what people say they would do may differ from real behaviour when actually ill (low ecological validity), and the design assumes people behave rationally, underplaying emotion, habit and denial.

Evaluating the Health Belief Model

  • Strengths — comprehensive, widely applicable, and predictive; it identifies specific beliefs interventions can target (e.g. raise perceived susceptibility).
  • Weaknesses — it is largely correlational and hard to falsify; it ignores emotional and social factors and habits; the components are hard to measure precisely and don't specify how they combine.
  • Compared with rational non-adherence, the HBM is broader (adds susceptibility/severity/cues) while Laba's model focuses tightly on the cost-benefit trade-off; they are complementary.

Research methods

  • Discrete-choice experiments generate quantitative, comparable data and can put a 'value' on trade-offs, but rely on stated (not revealed) preferences.
  • HBM research typically uses questionnaires/correlational designs — good for identifying belief-behaviour links but cannot establish cause.
  • Both approaches depend on self-report, sharing its validity limits.

Going deeper / exam use

  • Structure a 'reasons for non-adherence' answer as: types (intentional/unintentional) → problems (relapse, resistance, waste) → explanations (rational non-adherence + HBM).
  • Use the overlap: benefits-minus-barriers in the HBM IS a cost-benefit judgement, linking the two models.
  • Reductionism vs holism: both models risk reducing a complex behaviour to a tidy calculation.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Free will vs determinism: rational non-adherence assumes patients freely choose; the HBM implies behaviour is determined by prior beliefs — useful to contrast.
  • Application: interventions should lower barriers (cheaper/simpler regimens) and raise perceived benefit and self-efficacy to improve adherence.
  • Individual vs situational: beliefs (individual) versus real costs like price and access (situational) both drive non-adherence.
  • Usefulness: understanding rational non-adherence prevents blaming patients and redirects effort to system-level fixes.

Adherence: measuring non-adherence

Why measuring adherence is hard

  • To improve adherence we must first MEASURE it, but every method has flaws, so researchers use three broad approaches: SUBJECTIVE (self-report), OBJECTIVE (counting/tracking), and BIOLOGICAL (body samples).
  • A central problem is that patients OVER-ESTIMATE their own adherence, so measures relying on the patient tend to be too optimistic.

Subjective measures — self-report / interviews

  • Subjective methods ask the patient (or carer/doctor) how well they followed treatment, via interviews or questionnaires. They are cheap, quick, and can explore REASONS for non-adherence.
  • Why they OVER-estimate: SOCIAL DESIRABILITY (patients don't want to admit disobeying the doctor), faulty MEMORY (forgetting missed doses), and simply not knowing they were non-adherent (unintentional). So self-report sets an upper, unreliable estimate.
  • Riekert & Drotar (1999) illustrate a related problem: those who AGREE to take part in adherence studies (and complete interviews) may differ from those who refuse — non-consenters/dropouts are often the LESS adherent, so samples are biased toward more adherent patients, again inflating apparent adherence.

Objective measures — pill counting & electronic monitors

  • PILL COUNTING: count the tablets left in the bottle versus how many should remain. It seems concrete but is easily fooled — patients can 'dump' pills to look adherent, and it shows quantity, not whether pills were taken at the right TIME.
  • MEDICATION DISPENSERS / MEMS (Medication Event Monitoring System): a microchip in the bottle cap records the date and time each time the bottle is OPENED, giving a detailed pattern of use over time.
  • Chung & Naya (2000) used a MEMS-type electronic device on an asthma medication (a tablet, so it could be electronically tracked) and found adherence was HIGH overall but declined over the study period, and electronic records revealed patterns (e.g. dose timing) that self-report or pill counts would miss. This shows objective monitoring gives richer, more honest data.
  • Limitation: opening the bottle is not proof of SWALLOWING the dose, and knowing you are monitored can itself change behaviour (reactivity).

Biological measures — blood & urine

  • Biological methods test for the drug (or a marker/metabolite) in BLOOD or URINE, giving fairly direct evidence that the medicine was actually taken.
  • Strengths: objective and hard to fake in the moment. Weaknesses: they are invasive, costly, only show RECENT doses (not the long-term pattern), and can be affected by individual differences in metabolism — a patient could take the drug yet show low levels, or take it only just before the test ('white-coat adherence').

Key terms

  • Subjective/self-report measure: patient's own account of adherence.
  • Social desirability: answering to look good, biasing self-report upward.
  • Pill count: counting remaining medication to infer adherence.
  • MEMS: electronic bottle cap logging each opening with a timestamp.
  • Biological measure: assay of blood/urine for the drug or its metabolite.

Adherence: measuring non-adherence — evaluation & recall

Comparing the three approaches

  • Validity ranking (roughly): biological/electronic > pill count > self-report, because each removes more of the patient's opportunity to over-report. But higher validity usually costs more money, effort, and invasiveness.
  • Self-report is best for understanding WHY (reasons, beliefs); objective/biological methods are best for HOW MUCH. Good research often COMBINES them (triangulation).

Evaluating Riekert & Drotar (1999)

  • Key methodological lesson: consent/participation bias. If less-adherent patients are more likely to refuse or drop out, the remaining sample looks artificially adherent, threatening the validity of ANY adherence study.
  • This is a strong evaluation point to deploy generally: even a good measure gives biased results if the SAMPLE is self-selected toward compliant patients.

Evaluating Chung & Naya (2000) / MEMS

  • Strength — electronic monitoring gives objective, time-stamped, longitudinal data revealing patterns (declining adherence, dose timing) invisible to self-report or pill counts.
  • Weakness — records openings, not ingestion; the device may cause reactivity; and equipment is expensive, limiting sample sizes and everyday use.

Research methods & going deeper

  • Reliability vs validity: pill counts can be RELIABLE (consistent) yet not VALID (fooled by dumping). Distinguish the two in evaluation.
  • Reactivity/demand characteristics affect objective and biological measures too (being watched changes behaviour), so no method is perfectly 'objective'.
  • Exam use: for 'measuring adherence', give the three categories with an example study each, explain WHY self-report over-estimates, then argue for combining methods.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Application: choose the method to fit the goal — MEMS/biological for trials needing accuracy, interviews for understanding barriers in a clinic.
  • Usefulness of research: accurate measurement is the foundation for evaluating interventions; over-reliance on self-report can make useless treatments look effective.
  • Reductionism vs holism: biological measures reduce adherence to a chemical level, ignoring the reasons captured by interviews.
  • Ethics: covert or intrusive monitoring (MEMS, urine tests) raises consent and privacy concerns and can undermine trust.

Adherence: improving adherence

Three levels of improving adherence

  • Interventions to raise adherence work at three levels: making treatment engaging for CHILDREN, INDIVIDUAL behavioural techniques for one patient, and COMMUNITY-level programmes reaching whole populations.
  • Most techniques share a principle from learning theory: use POSITIVE REINFORCEMENT and REMINDERS to make the healthy behaviour easier and more rewarding than not doing it.

Improving adherence in children — Chaney et al. (2004): the Funhaler

  • Problem: young asthmatic children often resist their inhaler/spacer, so parents struggle to medicate them and adherence is poor.
  • Solution: the FUNHALER, a redesigned spacer with incentive TOYS — a spinning disc and a whistle that work ONLY when the child breathes correctly (deep, sustained breaths). Correct technique is therefore rewarded with play (positive reinforcement), turning treatment into a game.
  • Method: 32 children (aged ~1.5–6) used the Funhaler for 2 weeks; parents were interviewed before (using their existing device) and after (using the Funhaler) — a self-report comparison.
  • Results: with the Funhaler, far more parents reported successfully medicating their child — about 22 of 30 were 'always' successful, versus only 3 of 30 with the existing device (check book); children also took more effective breaths and technique improved.
  • Conclusion: building POSITIVE REINFORCEMENT into the device improves adherence in young children, showing behaviourist principles can be engineered into treatment.

Individual behavioural techniques

  • BEHAVIOURAL CONTRACTS: a written agreement between patient and practitioner setting goals and rewards, increasing commitment and making expectations explicit.
  • PROMPTS/REMINDERS: cues such as text reminders, phone calls, pill organisers with days marked, or alarm caps that tackle UNINTENTIONAL non-adherence (forgetting).
  • TAILORING: fitting the regimen to the patient's daily routine (e.g. taking a pill with breakfast) so the behaviour attaches to an existing habit, lowering the barrier of remembering.

Community interventions

  • Community-level programmes target many people at once: public campaigns, easier access (more/closer clinics, extended hours), and combining REMINDERS with INCENTIVES for whole populations (e.g. immunisation drives).
  • They can be cost-effective per person and reduce the situational BARRIERS (access, cost, awareness) that cause non-adherence, complementing individual techniques.

Key terms

  • Positive reinforcement: rewarding a behaviour so it is repeated.
  • Behavioural contract: written patient-practitioner agreement on goals/rewards.
  • Prompt/cue: a reminder that triggers the behaviour, addressing forgetting.
  • Tailoring: adapting the regimen to the individual's routine.
  • Community intervention: population-level programme (campaigns, access, incentives).
⭐ Key study — Yokley & Glenwick (1984) — increasing preschool immunisation via prompts and incentives

Aim: To compare several community interventions to find which most effectively increases attendance for preschool children's immunisations.

Method: Design: field experiment. Sample: families of 1,133 immunisation-deficient preschool children in a US public-health setting, randomly assigned to SIX conditions: (1) a GENERAL prompt (a general reminder to the community/parents); (2) a CLIENT-SPECIFIC prompt (a personalised reminder naming the child and clinic details); (3) specific prompt PLUS increased clinic ACCESS (extra clinic hours, e.g. evenings/Saturdays); (4) specific prompt PLUS a MONETARY INCENTIVE (a lottery/reward for attending); (5) a CONTACT control (contacted but no intervention); and (6) a NO-CONTACT control. Procedure: prompts were sent, access/incentives applied, and clinic attendance for immunisation was recorded and compared across conditions.

Results: All conditions EXCEPT the general prompt increased immunisation attendance compared with controls. The MONETARY INCENTIVE (with specific prompt) produced the LARGEST effect, followed by increased access, then the specific prompt, then the general prompt. However, the SPECIFIC PROMPT ALONE was the most COST-EFFECTIVE (cheap yet effective per child).

Conclusion: Community interventions using client-specific prompts, easier access and incentives all raise immunisation uptake; incentives are most powerful but specific prompts give the best value for money. Well-designed reminders and incentives are practical tools for improving population-level adherence.

Adherence: improving adherence — evaluation & recall

Evaluating Yokley & Glenwick (1984)

  • Strengths — large sample (1,133), random allocation to six conditions and a real public-health setting give strong control AND ecological validity; the multiple conditions let cost-effectiveness be compared, giving direct policy guidance.
  • Weaknesses — attendance measures behaviour but not whether interventions changed BELIEFS; effects may be short-term; and results (US, 1980s) may not generalise to other health systems or cultures.

  • Strengths — clever application of positive reinforcement with a large practical effect; addresses a real, common problem in young children.

Pain: types & theories

Functions of pain

  • Pain is unpleasant but ADAPTIVE. Its functions: it WARNS of tissue damage (triggering withdrawal, e.g. pulling from a flame), it PROMOTES healing by making us rest/protect the injured part, and it teaches us to AVOID danger in future (learning).
  • Evidence for its importance: people with congenital insensitivity to pain suffer repeated injuries and often die young, showing pain is protective, not merely a nuisance.

Acute vs chronic pain

  • ACUTE pain is short-term (under ~3–6 months), has a clear cause (injury, surgery), and resolves as the tissue heals. It is the 'useful warning' type.
  • CHRONIC pain persists beyond normal healing time (months or years), sometimes with no clear ongoing physical cause. It is often no longer protective and can be disabling, linked to depression and reduced quality of life — so it is treated as a problem in its own right.
  • This distinction matters because chronic pain needs different management (psychological approaches, gate-control-based methods) than acute pain (analgesia while healing).

Phantom limb pain and the mirror box

  • PHANTOM LIMB PAIN is pain FELT in a limb that has been amputated (or is paralysed) — the limb is gone but the brain still generates pain 'from' it. It challenges simple theories that pain requires tissue damage at the site.
  • MIRROR-BOX TREATMENT (developed by Ramachandran): a mirror is placed so the reflection of the INTACT limb appears where the missing limb would be. When the person moves the real limb, the brain 'sees' the phantom limb moving and unclenching, which can relieve the pain — visual feedback reorganises the brain's body map.
  • MacLachlan et al. (2004) reported a CASE STUDY of a man with phantom limb pain after leg amputation who used mirror treatment; over weeks his phantom pain and cramping sensations reduced, supporting mirror therapy as a low-cost, non-drug option (a single case, so cautious generalisation).

Specificity theory (von Frey)

  • Von Frey's SPECIFICITY THEORY is the older, simple view: the body has specific pain RECEPTORS that, when stimulated, send a signal along dedicated pain nerves DIRECTLY to a 'pain centre' in the brain. Pain intensity = amount of tissue damage.
  • It is like a 'bell wire': damage rings the bell in the brain, one-to-one. This explains basic acute pain but CANNOT explain phantom limb pain (no receptors left), why pain varies with mood/attention, or why the same injury hurts differently on different occasions.

Key terms

  • Acute pain: short-term pain with a clear cause that resolves with healing.
  • Chronic pain: pain persisting beyond normal healing, often without clear ongoing damage.
  • Phantom limb pain: pain felt in an absent/amputated limb.
  • Specificity theory: pain as a direct one-to-one signal from receptor to brain (von Frey).
  • Gate control: the theory that a spinal 'gate' modulates pain signals up or down.

Pain: types & theories — evaluation & recall

Gate control theory (Melzack & Wall, 1965)

  • Proposes a neural 'GATE' in the dorsal horn of the SPINAL CORD that controls how much pain signal reaches the brain — pain is MODULATED, not a fixed one-to-one signal.
  • Fibre balance: SMALL (nociceptive) fibres carry pain and tend to OPEN the gate; LARGE (touch/pressure) fibres tend to CLOSE it. This is why rubbing a knock, or using a TENS machine, reduces pain — large-fibre activity shuts the gate on the small-fibre pain signal.
  • DESCENDING CONTROL: signals from the BRAIN (thoughts, emotion, attention, expectation) can open or close the gate. Anxiety, focusing on pain, and low mood OPEN it (more pain); distraction, relaxation, and positive mood CLOSE it (less pain). This explains why soldiers may not feel wounds until safe, and why psychological therapy helps chronic pain.
  • Gate control succeeds where specificity fails: it explains phantom limb pain (the brain/central mechanism generates pain), psychological influences, and pain without injury.

Comparing the two theories

  • Specificity theory: simple, intuitive, and correct that specialised receptors exist, but too rigid — it cannot account for the many top-down and situational influences on pain.
  • Gate control: more complete and clinically fruitful (inspires TENS, distraction, CBT for pain), and it integrates physiology WITH psychology. Weakness: the exact 'gate' mechanism is hard to pinpoint anatomically and the theory is complex/harder to test directly.
  • Overall gate control is now the dominant model; use specificity as the contrast it improved upon.

Research methods

  • Phantom limb/mirror-box evidence is largely CASE STUDY based (e.g. MacLachlan et al. 2004): rich and clinically useful but ungeneralisable and open to placebo/expectation effects.
  • Gate control is supported by physiological and experimental evidence (fibre stimulation, TENS trials) plus everyday observation (rubbing pain away), triangulating lab and real-world data.

Going deeper / exam use

  • Structure a 'theories of pain' answer as: specificity (simple, receptor-to-brain) → its failures (phantom pain, psychological effects) → gate control (small/large fibres + descending control) → how it fixes those failures.
  • Use phantom limb pain as the killer evidence AGAINST specificity and FOR a central/gate mechanism.
  • Application: gate control justifies non-drug pain management — TENS (large fibres), distraction and relaxation (descending control) — a strong 'usefulness' point.
🧩 Issues & debates
  • Reductionism vs holism: specificity theory is reductionist (pain = tissue damage → signal); gate control is more holistic, integrating physical, emotional and cognitive factors.
  • Application to everyday life: gate control explains and justifies TENS, massage/rubbing, distraction, and psychological therapy for chronic pain.
  • Nature vs nurture / individual differences: why the same injury hurts people differently links to mood, attention and expectation acting via descending control.
  • Usefulness of case studies: mirror-box cases (MacLachlan et al.) are clinically inspiring but need controlled trials before firm conclusions.

Pain: measuring pain

Why measuring pain is difficult

  • Pain is a subjective, private experience: it cannot be seen directly or measured with a physical instrument the way blood pressure can, so psychologists must rely on indirect measures.
  • There is no perfect gold-standard measure, so clinicians combine several methods (self-report, observation, and for children, faces scales) to build a fuller picture.
  • Measures fall into three broad types: self-report (the person describes their own pain), behavioural/observational (someone watches what the person does), and physiological (rarely used clinically for pain).
  • Good measurement matters because under-treatment of pain is common, especially in groups who cannot articulate their pain well such as young children.

Self-report: clinical interview

  • A clinician asks open and closed questions to explore the location, intensity, quality (e.g. burning, stabbing), timing and triggers of pain, plus how it affects daily life and mood.
  • Strength: it is flexible and rich, letting the doctor follow up unexpected answers and build rapport, which can reveal psychological factors behind the pain.
  • Weakness: it is subjective, time-consuming, and vulnerable to social desirability (patients may downplay pain to seem brave) and to interviewer bias.

McGill Pain Questionnaire (MPQ)

  • Developed by Melzack, it measures the quality as well as the intensity of pain using lists of describing words (adjectives) grouped into categories.
  • It captures three dimensions: SENSORY (the physical quality, e.g. throbbing, shooting, burning), AFFECTIVE (the emotional impact, e.g. tiring, sickening, fearful) and EVALUATIVE (an overall judgement of intensity, e.g. annoying, unbearable).
  • Patients choose the words that fit their pain; scores such as the Pain Rating Index and the Present Pain Intensity are calculated, giving a numerical summary of a qualitative experience.
  • Strength: it recognises pain is multidimensional, not just 'how much', which helps distinguish different pain types (e.g. nerve pain vs muscle pain). Weakness: the vocabulary is complex, so it can be hard for children, people with low literacy, or non-native speakers.

Visual Analogue Scale (VAS)

  • A simple 10 cm horizontal line labelled 'no pain' at one end and 'worst pain imaginable' at the other; the patient marks a point and the distance is measured in mm/cm.
  • Strength: quick, easy, sensitive to small changes, and produces interval-level data useful for tracking whether treatment is working over time.
  • Weakness: it reduces a complex experience to a single number, ignoring the quality of pain, and some patients (young children, older adults) find the abstract line hard to use.

Behavioural measure: UAB Pain Behaviour Scale

  • The University of Alabama at Birmingham scale is an observational tool where a trained observer rates ten pain behaviours (e.g. verbal complaints, facial grimacing, standing posture, mobility, use of medication, limb guarding).
  • Each behaviour is scored 0 (none), 0.5 (occasional) or 1 (frequent), giving a total that reflects observable pain-related behaviour rather than what the patient says.
  • Strength: useful for patients who cannot self-report reliably and it is quick and objective. Weakness: it measures behaviour, not the felt experience, and patients may exaggerate or suppress behaviours when watched (observer effects).

Measuring pain in children: faces scales

  • Faces scales (e.g. Wong-Baker FACES) show a row of cartoon faces ranging from happy/no pain to crying/worst pain; the child points to the face matching their feeling.
  • They are age-appropriate because young children lack the vocabulary and numeracy for the MPQ or VAS but can match a picture to their state.
  • Strength: simple, non-verbal, works across languages. Weakness: children may pick the face showing their emotion (fear, sadness) rather than their pain level, confounding the two.

Key terms

  • Self-report: a measure in which the individual describes their own experience.
  • Multidimensional pain: the idea that pain has sensory, affective and evaluative aspects.
  • Behavioural/observational measure: assessing pain by watching what a person does.
  • Interval data: numerical data with equal intervals, allowing tracking of change (e.g. VAS).
  • Faces scale: a pictorial self-report scale using facial expressions, designed for children.
⭐ Key study — Brudvik et al. (2016) — A comparison of pain assessment by physicians, parents and children

Aim: To compare how children, their parents and physicians assess the child's pain, and to examine how these assessments affected whether physicians gave pain relief.

Method: Cross-sectional (correlational comparison) study at Bergen Accident and Emergency Department, Norway. Sample: 243 children aged 3-15 years attending as outpatients with various conditions (e.g. fractures, wounds, infections). Procedure: pain was rated on the 0-10 Numerical Rating Scale (NRS); younger children used a faces-based version. The same episode of pain was rated independently by the child (patient), a parent and the treating physician, and the researchers recorded whether painkillers were given.

Results: Physicians rated pain lowest (mean NRS 3.2), parents higher (mean 4.8) and children highest (mean 5.5). Agreement was moderate between child and parent (weighted kappa 0.55) but low between child and physician (0.12) and parent and physician (0.17). Physicians gave painkillers to only 4.1% of children with mild pain, 9.0% with moderate pain and 42.1% with severe pain.

Conclusion: Physicians systematically underestimate children's pain compared with the children themselves and their parents, and this leads to under-treatment. Children's own self-reports (and parents' views) should be given greater weight when deciding on pain relief.

Pain: measuring pain — evaluation & recall

Evaluation: strengths

  • Using multiple measures (self-report, MPQ, VAS, behavioural, faces) allows triangulation, improving validity because weaknesses of one method are offset by another.
  • Standardised tools like the MPQ and VAS are reliable and produce quantitative data, so treatment effects can be compared objectively across time and patients.
  • Faces scales and behavioural scales extend measurement to groups (young children, non-verbal patients) who would otherwise be ignored, improving fairness and reducing under-treatment.

Evaluation: weaknesses

  • All self-report is subjective and open to demand characteristics and social desirability; patients may exaggerate to get medication or minimise to appear stoic.
  • Reducing pain to a single number (VAS) loses the qualitative richness of the experience, while the MPQ's complex vocabulary lowers its accessibility.
  • Cultural and individual differences in how people express pain threaten validity: the same score may mean different things to different people.

Research methods

  • Brudvik used a cross-sectional correlational design comparing raters; it cannot establish cause, only association between rater type and score.
  • Weighted kappa is a statistic measuring level of agreement between raters (inter-rater reliability) beyond chance; low kappa shows physicians and children disagree.
  • The NRS and VAS give quantitative interval-type data suited to statistical comparison; the clinical interview and MPQ generate qualitative and quantitative data respectively.
  • A key issue is validity: does the measure capture true felt pain? Behavioural measures may lack validity because behaviour and experience can diverge.

  • Use Brudvik as evidence for why self-report should be prioritised and how measurement failures cause real harm (under-medication), linking to the ethics of care.
  • Contrast objective (behavioural, UAB) versus subjective (self-report) measures in essays: objective measures reduce bias but may lack validity for the private experience of pain.

  • Objective vs subjective data: behavioural scales (UAB) offer observer objectivity but self-report captures the true subjective experience — Brudvik shows the two can conflict.
  • Individual and cultural differences: pain expression varies by age, gender and culture, so a single standardised scale may not be valid for everyone.
  • Usefulness/application: accurate measurement directly improves treatment decisions, as Brudvik's under-medication findings demonstrate.
  • Reductionism vs holism: the VAS reduces pain to one number (reductionist) whereas the MPQ takes a more holistic multidimensional view.

Pain: managing and controlling pain

Overview of pain management approaches

  • Pain can be managed biologically/biochemically (drugs that block pain chemically or neurally), psychologically (changing thoughts and attention) or through alternative/physical techniques (acupuncture, TENS).
  • The choice depends on the type, severity and cause of pain; chronic pain often needs a combination because no single method fully removes it.
  • Understanding the Gate Control Theory of pain (Melzack & Wall) underpins several methods: a neural 'gate' in the spinal cord can be opened or closed, so non-painful input and psychological factors can reduce the pain signal reaching the brain.

Biological/biochemical: non-opioid analgesics and NSAIDs

  • Non-opioid analgesics such as paracetamol and aspirin, and NSAIDs (Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs) like ibuprofen, are used for mild to moderate pain.
  • NSAIDs work peripherally by blocking the enzyme cyclo-oxygenase (COX), reducing production of prostaglandins — chemicals released by damaged tissue that sensitise nerve endings and cause inflammation and pain.
  • Strength: cheap, widely available, effective for everyday and inflammatory pain, and non-addictive. Weakness: side effects such as stomach irritation, ulcers and bleeding with long-term NSAID use, and a ceiling to how much pain they can control.

Biological/biochemical: opioids

  • Opioids (e.g. morphine, codeine) are used for severe pain (surgery, cancer). They act centrally by binding to opioid receptors in the brain and spinal cord, mimicking the body's natural endorphins and blocking transmission of pain signals.
  • They also alter the emotional response to pain, so the patient may still feel it but 'mind it less'.
  • Strength: very powerful, effective when other drugs fail. Weakness: tolerance (needing more for the same effect), dependence/addiction, and dangerous side effects including constipation and respiratory depression.

Biological/biochemical: local anaesthetics

  • Local anaesthetics (e.g. lidocaine) are injected or applied near a specific site and block sodium channels in nerve membranes, preventing the nerve from firing so the pain signal never leaves the area.
  • They numb a targeted region (e.g. at the dentist) while the patient stays conscious.
  • Strength: fast, targeted, avoids the systemic side effects of general drugs. Weakness: temporary, only covers a local area, and risks nerve damage or allergic reaction.

Psychological: cognitive strategies

  • ATTENTION DIVERSION means focusing attention away from the pain onto something else (e.g. counting ceiling tiles, playing a game, focusing on breathing); because attention is limited, less is available to process pain.
  • NON-PAIN IMAGERY means creating a vivid, pleasant mental image (e.g. lying on a warm beach) that is incompatible with the pain and absorbs attention.
  • COGNITIVE REDEFINITION (reappraisal) means changing how one interprets the pain, e.g. telling oneself 'this ache means the muscle is healing' rather than 'this is unbearable damage', which lowers the emotional (affective) component.
  • Strength: no drugs, no side effects, gives the patient a sense of control. Weakness: less effective for intense acute pain, requires effort/practice, and individual differences in imagination affect success.

Alternative: acupuncture and TENS

  • ACUPUNCTURE involves inserting fine needles at specific body points; it is thought to stimulate nerves that close the pain 'gate' and trigger release of endorphins, the body's natural painkillers.
  • TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation) delivers a mild electrical current through skin electrodes near the pain. Its rationale is Gate Control Theory: the electrical stimulation activates large, fast non-pain nerve fibres that 'close the gate' in the spinal cord, blocking the slower pain signals.
  • Strength: non-invasive/drug-free, patient-controlled (TENS), few side effects. Weakness: effects can be short-lived, evidence is mixed, and some benefit may be placebo.

Key terms

  • Analgesic: a pain-relieving drug.
  • Prostaglandins: chemicals from damaged tissue that sensitise nerves; blocked by NSAIDs.
  • Endorphins: the body's natural opioid painkillers.
  • Gate Control Theory: the idea that a spinal 'gate' can open or close to let pain signals through, influenced by other nerve input and psychological state.
  • TENS: transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation, a gate-closing pain therapy.

Pain: managing and controlling pain — evaluation & recall

Evaluation: biological methods

  • Strength: drugs are fast-acting, effective and well-tested through clinical trials, making them the front line for acute and severe pain.
  • Weakness: they treat symptoms not causes, carry side effects, and opioids risk tolerance and addiction — a major public-health concern.
  • They are a reductionist approach, explaining and treating pain purely at the biochemical/neural level and ignoring psychological and social factors.

Evaluation: psychological and alternative methods

  • Strength: cognitive strategies and TENS are non-invasive, cheap, side-effect-free and give patients control, which itself reduces perceived pain.
  • Weakness: they are usually less powerful than drugs for severe acute pain and their effectiveness varies between individuals; placebo effects make it hard to prove they work.
  • The best outcomes often come from combining methods (e.g. drugs plus attention diversion), supporting a holistic, biopsychosocial model.

Research methods

  • Drug trials use randomised controlled trials with placebo control and often double-blind procedures to remove bias and demonstrate cause and effect.
  • The placebo effect is a major confound in pain research, especially for acupuncture and TENS, so 'sham' acupuncture/placebo TENS conditions are used as controls.
  • Measuring outcomes relies on the pain measures from Day 32 (VAS, MPQ), so validity of results depends on validity of those self-report tools.

  • Use Gate Control Theory as the explanatory backbone linking TENS, acupuncture and even attention diversion (psychological factors can close the gate).

  • Link to debates: drugs = reductionist and treat the individual passively; psychological methods = more holistic and give the patient an active role.

  • Reductionism vs holism: biochemical drugs reduce pain to neural chemistry, whereas combining drugs with cognitive strategies reflects a holistic biopsychosocial view.
  • Free will vs determinism: cognitive strategies give patients active control over their pain, contrasting with the passive drug-taking of the biological approach.
  • Application/usefulness: TENS and cognitive strategies are practical, low-cost, drug-free options especially valuable for chronic pain.

Stress: sources of stress

What is stress?

  • Stress is the response that occurs when the perceived demands on a person exceed their perceived ability to cope; it has physiological (bodily) and psychological components.
  • A stressor is any stimulus (event or situation) that triggers this response. Sources covered here are the body's own stress physiology, major life events, and workplace stress.
  • Whether something is stressful depends on appraisal — how the person interprets it — which is why the same event stresses some people but not others.

Physiology of stress: Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

  • Hans Selye proposed the body responds to any prolonged stressor with the same three-stage pattern, the GAS.
  • STAGE 1 ALARM: the body detects the stressor and the sympathetic nervous system triggers the 'fight or flight' response — adrenaline is released, heart rate and breathing rise, preparing the body for action.
  • STAGE 2 RESISTANCE: if the stressor continues, the body adapts and tries to cope, keeping physiological arousal high (via cortisol) while appearing outwardly normal; resources are being used up.
  • STAGE 3 EXHAUSTION: if stress is prolonged, the body's resources are depleted, coping fails, and stress-related illnesses appear (e.g. high blood pressure, ulcers, weakened immunity).

Effects on health and the immune system

  • Chronic stress keeps cortisol levels high; sustained cortisol suppresses the immune system, reducing the activity of white blood cells and making infection and illness more likely.
  • Long-term stress is linked to cardiovascular problems (raised blood pressure and heart disease) because of prolonged high arousal.
  • This explains why people under long-term stress catch more colds and heal more slowly — the exhaustion stage of GAS in action.

Life events: Holmes & Rahe SRRS

  • Holmes and Rahe argued that any change requiring readjustment is stressful, whether positive (marriage) or negative (divorce).
  • They created the Social Readjustment Rating Scale (SRRS), listing 43 life events each given a value in Life Change Units (LCUs) reflecting how much readjustment it requires — e.g. death of a spouse = 100 LCUs, marriage = 50, minor law violations low.
  • A person totals the LCUs for events in the past year; higher totals (e.g. above 300) predict a greater risk of stress-related illness.
  • Strength: it quantifies stress and predicts illness; Weakness: it ignores individual differences in how events are appraised and treats positive and negative change the same.

Work stress: Chandola et al. (2008) Whitehall II

  • The Whitehall II study followed British civil servants to test how chronic work stress affects heart health.
  • Chandola et al. found that participants with more chronic work stress had a higher risk of coronary heart disease, and that this link was partly explained by the effect of stress on the body (poorer heart-rate variability, raised cortisol) and partly by unhealthy behaviours (poor diet, less exercise).
  • The effect was strongest in younger workers, showing long-term work stress has a real biological pathway to illness, not just a psychological one.

Type A personality: Friedman & Rosenman

  • Friedman and Rosenman identified Type A personality — competitive, impatient, time-pressured, hostile and achievement-driven — as more prone to experiencing stress and to coronary heart disease.
  • Type B individuals are relaxed, patient and easy-going and show lower stress-related illness.
  • The personality itself acts as a source/moderator of stress: Type A people appraise more situations as threatening and stay physiologically aroused, increasing cardiovascular risk.

Key terms

  • Stressor: a stimulus that triggers the stress response.
  • GAS: Selye's three-stage bodily stress response (alarm, resistance, exhaustion).
  • Cortisol: a stress hormone that, when chronically high, suppresses immunity.
  • Life Change Units (LCUs): SRRS scores for the readjustment each life event demands.
  • Type A personality: a competitive, hostile, time-urgent personality linked to higher stress and heart disease.

Stress: sources of stress — evaluation & recall

Evaluation: GAS model

  • Strength: Selye's model is supported by biological evidence and explains the physical illness that follows prolonged stress via cortisol and immune suppression.
  • Weakness: it is largely based on animal (rat) research and assumes the same response to all stressors, ignoring that psychological appraisal shapes the human response.
  • It is a reductionist, biological account that underplays cognitive and individual differences in coping.

Evaluation: SRRS (life events)

  • Strength: the SRRS is a quick, standardised, quantitative tool that reliably predicts illness at a group level.
  • Weakness: it relies on retrospective self-report (memory bias), ignores daily hassles, and treats a fixed LCU value as the same for everyone despite individual differences in appraisal.
  • Correlational data from the SRRS cannot prove life events cause illness — a third factor could underlie both.

Research methods

  • Whitehall II is a longitudinal cohort study, strong for tracking how chronic stress leads to disease over years but expensive and prone to participant dropout.
  • The SRRS and Type A questionnaires are self-report measures, quick but open to social desirability and memory bias.
  • Much stress-illness research is correlational, so causation must be inferred cautiously; Chandola strengthens causal claims by identifying biological pathways.

  • Distinguish the sources: internal physiology (GAS), external events (SRRS), the workplace (Whitehall II) and personality (Type A) — a good essay covers all four.

  • Link Type A to appraisal: it is not the event alone but how the person interprets it that determines stress, bridging biological and cognitive explanations.

  • Nature vs nurture: Type A personality (partly dispositional) versus life events and work conditions (environmental) as sources of stress.
  • Individual differences: appraisal means the same stressor affects people differently, a weakness of the fixed-score SRRS.
  • Reductionism vs holism: GAS is a reductionist biological model, whereas Chandola's mix of biology and behaviour is more holistic.
  • Application/usefulness: identifying work stress (Whitehall II) and Type A as risk factors allows targeted prevention of heart disease.

Stress: measuring stress

Objective vs subjective measures

  • Stress can be measured biologically/physiologically (objective — recording bodily changes) or psychologically (subjective — self-report of how stressed a person feels).
  • Objective measures are less open to bias and give numerical data, but a raised heart rate could be due to exercise or excitement rather than stress, so they may lack validity for stress specifically.
  • Subjective measures capture the person's actual experience and appraisal, but are open to social desirability and memory bias.
  • Using both together triangulates the data and improves overall validity.

Biological measure: heart rate

  • Heart rate (and blood pressure) rise during the alarm/fight-or-flight response, so a raised heart rate can indicate stress.
  • It is easy to measure objectively and continuously, and responds quickly to acute stressors.
  • Weakness: it is not specific to stress — physical activity, caffeine, illness or excitement also raise heart rate — so on its own it can be invalid as a stress measure.

Biological measure: fMRI — Wang et al. (2005)

  • fMRI (functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging) shows which brain areas are active by tracking blood flow/oxygen use while a person performs a task.
  • Wang et al. (2005) had participants do stressful mental arithmetic under time pressure while being scanned; they found increased activity in the right prefrontal cortex and changes in cerebral blood flow that correlated with stress and with rises in heart rate and cortisol.
  • Strength: it is objective and reveals the biological reality of stress in the brain. Weakness: fMRI is expensive, the artificial lab task lacks ecological validity, and lying still in a scanner is itself unusual/stressful.

Biological measure: salivary cortisol — Evans & Wener (2007)

  • Cortisol, the main stress hormone, can be measured in saliva samples — a non-invasive, objective biochemical marker of stress.
  • Evans and Wener (2007) studied commuters and found that longer, more crowded train commutes were associated with higher salivary cortisol and poorer task performance/higher perceived stress, showing everyday environmental stressors raise cortisol.
  • Strength: cortisol is a direct hormonal indicator and saliva sampling is easy and non-invasive. Weakness: cortisol follows a natural daily (diurnal) rhythm — high on waking, falling through the day — so samples must be taken at controlled times or the reading is misleading.

Psychological (self-report) measures

  • The Social Readjustment Rating Scale (SRRS, Holmes & Rahe) measures stress from life events by summing Life Change Units, giving a numerical stress score from self-reported experiences.
  • Type A questionnaires assess personality traits (competitiveness, hostility, time-urgency) linked to stress proneness.
  • Strength: quick, cheap, and directly access the person's own appraisal of their stress. Weakness: subjective and open to social desirability and inaccurate memory; scores may not match the person's actual physiological state.

The diurnal cortisol issue and objectivity

  • Because cortisol naturally rises and falls over 24 hours, a single sample is meaningless without knowing the time of day; researchers must standardise sampling times or take multiple samples.
  • This shows even 'objective' biological measures need careful controls to be valid.
  • Comparing objective and subjective: objective measures reduce bias but may lack specificity/validity; subjective measures are valid for felt stress but unreliable — hence combining them is best practice.

Key terms

  • Objective measure: an unbiased, quantifiable measure (e.g. cortisol, heart rate).
  • Subjective measure: a self-report reflecting the person's own experience.
  • Salivary cortisol: a stress hormone measured in saliva as a biological marker.
  • Diurnal rhythm: the natural daily cycle of cortisol, high on waking and falling through the day.
  • fMRI: a brain-imaging technique showing activity via blood flow.

Evaluation: biological measures

  • Strength: physiological measures (cortisol, heart rate, fMRI) are objective, quantitative and free from social desirability, giving scientific credibility.
  • Weakness: they can lack validity because bodily changes are not specific to stress; heart rate rises for many reasons and cortisol varies with time of day.

Evaluation: self-report measures

  • Strength: they directly capture the person's subjective appraisal — the essence of stress — and are cheap and quick to administer on a large scale.
  • Weakness: they are open to social desirability, memory bias and individual interpretation of the questions, lowering reliability.
  • Self-report scores often correlate poorly with physiological measures, showing the two tap different aspects of stress.

Research methods

  • Evans & Wener used a field study (real commuters), giving high ecological validity but less control over confounds than a lab.
  • Wang et al. used a controlled lab experiment with fMRI, giving control and objectivity but low ecological validity.
  • Correlations are common (e.g. commute length with cortisol), so causation must be inferred cautiously; standardising cortisol sampling times is a crucial control.

Stress: managing stress

Overview of stress management

  • Stress can be managed physiologically (reducing bodily arousal, e.g. biofeedback, relaxation) or psychologically/cognitively (changing how one thinks and copes, e.g. stress inoculation training).
  • Physiological methods target the symptoms (high heart rate, muscle tension); cognitive methods target the causes (appraisal and coping skills), giving longer-lasting protection.
  • Combining approaches is often most effective.

Biofeedback

  • Biofeedback uses electronic equipment to give a person real-time information (audio or visual signal) about a bodily process they cannot normally sense, such as muscle tension (EMG), heart rate or skin temperature.
  • By seeing/hearing the signal, the person learns through operant conditioning and trial-and-error to consciously reduce that arousal (e.g. relaxing muscles lowers the tone), gaining voluntary control over the stress response.
  • There are four elements: measuring the physiological response, converting it to a signal, feeding it back, and the person learning to alter it.
  • Strength: gives the patient active control and no drugs/side effects. Weakness: requires expensive equipment and training, and benefits may not transfer to everyday life away from the machine.

Biofeedback evidence: Budzynski et al. (1969)

  • Budzynski et al. used EMG biofeedback with patients suffering tension headaches, feeding back forehead (frontalis) muscle tension as a tone that dropped as the muscle relaxed.
  • Patients who received true feedback learned to reduce their muscle tension and reported fewer headaches than a control group given false/no feedback.
  • This showed biofeedback can teach people to control a physiological stress response and reduce a real symptom.

Relaxation and imagery

  • Relaxation techniques (e.g. progressive muscle relaxation — tensing then releasing muscle groups in turn, and controlled deep breathing) activate the parasympathetic nervous system, lowering heart rate, blood pressure and muscle tension.
  • Imagery involves picturing a calm, pleasant scene (e.g. a peaceful beach) which is incompatible with anxiety and directs attention away from the stressor.
  • Strength: cheap, easy to learn, no side effects and can be self-administered anywhere. Weakness: needs practice and motivation, and may not tackle the underlying cause of chronic stress.

Stress Inoculation Training (SIT) — Meichenbaum

  • SIT is a cognitive-behavioural technique that 'inoculates' people against stress by preparing coping skills in advance, in three phases.
  • PHASE 1 CONCEPTUALISATION: the therapist helps the client understand their stress, identify stressors and how they currently (unhelpfully) think about them.
  • PHASE 2 SKILLS ACQUISITION: the client learns and rehearses coping skills — relaxation, positive self-statements (e.g. 'I can handle this step by step'), and problem-solving.
  • PHASE 3 APPLICATION: the client practises the new skills first on small imagined or role-played stressors, then on real-life stressors, building confidence.
  • Strength: it targets the cause (thinking/coping), so effects are long-lasting and preventative. Weakness: it is time-consuming, expensive, requires commitment and a skilled therapist.

Key terms

  • Biofeedback: learning to control a bodily process using real-time feedback from equipment.
  • EMG: electromyography, measurement of muscle tension.
  • Progressive muscle relaxation: systematically tensing and releasing muscle groups to induce calm.
  • Stress Inoculation Training: Meichenbaum's three-phase cognitive-behavioural stress management.
  • Self-statements: positive coping thoughts rehearsed in SIT.
⭐ Key study — Bridge et al. (1988) — Relaxation and imagery in breast cancer patients

Aim: To investigate whether relaxation, with or without guided imagery, reduces stress and improves mood in women receiving radiotherapy for early breast cancer.

Method: Field/clinical experiment with a randomised, independent-groups design. Sample: 154 women with early (Stage I or II) breast cancer receiving radiotherapy. They were randomly allocated to three conditions for six weeks: (1) a control group who simply talked to the researcher about themselves for the same amount of time; (2) a relaxation-only group taught progressive muscle relaxation to practise daily; (3) a relaxation-plus-imagery group who additionally imagined a peaceful, pleasant scene of their choice. Mood and stress were measured before and after using the Profile of Mood States (POMS) questionnaire and the Leeds General Scale for anxiety and depression.

Results: Both intervention groups were less tense/anxious than the control group, and the relaxation-plus-imagery group showed the greatest improvement in mood, being significantly more relaxed than the relaxation-only group. The effect was clearest for older women.

Conclusion: Relaxation reduces stress in cancer patients, and adding guided imagery enhances the benefit; simple psychological techniques can meaningfully improve wellbeing during a highly stressful medical treatment.

Health promotion: strategies

What is health promotion?

  • Health promotion is any strategy designed to encourage healthy behaviour and prevent illness, e.g. campaigns to improve dental hygiene, quit smoking or recover from illness.
  • Two key strategies studied here are FEAR AROUSAL (using frightening messages to motivate change) and PROVIDING INFORMATION (giving people the knowledge and skills to manage their health).
  • Effective promotion depends not just on the message content but on how people process and respond to it psychologically.

Fear arousal: the theory

  • The idea is that arousing fear about the consequences of an unhealthy behaviour motivates people to change it (e.g. graphic images on cigarette packets).
  • However, fear works as an inverted-U: MODERATE fear is most effective, while TOO LITTLE fear fails to motivate and TOO MUCH fear backfires — people become defensive, deny the threat, or feel helpless and switch off.
  • Fear works best when combined with clear, achievable advice on what to do (efficacy) so people feel able to act on the fear rather than being overwhelmed.

Fear arousal study: Janis & Feshbach (1953)

  • Aim: to test how different levels of fear affect whether people follow dental-hygiene advice.
  • Method: an experiment with around 200 US high-school students randomly split into four groups. Three groups saw a 15-minute talk on tooth decay with HIGH fear (graphic, painful images of diseased gums/decay), MODERATE fear, or LOW fear (factual, mild), and a fourth was a control (unrelated topic). Attitudes and toothbrushing behaviour were measured before, after and one week later.
  • Results: the HIGH-fear group reported the most immediate worry but showed the LEAST actual change in brushing behaviour a week later, whereas the LOW-fear group showed the most conformity to the dental advice. The high-fear group was also more likely to dismiss/deny the message.
  • Conclusion: too much fear is counter-productive — it triggers defensive avoidance — so minimal-to-moderate fear combined with reassuring, practical advice is more effective for changing behaviour.

Providing information: the theory

  • Giving people accurate, clear information and practical skills empowers them to take control of their own health (self-management), especially for recovery and chronic conditions.
  • Well-designed information should be understandable, tailored and give people confidence (self-efficacy) that they can carry out the recommended actions.
  • This is a less emotive, more cognitive strategy than fear arousal.

Providing information study: Lewin et al. (1992) Heart Manual

  • Aim: to test whether a self-help rehabilitation manual improves recovery in patients after a heart attack (myocardial infarction).
  • Method: patients recovering from a first heart attack were randomly allocated either to receive 'The Heart Manual' — a home-based self-management programme with information, a relaxation/exercise plan and advice on managing worry, plus brief therapist contact — or to a placebo/standard-care control group.
  • Results: patients using the Heart Manual showed less anxiety and depression, fewer visits to their GP, and fewer re-admissions to hospital in the following year compared with controls.
  • Conclusion: providing structured self-management information helps patients recover psychologically and physically and reduces demand on health services.

Key terms

  • Health promotion: strategies to encourage healthy behaviour and prevent illness.
  • Fear arousal: using frightening messages to motivate behaviour change.
  • Defensive avoidance: rejecting or denying a message because it is too frightening.
  • Self-efficacy: a person's belief that they can successfully perform a behaviour.
  • Self-management: giving people information/skills to look after their own health.

Health promotion: strategies — evaluation & recall

Evaluation: fear arousal

  • Strength: fear campaigns are cheap, reach mass audiences and can grab attention (e.g. smoking-pack images), and Janis & Feshbach give clear experimental evidence on how it works.
  • Weakness: high fear backfires through defensive avoidance, so campaigns can fail or even entrench the behaviour; fear alone without practical advice is ineffective.
  • Ethical issue: deliberately frightening people can cause distress and may be seen as manipulative.

Evaluation: providing information

  • Strength: it respects the individual's autonomy, builds self-efficacy and, as Lewin shows, produces real physical and psychological benefits while saving healthcare costs.
  • Weakness: information alone may not change deeply habitual behaviour, and success depends on the person understanding and being motivated to act on it.
  • Written manuals may exclude people with low literacy or poor access, raising equity concerns.

Research methods

  • Both studies used experiments with control groups; Janis & Feshbach was a controlled experiment allowing cause-effect but with an artificial, one-off manipulation.
  • Lewin et al. used a randomised controlled field trial with real patients, giving high ecological validity and reliable comparison to a control.
  • Outcome measures (self-reported brushing; anxiety/depression scores, re-admission rates) mix self-report and objective data; self-report parts risk social desirability.

  • Contrast the two strategies: fear = emotional and can backfire; information = cognitive and empowering — use Janis & Feshbach and Lewin as the paired evidence.

  • Link Janis & Feshbach to ethics (causing fear) and to individual differences (high-anxiety people react worse to fear appeals).

  • Application/usefulness: both strategies inform real public-health campaigns; Lewin's manual reduces hospital re-admissions.
  • Ethics: fear arousal risks causing psychological distress and may be manipulative, unlike neutral information-giving.
  • Individual differences: the same fear appeal helps some but triggers defensive avoidance in others, especially the highly anxious.
  • Free will vs determinism: providing information respects autonomy (free choice), whereas fear appeals try to push behaviour more coercively.

Health promotion in schools and worksites

Why target schools and worksites?

  • Schools and worksites are 'captive' settings where large numbers of people can be reached repeatedly over time, making them ideal for changing health behaviour.
  • Schools shape lifelong habits early (e.g. healthy eating), while worksites can improve health and safety and reduce accidents/absence.
  • Both examples here use behaviourist principles — modelling, reinforcement and repeated exposure — to change behaviour.

Schools — healthy eating: Tapper et al. (2003) Food Dudes

  • Aim: to increase children's consumption of fruit and vegetables using the Food Dudes programme.
  • The programme combines three psychological techniques: MODELLING (children watch video adventures of older, heroic 'Food Dudes' characters who gain power by eating fruit and vegetables, so children imitate admired role models); REPEATED EXPOSURE (children are given fruit and vegetables regularly, and taste familiarity increases liking over time); and REWARDS (children earn small rewards, e.g. stickers and prizes, for eating them — positive reinforcement).
  • Method: children in participating schools took part over a period, with fruit/vegetable consumption measured before and after and compared to a control.
  • Results: children in the Food Dudes programme substantially increased their fruit and vegetable consumption, including previously fussy eaters, and increases were maintained at follow-up, especially for children who ate little to begin with.
  • Conclusion: combining modelling, repeated exposure and rewards is an effective, lasting way to improve children's eating habits.

Why Food Dudes works (theory)

  • Modelling draws on Social Learning Theory: children imitate behaviour of attractive, successful role models they identify with.
  • Repeated exposure exploits the 'mere exposure' effect — the more often a child tastes a food, the more they come to like it, overcoming initial neophobia (fear of new foods).
  • Rewards use operant conditioning (positive reinforcement) to strengthen the behaviour; crucially, when rewards are faded out, the newly-liked foods and habits persist.

Worksites — health & safety: Fox et al. (1987) token economy in mining

  • Aim: to reduce accidents and injuries in two dangerous open-cast (open-pit) mines using a token economy.
  • Method: a field study/quasi-experiment over several years. Workers earned tokens (trading stamps) for working without lost-time injuries, for their group having no injuries, for making safety suggestions and for avoiding equipment damage; tokens were exchangeable for goods at redemption stores. Tokens were removed if a worker or a workmate was injured, encouraging peer safety monitoring.
  • Results: after the token economy was introduced, the number of days lost through injury fell dramatically at both mines and stayed low for years, and the cost of the tokens was far outweighed by the savings from fewer accidents.
  • Conclusion: a token economy using operant conditioning is an effective, cost-beneficial way to promote safe behaviour in a high-risk workplace.

Key terms

  • Modelling: learning by imitating an admired role model (Social Learning Theory).
  • Repeated exposure: increased liking of a food through repeated tasting.
  • Positive reinforcement: rewarding a behaviour to make it more likely to recur.
  • Token economy: a system where tokens earned for target behaviour are exchanged for rewards.
  • Quasi-experiment: a study using naturally occurring conditions rather than full random allocation.

Evaluation: Food Dudes (schools)

  • Strength: it changed behaviour in a real setting with lasting effects and helped the fussiest eaters most, showing high practical value and ecological validity.
  • Weakness: it relies on continued resources (videos, rewards), and effects may weaken if the programme stops; measuring consumption accurately in schools is hard.
  • It combines several techniques, so it is difficult to know which component (modelling, exposure or rewards) is most responsible.

Evaluation: Fox et al. token economy (worksites)

  • Strength: it produced a large, long-lasting reduction in injuries and was highly cost-effective, with strong real-world impact over many years.
  • Weakness: rewarding 'no reported injuries' may lead workers to hide or under-report minor injuries to keep their tokens, distorting the data.
  • As a field study there was little control over confounding variables (e.g. changes in equipment or management over time).

Research methods

  • Both are field studies/quasi-experiments with high ecological validity but reduced control over extraneous variables.
  • Longitudinal follow-up in both (maintenance of eating; years of accident data) strengthens claims that the change is durable.
  • Ethical and validity issues arise from possible under-reporting (Fox) and from measuring children's real intake accurately (Tapper).

Changing health beliefs and Health wrap-up

Changing health beliefs: the challenge

  • People's health beliefs — what they think about their own risk and wellbeing — strongly shape their behaviour, and faulty beliefs (like thinking 'it won't happen to me') undermine health promotion.
  • Two ideas here address this: UNREALISTIC OPTIMISM (Weinstein) explains why people underestimate their risk, and POSITIVE PSYCHOLOGY (Seligman) shifts focus from treating illness to building wellbeing.

Unrealistic optimism: Weinstein (1980)

  • Unrealistic (comparative) optimism is the tendency to believe that bad things (illness, accidents) are less likely to happen to oneself than to other people.
  • Weinstein (1980) asked around 250 US college students to rate their own chances, compared to peers, of experiencing various positive and negative life events.
  • Results: on average students judged themselves more likely than others to experience positive events and less likely to experience negative events (e.g. getting a disease) — statistically impossible for a whole group, revealing systematic bias.
  • Four factors increase unrealistic optimism: believing there are no preventive actions, a belief the problem is rare, lack of personal experience of it, and the belief that if it hasn't happened yet it won't. This bias is a barrier to health promotion because people ignore risks they think don't apply to them.

Positive psychology: Seligman (2004)

  • Positive psychology studies what makes life worth living and how to build strengths and happiness, rather than only fixing disorders.
  • Seligman described three routes to happiness/authentic wellbeing: the PLEASANT LIFE (maximising positive emotions and pleasures, e.g. enjoyable experiences); the GOOD (engaged) LIFE (using one's signature strengths and becoming absorbed in activities — 'flow'); and the MEANINGFUL LIFE (using one's strengths in the service of something bigger than oneself, e.g. helping others or a cause).
  • The meaningful and good lives are thought to bring deeper, more lasting wellbeing than the pleasant life alone.
  • Applied to health, building wellbeing and positive beliefs (optimism, resilience) can protect mental and physical health and support behaviour change.

Key terms

  • Unrealistic optimism: believing one is less at risk of harm than other people are.
  • Comparative judgement: rating one's own risk relative to peers.
  • Positive psychology: the scientific study of wellbeing, strengths and flourishing.
  • The pleasant / good / meaningful life: Seligman's three routes to happiness.
  • Flow: complete absorption in an activity that uses one's strengths.
⭐ Key study — Shoshani & Steinmetz (2014) — Positive psychology school intervention

Aim: To test whether a school-based positive-psychology programme improves adolescents' mental health and wellbeing and reduces distress, anxiety and depression.

Method: A two-year longitudinal field experiment with repeated measures, using a control school for comparison (quasi-experimental design). Sample: about 537 students aged roughly 12-15 (grades 7-9) at a large middle school in central Israel, compared with about 501 students at a demographically similar control school. Procedure: teachers at the intervention school were first trained in positive psychology, then delivered a year-long programme of workshops to students focusing on cultivating positive emotions, character strengths, gratitude, optimism and positive relationships. Students completed self-report questionnaires measuring wellbeing, self-esteem, self-efficacy, optimism and psychological distress (anxiety and depression symptoms) before and after.

Results: Students in the intervention school showed significant decreases in general distress, anxiety and depression, and significant increases in self-esteem, self-efficacy, optimism and reductions in interpersonal sensitivity, whereas in the control school distress symptoms significantly increased over the same period.

Conclusion: A structured positive-psychology intervention in schools can improve adolescents' wellbeing and act as a protective factor against the rise in mental-health problems seen in the untreated control group.

How to make effective flashcards

  • Active recall is the core principle: a good card forces you to retrieve the answer from memory rather than merely recognise it. Put a genuine question or cue on the front and the answer on the back, so that seeing the front makes you work. Recognition (re-reading notes) feels productive but produces far weaker learning than retrieval.
  • Spaced repetition schedules your reviews so that you see a card just as you are about to forget it. Use a system (physical Leitner boxes or an app such as Anki) that shows cards you find hard more often and cards you know well less often. Cramming all reviews into one session is much less durable than spacing them across days and weeks.
  • Interleaving means mixing different topics and study types in one session rather than blocking (e.g. only doing schizophrenia for an hour). Shuffling schizophrenia, OCD and stress cards together forces your brain to first identify which type of problem it is facing, which is exactly the discrimination skill the exam demands.
  • Keep each card to one idea. A card asking 'explain everything about OCD' cannot be answered cleanly; split it into obsessions, compulsions, one explanation, one treatment. Small atomic cards give clean pass/fail feedback for spaced repetition.

The ideal key-study card layout

  • Front of the card: the study name and date plus a one-line prompt, e.g. 'Freeman et al. (2003) — aim, method, results, conclusion, evaluation, method used, which topic?'. This single prompt forces you to reconstruct the whole study.
  • Back of the card, in fixed order so recall becomes automatic: Aim (what they wanted to find out); Method (design, sample, IV/DV or procedure, key controls); Results (the specific findings, with any numbers you can remember); Conclusion (what the results mean); Evaluation (one strength and one weakness — usually validity, reliability, ethics, generalisability, or usefulness).
  • Add two extra lines that examiners reward: the research method used (e.g. laboratory experiment, self-report/questionnaire, correlation, case study) because Paper 4 and part (c) questions ask you to identify and evaluate methods; and which debate the study best illustrates (e.g. Freeman links to the individual vs situational debate and to application of psychology to everyday life).
  • Practising the card in this order builds a template you can reuse under pressure: every study answer then flows aim to method to results to conclusion to evaluation, which is exactly the structure examiners expect for the named studies.

Paper 4 planning: general features

What the planning question asks of you

  • Paper 4 gives you a novel scenario and asks you to design a study to investigate it. You are marked on the quality, detail and appropriateness of your plan, and separately on your evaluation of it. Every plan, whatever the method, must contain the same general features described below; the method-specific features come on top.
  • The golden rule is replicability: write in enough detail that another psychologist could carry out your study exactly as you intended without asking you a single question. Vagueness is the commonest reason plans lose marks.

Aims and hypotheses

  • The aim states the general purpose of the study — what you want to find out — in one clear sentence, e.g. 'to investigate whether a patient-centred consulting style improves adherence to medication'.
  • A hypothesis is a precise, testable prediction and must be operationalised, meaning both variables are defined in terms of exactly how they will be measured. 'Adherence' becomes 'the percentage of prescribed pills taken, measured by pill count over four weeks'.
  • The alternative (experimental) hypothesis predicts a difference or relationship. A directional (one-tailed) hypothesis states the direction of the effect ('patients given a patient-centred style will take a higher percentage of pills than those given a doctor-centred style'); a non-directional (two-tailed) hypothesis predicts a difference without stating direction. Use directional only when previous research justifies the direction.
  • The null hypothesis predicts no difference or relationship (any difference is due to chance), e.g. 'there will be no difference in the percentage of pills taken between the two consulting-style conditions'. Always state which hypothesis you would retain depends on the results.

Sample and sampling techniques

  • Specify the target population, the sample size, and crucially the sampling technique with a full definition, because examiners frequently award marks for the definition and a reason it suits your study.
  • Random sampling: every member of the target population has an equal chance of selection (e.g. names drawn from a numbered list by a random number generator). Strength: unbiased and representative; weakness: needs a full list and can still, by chance, be unrepresentative.
  • Opportunity sampling: using whoever is available and willing at the time. Strength: quick and cheap; weakness: often biased and unrepresentative of the wider population.
  • Volunteer (self-selected) sampling: participants respond to an advert. Strength: easy access to motivated participants; weakness: volunteers may differ systematically (volunteer bias).
  • Systematic sampling: selecting every nth person from a list (e.g. every 5th patient). Strength: simple and spread across the list; weakness: a hidden pattern in the list can bias it.
  • Stratified sampling: dividing the population into strata (e.g. age groups) and sampling proportionally from each, usually randomly. Strength: representative of key subgroups; weakness: time-consuming and requires knowledge of the strata.

Ethics, validity and reliability

  • Ethics you must address explicitly: informed consent (participants agree knowing the general nature of the study); right to withdraw (they may leave at any time and remove their data); confidentiality (data kept anonymous and secure); protection from harm (no physical or psychological harm beyond everyday life); and deception with debrief (avoid deception where possible; where minor deception is unavoidable, fully debrief afterwards).
  • Validity is whether the study measures what it claims to. Build in validity by operationalising variables well, controlling extraneous variables, using standardised procedures, and choosing measures that genuinely reflect the concept (e.g. using an objective pill count rather than only self-report for adherence).
  • Reliability is consistency. Build it in through standardised, identical procedures for every participant, clear scoring criteria, and, where relevant, inter-rater reliability (two observers checked for agreement) or test-retest checks.

Data, analysis and descriptive statistics

  • State whether your data are quantitative (numbers) or qualitative (words/themes), and whether the measurement level lends itself to numerical analysis.
  • Descriptive statistics summarise data. Measures of central tendency: the mean (arithmetic average, uses all scores but distorted by outliers), the median (middle value, resistant to outliers), and the mode (most frequent value, useful for categories).
  • Measures of spread: the range (highest minus lowest, simple but affected by extremes) and the standard deviation (average distance of scores from the mean, more informative but assumes roughly normal data).
  • Say how you will display results: tables of means, bar charts (for categories/conditions), scatter graphs (for correlations). This shows the examiner you can turn raw data into interpretable summaries.

Paper 4 planning: general features — evaluation & recall

How the planning question is marked

  • The Paper 4 planning question is worth 10 marks for the plan (the design itself) and a further set of marks for evaluating that plan; the evaluation of methodology and the discussion of results/debates carries the larger analytical weight (the section is commonly framed as plan 10 + evaluate 14). Treat them as two distinct tasks and budget your time for both — do not spend everything on the plan and leave the evaluation thin.
  • High marks on the plan come from appropriateness (the method genuinely tests the aim), detail (enough for replication), and coverage of the general features above: aim/hypothesis, sample and sampling, procedure, ethics, controls, and data handling.
  • High marks on the evaluation come from applying methodological concepts to your own plan specifically — not generic textbook points. Say why your sampling choice threatens or protects generalisability, whether your controls are sufficient for internal validity, and how your design trades off against ecological validity.

Writing in enough detail for replication

  • Imagine handing your plan to a stranger. State exactly what materials are used, what each participant is told (you can quote standardised instructions), what they do step by step, how long it lasts, where it happens, and precisely how each variable is measured and scored.
  • Quantify everything you can: number of participants, number in each condition, number of trials, time limits, and the scale used (e.g. a 1-5 rating). Numbers remove ambiguity and are a fast route to detail marks.
  • Name your controls and standardisations explicitly (same instructions, same room, same time of day, same order of tasks or counterbalanced). Each named control is both a detail mark on the plan and an evaluation point later.

Paper 4 planning: experiments & self-reports

Experiment-specific features

  • Type of experiment: state whether it is a laboratory experiment (controlled setting, high control, lower ecological validity), a field experiment (natural setting, real behaviour, less control), or a natural/quasi experiment (IV is naturally occurring, e.g. patients vs non-patients, so no random allocation is possible). The type determines how much control and ecological validity you can claim.
  • Independent variable (IV): the variable you manipulate, stated with its exact levels/conditions, e.g. IV = consulting style, with two levels: doctor-centred vs patient-centred.
  • Dependent variable (DV): the variable you measure, fully operationalised, e.g. DV = adherence measured as percentage of prescribed pills taken over four weeks by pill count.
  • Controls and standardisation: identify extraneous variables (things other than the IV that could affect the DV) and say how you hold them constant — same script, same room, same duration, same instructions. Standardisation means every participant is treated identically except for the IV.

Design and allocation

  • Experimental design: independent measures (different participants in each condition — avoids order effects but introduces participant variables), repeated measures (same participants in all conditions — controls participant variables but risks order effects), or matched pairs (participants matched on key variables then split — a compromise).
  • Counterbalancing is used in repeated measures to control order effects: half the participants do condition A then B, the other half B then A (an ABBA arrangement), so practice or fatigue effects cancel out.
  • Random allocation is used in independent measures: participants are assigned to conditions by chance (e.g. coin flip or random number) so that participant variables are spread evenly across conditions rather than clustering in one.
  • Location: state where the study happens (clinic, laboratory, participant's home) and justify it against your aim and the control/ecological-validity trade-off.

Self-report specifics: questionnaires

  • Decide the question types. Closed questions offer fixed responses (yes/no, or a rating scale) and produce quantitative data that is easy to analyse but limited in depth. Open questions invite free written answers and produce rich qualitative data that is harder to analyse.
  • Give example questions in your plan — actually write two or three. This is one of the fastest ways to earn detail marks and shows the examiner your measure is real, e.g. a Likert item 'I take my medication exactly as prescribed' rated 1 (never) to 5 (always).
  • State the scoring: how responses convert to a number (e.g. sum the Likert items for a total adherence score from 5 to 25) and how open answers are analysed (thematic analysis into categories).
  • Note the technique/administration: how it is delivered (postal, online, face-to-face), whether anonymous, and any controls for social desirability (anonymity, neutral wording).

Self-report specifics: interviews

  • State the format: structured (fixed questions in a fixed order — reliable and comparable but inflexible), unstructured (a guiding topic with free-flowing conversation — rich but hard to replicate), or semi-structured (set core questions plus follow-up probes — a common, flexible compromise).
  • State the technique: whether it is face-to-face or by telephone, recorded and transcribed, who conducts it and how they are trained to avoid leading questions, and how rapport is built while staying neutral.
  • Match format to aim: use structured interviews when you need comparable quantitative data across many people, and unstructured/semi-structured when you want depth about experiences (e.g. a patient's experience of chronic pain).

Paper 4 planning: observations, correlations, case studies, longitudinal

Observations

  • Decide the setting: naturalistic (behaviour observed where it normally occurs — high ecological validity, low control) or controlled (in a set-up environment — more control, less natural).
  • Decide the observer role: participant (observer joins the group — rich insight but risk of bias/going native) or non-participant (observer stays separate — more objective but less insight); and whether it is overt (participants know) or covert (they do not — reduces demand characteristics but raises ethical issues around consent).
  • Design the recording system: define behavioural categories precisely (operationalised, mutually exclusive), and choose a sampling method — event sampling (count each time a target behaviour occurs) or time sampling (record what is happening at fixed intervals).
  • Build in inter-rater reliability: two independent observers use the same category checklist and their records are correlated; high agreement shows the observation is reliable. This is a key design feature to name in an observation plan.

Correlations

  • A correlational study measures two co-variables and tests whether they are associated; there is no IV/DV and no manipulation. State the two co-variables, both operationalised, e.g. co-variable 1 = self-reported daily hassles score (Hassles Scale), co-variable 2 = number of GP visits in six months.
  • State the predicted correlation and its direction: positive (both rise together), negative (one rises as the other falls), or a hypothesis of no correlation. Plan to display data on a scatter graph and to report a correlation coefficient (between -1 and +1).
  • Crucial evaluation point to design around: correlation does not establish causation — a third variable may explain both, and the direction of any causal link is unknown. Never write a causal hypothesis for a correlation.
  • Ensure both measures are valid and reliable, since a weak measure will attenuate any real relationship.

Case studies

  • A case study is an in-depth investigation of one person, small group or institution, typically using multiple methods (interview, observation, tests, records) to build a rich, holistic picture. State who or what the case is and why it is of interest (often a rare or unusual case).
  • Plan the methods triangulated: e.g. semi-structured interviews plus standardised clinical tests plus observation over several sessions. Describe how data are gathered and combined.
  • Strengths to design toward: great depth and detail, ideal for rare conditions and for generating hypotheses. Weaknesses to acknowledge: findings may not generalise beyond the single case; researcher subjectivity and the reliance on retrospective/self-report data can threaten validity and reliability.
  • Address ethics carefully because a single identifiable person is involved: strong confidentiality (pseudonyms), consent (and assent/guardian consent where needed), and protection from harm given the intensive involvement.

Longitudinal designs

  • A longitudinal study follows the same participants over an extended period, measuring the same variables at intervals, so you can track change over time (e.g. adherence or symptom severity across two years).
  • State the number and timing of measurement points and the total duration, and how you will keep procedures identical at each point to allow fair comparison over time.
  • Strength: it can show development and long-term effects and rules out some cohort/participant-variable confounds because each person is their own baseline. Weaknesses: participant attrition (drop-out) can bias the remaining sample; it is time-consuming and expensive; and repeated testing may cause practice effects.
  • Contrast with cross-sectional designs (different age/stage groups compared at one time), which are quicker but confound age with cohort differences.

Paper 4 planning: observations, correlations, case studies, longitudinal — evaluation & recall

How to choose the right method for an aim

  • Read the aim's verb. If it asks about the effect or cause of one variable on another and the variable can be manipulated, choose an experiment. If it asks about a relationship or association between two things that cannot ethically be manipulated (e.g. stress and illness), choose a correlation.
  • If it asks what people think, feel or report, choose a self-report (questionnaire for breadth, interview for depth). If it asks about behaviour as it actually happens, choose an observation. If it asks for deep understanding of a rare or complex single case, choose a case study. If it asks about change over time, choose a longitudinal design.
  • Always weigh control against ecological validity: laboratory methods maximise control and internal validity; naturalistic methods maximise realism. The best plan justifies its choice explicitly against the aim rather than defaulting to an experiment.

How to evaluate a plan

  • Structure the evaluation around the same concepts every time so nothing is missed: validity (internal — are confounds controlled? and ecological — is it realistic?), reliability (are procedures standardised, is there inter-rater reliability?), generalisability (does the sample and setting allow you to extend the findings?), ethics (are all guidelines met?), and objectivity/subjectivity of the data.
  • Make each point specific to the plan in front of you: name the actual confound, the actual sampling weakness, the actual ethical risk. Generic evaluation earns little.
  • Finish by weighing strengths against weaknesses and offering one concrete, justified improvement — this demonstrates the higher-level analysis examiners reward, e.g. adding a second observer to raise reliability, or a biological measure to raise validity of the DV.

Key studies mastery — Clinical

Freeman et al. (2003) — persecutory ideation and virtual reality

  • Aim: to investigate whether persecutory (paranoid) thoughts occur in non-clinical individuals and can be studied using a neutral virtual reality environment.
  • Method: participants entered a VR library populated by neutral computer characters programmed to show no hostility, then reported their thoughts and feelings about the avatars; individual differences (e.g. anxiety) were also measured.
  • Key findings: a substantial proportion of ordinary participants attributed hostile intent to the neutral avatars, reporting persecutory thoughts even though the environment was designed to be neutral.
  • Evaluation point: high control and standardisation give the VR method good reliability and let researchers study paranoia ethically without real threat; but the artificial VR setting lowers ecological validity. Research method: laboratory experiment using VR.

Oruč et al. (1997) — genetics of bipolar disorder

  • Aim: to examine whether genetic variations in serotonin-related and dopamine-related genes are associated with bipolar affective disorder.
  • Method: a case-control genetic association study comparing bipolar patients with healthy controls on polymorphisms of the serotonin transporter (5-HTT) and tyrosine hydroxylase genes, analysed separately by sex.
  • Key findings: weak associations were found between the genetic markers and bipolar disorder, with some effects differing by sex, supporting a genetic contribution but not a single 'bipolar gene'.
  • Evaluation point: an objective, scientific method supporting biological explanations; but the modest associations show genes alone do not determine the disorder, pointing to gene-environment interaction. Research method: correlational/case-control genetic study.

Grant et al. (2008) — naltrexone for kleptomania

  • Aim: to test whether the opioid antagonist naltrexone reduces kleptomania symptoms (urges to steal and stealing behaviour).
  • Method: a double-blind, placebo-controlled randomised drug trial; kleptomania patients received either naltrexone or placebo over several weeks, with symptoms measured on standardised rating scales.
  • Key findings: naltrexone produced significantly greater reductions in stealing urges and behaviour than placebo, implicating the brain's opioid/reward system in impulse control.
  • Evaluation point: a rigorous double-blind RCT gives strong internal validity and a directly applicable biochemical treatment; but the reductionist drug focus ignores psychological triggers and possible side effects. Research method: laboratory drug experiment (RCT).

Chapman & DeLapp (2013) — habit reversal for a complex tic

  • Aim: to evaluate whether habit reversal training could reduce a highly complex, disabling tic in a single patient.
  • Method: an idiographic case study/behavioural intervention in which the patient was taught awareness of the tic and a competing response to replace it, with tic frequency monitored across sessions.
  • Key findings: habit reversal substantially reduced the frequency of the tic, demonstrating a behavioural treatment can work for even a very complex tic.
  • Evaluation point: rich, detailed evidence that a non-drug behavioural method is effective and low-risk; but as a single case it may not generalise. Research method: case study with a behavioural intervention.

Lovell et al. (2006) — telephone CBT for OCD

  • Aim: to test whether CBT delivered by telephone is as effective as face-to-face CBT for treating OCD.
  • Method: a randomised controlled trial comparing telephone-administered CBT with conventional face-to-face CBT, measuring OCD symptom severity before and after treatment.
  • Key findings: telephone CBT was as effective as face-to-face CBT in reducing OCD symptoms, with the advantage of greater accessibility.
  • Evaluation point: high usefulness and application because it widens access to treatment for housebound or remote patients; but telephone delivery may not suit everyone and lacks the presence of face-to-face contact. Research method: field experiment/RCT.

Key studies mastery — Health

Savage & Armstrong (1990) — the doctor's consulting style

  • Aim: to investigate whether a doctor's consulting style (a directing/doctor-centred style versus a sharing/patient-centred style) affects patient satisfaction.
  • Method: a field experiment in a general practice; patients were randomly allocated to receive either a directing consultation (firm diagnosis and instructions) or a sharing consultation (discussion of the problem), then completed satisfaction questionnaires.
  • Key findings: patients with physical problems reported higher satisfaction with the directing/doctor-centred style, challenging the assumption that a sharing style is always preferable.
  • Evaluation point: good ecological validity as a real-practice field experiment with random allocation; but self-report satisfaction may be affected by social desirability, and findings may not extend to psychological complaints. Research method: field experiment.

Yokley & Glenwick (1984) — increasing child immunisation

  • Aim: to find which prompts and incentives most effectively increase attendance for preschool children's immunisations.
  • Method: a field experiment comparing conditions such as a general prompt, a specific prompt, a monetary incentive and a control, measuring the number of children immunised.
  • Key findings: specific prompts and incentives increased immunisation attendance more than the control, with tangible incentives being particularly effective.
  • Evaluation point: highly useful and directly applicable to public-health policy in a real community; but effectiveness may depend on the specific community and incentive, limiting generalisation. Research method: field experiment.

Brudvik et al. (2016) — discrepancy in pain assessment

  • Aim: to compare how doctors, parents and children themselves rate the child's pain in an emergency setting.
  • Method: pain in children attending emergency care was rated independently by the physician, the accompanying parent and the child (using age-appropriate pain scales), and the ratings were compared.
  • Key findings: doctors systematically rated children's pain lower than the children and their parents did, revealing that clinicians tend to underestimate paediatric pain.
  • Evaluation point: important applied finding that should improve clinical pain management; but self- and proxy-reports of pain are subjective, and cultural or expressive differences may affect ratings. Research method: correlational/comparative study using self-report pain scales.

Bridge et al. (1988) — relaxation and imagery for breast cancer

  • Aim: to test whether relaxation, with or without guided imagery, reduces stress in women receiving radiotherapy for breast cancer.
  • Method: a field experiment; women were allocated to a control group, a relaxation-only group, or a relaxation-plus-imagery group over several weeks, with mood/stress measured by standardised questionnaires.
  • Key findings: both intervention groups showed less deterioration in mood than the control, and the relaxation-plus-imagery group showed the greatest benefit, indicating psychological techniques can manage stress in illness.
  • Evaluation point: useful, low-cost stress-management technique with real patients (high application); but the sample is specific to breast-cancer patients and self-report mood measures are subjective. Research method: field experiment.

Shoshani & Steinmetz (2014) — positive psychology in schools

  • Aim: to test whether a school-based positive-psychology intervention improves adolescents' mental health and well-being.
  • Method: a longitudinal field study/quasi-experiment; students in intervention schools received positive-psychology training (teachers trained to deliver it) while a comparison group did not, with well-being and distress measured over time.
  • Key findings: students in the intervention group showed improvements in well-being and self-esteem and reductions in distress and anxiety compared with the control, supporting health promotion through positive psychology.
  • Evaluation point: strong application to health promotion in a real educational setting with a longitudinal design; but quasi-experimental allocation and reliance on self-report limit causal certainty. Research method: longitudinal field/quasi-experiment.

Command words glossary

  • Describe: give an accurate, detailed account (AO1 content) - no evaluation.
  • Explain: give reasons/how or why - show understanding, often applied (AO1/AO2).
